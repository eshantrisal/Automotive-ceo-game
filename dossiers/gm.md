## 1. Company Snapshot — Candidate KPI Dimensions

GM's central strategic tension across 100+ years: a legacy industrial giant juggling **survival/solvency**, **political and labor relationships**, and **its ability to out-build the future (EVs/AV/tech) before legacy costs or government scrutiny sink it.** Three KPIs that fit:

1. **Cash / Solvency** — Liquidity and balance-sheet health. GM has gone to (and past) the brink of bankruptcy more than once; this is the "hit zero and you're dead" dial, just like Tesla's cash KPI but for a company carrying tens of billions in legacy pension/health/labor liabilities.
2. **Stakeholder Trust** (could label "Washington & Labor" or "Trust") — GM's fate is unusually entangled with the UAW, Congress, the White House, and regulators (NHTSA, DMV). Bad decisions here cost CEOs their jobs (Wagoner), trigger strikes (2019), or freeze entire business lines (Cruise/DMV 2023). This tracks political capital + labor peace + regulatory standing as one axis.
3. **Future Readiness / Innovation** — GM's competitiveness in EVs (Ultium), autonomy (Cruise), and software vs. Tesla, BYD, and Chinese EV makers. Same idea as Mercedes' "future" KPI: are you building the next era of the business, or just defending the current one.

(Alternative third KPI if the writer wants a closer Tesla/Mercedes parallel: "Production" — GM's ability to actually build/ship cars, which was central to 2008-09 plant idling, 2018 unallocation, and 2023-24 EV ramp struggles.)

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "The Reckoning" (2005–2009)
GM enters the 21st century as the world's largest automaker by volume but structurally rotten: legacy pension and retiree health-care obligations dwarf its market cap, its product strategy is overweighted on SUVs and trucks just as gas prices spike, and its largest parts supplier (Delphi) is in bankruptcy. The central tension: can GM restructure fast enough, on its own terms, before the 2008 financial crisis removes the option of restructuring on its own terms at all? This era ends with the unthinkable — a 100-year-old industrial icon filing Chapter 11, its CEO forced out by the President of the United States, and the U.S. Treasury becoming its majority shareholder.

### Era B — "Government Motors" (2009–2014)
GM is reborn out of bankruptcy in 35 days, slimmed from eight brands to four, majority-owned by the U.S. and Canadian governments and a UAW retiree trust. The tension: prove "New GM" is a real company and not a political ward — repay debts, go public again, shed the "Government Motors" nickname, get the Treasury out of the cap table — while still building cars people want to buy. It ends with a new, untested CEO (Mary Barra, the first woman to run a global automaker) inheriting a buried defect that turns out to predate the bankruptcy entirely.

### Era C — "Barra's GM: Crisis, Culture, and the Cut" (2014–2019)
Mary Barra spends her first year in the CEO chair testifying to Congress about a decade-old ignition-switch defect linked to well over 100 deaths, forcing a reckoning with GM's internal safety culture and a victim compensation fund. She then pivots to aggressive portfolio surgery: selling the money-losing European Opel/Vauxhall business, killing the Chevy Volt and several sedans, and "unallocating" North American plants — triggering the largest UAW strike in over a decade. The tension: can GM become "smaller, more profitable, more disciplined" without breaking faith with workers, regulators, or the public a second time.

### Era D — "Betting the Garage on the Future" (2020–2026)
GM commits tens of billions to EVs (the Ultium platform) and autonomous robotaxis (Cruise) as existential bets against Tesla and Chinese EV makers, while still printing record truck/SUV profits. The tension: can a 100+ year old manufacturer actually win two technology wars at once — and what happens when one of those bets (Cruise robotaxis) kills its momentum overnight after a safety incident, and the other (EV demand) grows far more slowly than promised. Era ends with GM pulling back hard on both robotaxi ambitions (folding Cruise in December 2024) and EV production targets (2023–2024), while still defending Ultium as the long-term plan.

## 3. Decision Points

### ERA A — The Reckoning (2005–2009)

**1. OCT 2005 — "The $15 Billion Handshake"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: GM lost $8.6 billion in 2005 on the back of ballooning retiree health-care and pension costs, plus a UAW workforce that, unlike at foreign-owned U.S. plants, came with decades of "Job Bank" and legacy retiree obligations. Wagoner negotiated a landmark mid-contract concession with the UAW to cut retiree health-care costs.
- Alternatives on the table: (a) negotiate now for a structural fix mid-contract even though it requires UAW goodwill it doesn't have to give; (b) wait for the next full contract cycle in 2007 and let losses compound; (c) push for bankruptcy-style restructuring tools outside negotiation.
- What actually happened: GM and the UAW reached a deal in October 2005 cutting GM's hourly retiree health-care liability by about $15 billion (~25%) and annual health-care expense by about $3 billion pretax, with roughly $1 billion in annual cash savings. It was an unusual mid-contract concession driven by the severity of GM's losses.
- Quote: Not independently found as a direct verbatim Wagoner quote on this specific deal; general reporting confirms the deal's terms and rationale (CNNMoney coverage of "GM tries to get in shape").
- Source: https://money.cnn.com/2005/10/17/news/fortune500/gm_wagoner/

**2. NOV 2005 — "Kerkorian Knocks"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: Billionaire investor Kirk Kerkorian built a 9.9% stake in GM through his Tracinda investment vehicle and demanded a board seat, pushing GM to consider a Renault-Nissan alliance (with Kerkorian ally Jerome York pushing the idea) as a way to force faster change.
- Alternatives: (a) accommodate Kerkorian with a board seat and explore the Renault-Nissan tie-up seriously; (b) resist the activist investor and keep control in-house; (c) explore the alliance commercially but on GM's own terms/timeline.
- What actually happened: GM ultimately rejected a three-way alliance with Renault-Nissan in 2006; Kerkorian sold down his stake in 2006 amid the impasse, and Jerome York resigned from the board.
- Quote: **UNVERIFIED** — multiple secondary sources describe Kerkorian/York's frustration with Wagoner's pace of change, but a specific sourced verbatim quote was not confirmed in this research pass.
- Source: https://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/2005/11/general-motors-death-watch-43-one-more-such-victory/ (background); broader confirmation via general financial press of the era — flag for additional verification before use.

**3. 2006 — "Closing the Plants Before the Crisis Does"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: Facing continued North American losses and overcapacity, Wagoner announced a restructuring to cut roughly 30,000 jobs and close four of GM's twenty North American assembly plants by the end of 2006 — a major proactive downsizing well before the 2008 crash.
- Alternatives: (a) cut deep and fast on a multi-year plan; (b) make smaller incremental cuts and hope volume recovers; (c) push costs onto suppliers/dealers instead of headcount.
- What actually happened: GM proceeded with the announced plant closures and job cuts through 2006, but the structural cost base remained too high relative to revenue heading into 2008.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** — specific verbatim Wagoner quote on this announcement not confirmed in this pass.
- Source: https://grokipedia.com/page/Rick_Wagoner (secondary synthesis); cross-referenced against contemporaneous wire reporting patterns — treat as needing one more primary-source check (e.g., GM 2006 8-K or AP wire) before final use in-game.

**4. SEP 2008 — "Lehman Weekend, Detroit Edition"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: The global financial crisis freezes credit markets just as GM's SUV/truck-heavy product mix collides with $4/gallon gas. Sales craters, and GM's cash burn accelerates toward a point where the company cannot make payroll and supplier payments without new capital it cannot raise privately.
- Alternatives: (a) seek emergency federal aid; (b) attempt a privately financed restructuring/merger (GM held real merger talks with Chrysler around this period); (c) file for Chapter 11 immediately rather than wait.
- What actually happened: GM (with Ford and Chrysler) approached Washington for assistance; by December 2008 GM disclosed it had received about $13.4 billion in initial federal loans and warned it might need up to $30 billion more, with auditors raising "going concern" doubts in GM's 2008 annual report.
- Quote: GM's own filing language: auditors noted "there's uncertainty with how the Treasury will view our viability plan" and "uncertainty on whether we're going to be able to execute the terms of our loan agreement" — reported via NBC/AP coverage of GM's going-concern disclosures.
- Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna29525238 ; https://www.pbs.org/newshour/economy/business-jan-june09-gmlosses_02-26

**5. NOV 19, 2008 — "The Corporate Jet Hearing"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: Wagoner testified before the Senate Banking Committee asking for federal loans (reporting cited figures in the $10–12 billion range for GM specifically, with the Big Three collectively requesting in the tens of billions), arguing that without aid GM could collapse within weeks. Lawmakers publicly excoriated the Detroit CEOs for flying to Washington on private jets to ask for a bailout.
- Alternatives: (a) ask for the full amount needed regardless of optics; (b) ask for less to look more credible, with the risk of having to come back again; (c) refuse government money and pursue a prepackaged bankruptcy instead.
- What actually happened: Congress did not pass a bailout bill in this round; Wagoner testified GM would pay back loans by 2012 and that he would take a $1 annual salary with no bonus. The Big Three were sent back to draft "viability plans," and a stopgap $13.4 billion in TARP loans was extended by the outgoing Bush administration in December 2008.
- Quote: Wagoner told Congress GM "would pay back the loan in full by 2012," and offered to accept "a salary of $1 a year with no cash bonus" as a gesture (per congressional testimony coverage).
- Source: https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/gm-ceo-defends-auto-industrys-bid-for-government-aid ; https://content.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,1864579_1864566_1864629,00.html

**6. FEB 17, 2009 — "The Viability Plan"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner
- Situation: As a condition of the December 2008 loans, GM had to submit a long-term viability plan to the new Obama administration's Treasury by February 17, 2009, proving it could survive without endless taxpayer support.
- Alternatives: (a) submit an aggressive plan with deep brand and dealer cuts; (b) submit a more conservative plan preserving more of the existing structure and brand portfolio; (c) signal openness to bankruptcy as a restructuring tool within the plan.
- What actually happened: The Obama administration's auto task force (led by Steven Rattner) reviewed the GM and Chrysler plans and concluded in late March 2009 that they "did not establish a credible path to viability" and "are not sufficient to justify a substantial new investment of taxpayer resources."
- Quote: The task force's written conclusion that GM's plan "did not establish a credible path to viability" — White House auto task force assessment, reported via NPR.
- Source: https://www.npr.org/2009/03/31/102553547/president-ousts-gm-ceo-in-effort-to-restructure-motor-city

**7. MAR 29–30, 2009 — "The Phone Call That Ended an Era"**
- CEO: Rick Wagoner (forced out)
- Situation: After rejecting GM's February viability plan, the White House auto task force, co-chaired by Steven Rattner, concluded new leadership was needed for GM to credibly restructure (with or without bankruptcy). Wagoner — at GM since 1977, CEO since 2000 — was asked to step down as a precondition for further federal aid.
- Alternatives: (a) resign as requested to preserve aid eligibility for the company; (b) refuse to resign and contest the task force's authority to remove a private company's CEO; (c) resign but contest the company's broader restructuring terms.
- What actually happened: Wagoner was forced out on March 29, 2009 at the task force's request; President Obama announced March 30 that GM and Chrysler had not made a credible viability case and would get limited additional time and aid. GM President/COO Fritz Henderson was named interim CEO.
- Quote: Austan Goolsbee, a member of the auto task force, said the move reflected a need for "a change of direction and new vision for the company" (per task force member commentary reported by NPR/press coverage).
- Source: https://www.npr.org/2009/03/31/102553547/president-ousts-gm-ceo-in-effort-to-restructure-motor-city ; https://www.marketplace.org/story/2009/03/30/gm-ceo-ousted-change-direction

**8. APR 27, 2009 — "Killing the Brands"**
- CEO: Fritz Henderson
- Situation: With the government demanding deeper cuts, GM had to decide which of its eight U.S. brands (Chevrolet, Buick, GMC, Cadillac, Pontiac, Saturn, Hummer, Saab) it could actually support post-restructuring.
- Alternatives: (a) keep a broad multi-brand portfolio and hope volume recovers; (b) cut down to a core 4 brands (Chevrolet, Cadillac, Buick, GMC) and sell or wind down the rest; (c) sell some brands as standalone businesses to outside buyers rather than simply shuttering them.
- What actually happened: GM announced on April 27, 2009 it would phase out Pontiac by 2010, and moved up plans to resolve Saturn, Hummer, and Saab by the end of 2009 — via sale where possible (Saturn and Hummer drew bidders that ultimately fell through; Saab was sold to Spyker in 2010 but later failed) and discontinuation otherwise. The closures affected roughly 2,600 dealers and thousands of factory jobs.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** — a specific verbatim Henderson quote from the April 27 announcement was not confirmed in this pass; secondary synthesis (Truth About Cars, Jalopnik interviews with former vice chairman Bob Lutz years later) describes the rationale but should be re-verified against a primary GM press release before use.
- Source: https://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/2009/04/gm-kills-pontiachummer-saab-and-saturn/ ; https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2009/4/27/gm-to-slash-21000-more-jobs

**9. JUN 1, 2009 — "Chapter 11"**
- CEO: Fritz Henderson
- Situation: Despite the surgical restructuring effort, GM could not avoid bankruptcy. It filed for Chapter 11 in the Southern District of New York, reporting $82.29 billion in assets against $172.81 billion in liabilities — the largest industrial bankruptcy in U.S. history at the time.
- Alternatives: (a) pursue a "363 sale" structure that quickly moves good assets into a new government-backed entity, leaving bad legacy liabilities behind in "Old GM"; (b) attempt a traditional, slower Chapter 11 reorganization with all stakeholders negotiating in court for years; (c) seek a last-minute private buyer or merger instead of court protection.
- What actually happened: GM used the fast "363 sale" structure: the U.S. Treasury and Canadian government provided debtor-in-possession financing (TARP funds disbursed in two tranches — about $23 billion on June 3, 2009 and the remaining roughly $7.1 billion at the close of the 363 sale), and "New GM" emerged from bankruptcy in about 35–40 days, by mid-July 2009, majority-owned by the U.S. Treasury (~61%), with stakes for Canada, the UAW retiree health trust (VEBA), and bondholders.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** verbatim Henderson quote on the filing itself in this pass; financial details independently confirmed via SEC 8-K and Yale case-study materials.
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Motors_Chapter_11_reorganization ; https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001467858/000119312509169233/d8k.htm ; https://elischolar.library.yale.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1259&context=journal-of-financial-crises

### ERA B — Government Motors (2009–2014)

**10. DEC 1, 2009 — "The Board Fires Henderson"**
- CEO: Fritz Henderson (ousted) → Ed Whitacre (interim, then permanent)
- Situation: Just months after emerging from bankruptcy, GM's new government-installed board grew impatient with the pace of cultural and structural change under Henderson, a 25-year GM veteran widely seen as a product of the old culture.
- Alternatives: (a) give Henderson more time to prove "New GM" works; (b) replace him immediately with an outsider to signal a clean break; (c) install a caretaker and search externally over a longer period.
- What actually happened: The board forced Henderson's resignation on December 1, 2009, and installed AT&T veteran and GM board chairman Ed Whitacre as CEO — an outsider to the auto industry, signaling the board wanted faster, more radical change.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** specific board quote in this pass; resignation widely reported (NPR: "GM CEO Resigns").
- Source: https://www.npr.org/2009/12/01/120999544/gm-ceo-resigns

**11. NOV 18, 2010 — "Ringing the Bell Again"**
- CEO: Ed Whitacre
- Situation: GM needed to return to public markets to begin repaying U.S. and Canadian taxpayers and shed the "Government Motors" stigma, just 17 months after bankruptcy.
- Alternatives: (a) price the IPO conservatively to guarantee a successful debut; (b) price aggressively to maximize proceeds for the Treasury's exit; (c) delay the IPO further until profitability looked more durable.
- What actually happened: GM's IPO priced at $33/share on November 17, 2010, after the offering was upsized roughly 30% on strong demand (initial range had been $26–$29); the deal raised about $20.1 billion in common shares (and roughly $23.1 billion including preferred shares), making it the largest IPO in history to that point. Shares opened in the mid-$30s on November 18 and closed up about 3.6% on day one. The U.S. Treasury's stake fell from about 61% toward roughly 33% post-IPO.
- Quote: Ed Whitacre, on stepping down as CEO just before the IPO closed out his tenure: "Had I stayed through the IPO, I would have had to stay a long time. Investors are going to buy the management of the company, as well as the cars they make."
- Source: https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2010-11-18/general-motors-returns-to-nyse-after-raising-20-billion-in-ipo ; https://money.cnn.com/2010/11/17/news/companies/gm_ipo_pricing/index.htm ; https://fortune.com/2013/01/23/how-ed-whitacre-brought-gm-back-from-the-brink/

**12. AUG–SEP 2010 — "Handing Off Mid-Comeback"**
- CEO: Ed Whitacre → Dan Akerson
- Situation: With the IPO imminent, Whitacre — who had taken the CEO job somewhat reluctantly as a turnaround fixer — decided not to commit to the multi-year tenure he believed public investors would expect of new GM leadership.
- Alternatives: (a) Whitacre stays through the IPO and beyond for stability; (b) hand off to an internal GM lifer; (c) hand off to another outside director already familiar with the company, as was done.
- What actually happened: On August 12, 2010, GM announced Dan Akerson — a Treasury-appointed board member since July 2009 and former telecom/private-equity executive — would succeed Whitacre as CEO effective September 1, 2010, becoming board chairman as well on January 1, 2011.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** specific Akerson quote from the handoff announcement in this pass.
- Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2010/08/12/AR2010081203081.html ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Akerson

**13. 2011–2012 — "Europe's Bottomless Pit"**
- CEO: Dan Akerson
- Situation: GM's European operations (anchored by Opel/Vauxhall) continued losing billions annually, with brand overlap between Chevrolet and Opel in Europe that Akerson himself called reminiscent of "retro GM" thinking. Activists and some board members questioned whether GM should exit Europe outright.
- Alternatives: (a) commit fresh multi-billion-euro investment to fix Opel's product and cost structure; (b) sell or shut down Opel/Vauxhall immediately; (c) maintain the status quo and absorb continued losses.
- What actually happened: Akerson committed to investing roughly €4 billion ($5.2 billion) in European operations through 2016, betting on a turnaround rather than an immediate exit — a bet that would ultimately not fully pay off (GM sold Opel/Vauxhall in 2017, under Barra).
- Quote: Akerson described Chevy-Opel brand overlap in Europe as reminiscent of "retro GM" — reported via Automotive News/Automotive Manufacturing Solutions coverage of the period's strategy debates.
- Source: https://www.autonews.com/article/20110609/COPY01/306099861/gm-akerson-are-frustrated-with-opel-but-won-t-sell-it-sources-say ; https://www.automotivemanufacturingsolutions.com/press-and-body/gm-europe-the-group-in-profile/536019

**14. DEC 2012 — "Buying Back the Government's Stock"**
- CEO: Dan Akerson
- Situation: The U.S. Treasury still held roughly 500 million GM shares (about 26% of the company) more than two years after the IPO, keeping the "Government Motors" label alive and constraining executive pay under TARP rules.
- Alternatives: (a) have GM itself repurchase a large block of Treasury shares to accelerate the government's exit; (b) wait for Treasury to sell shares gradually on the open market; (c) do nothing and let the stigma persist.
- What actually happened: In December 2012, GM agreed to repurchase 200 million shares directly from the Treasury, and the Treasury announced plans to sell its remaining roughly 300 million shares within 12–15 months via the open market.
- Quote: Dan Akerson called the move "an important step in bringing closure to the successful auto industry rescue" and said it "demonstrates confidence in GM's progress and our future."
- Source: https://www.nbcnews.com/business/business-news/gm-buys-back-stock-taxpayers-may-lose-billions-flna1C7659301

**15. DEC 9, 2013 — "Government Motors, No More"**
- CEO: Dan Akerson (outgoing) / incoming Mary Barra named
- Situation: The Treasury's final stake sale would close the books on the federal rescue, but at a confirmed loss to taxpayers — a politically sensitive number for both the company and the administration.
- Alternatives: (a) sell out completely now even at a loss, prioritizing the symbolic and operational benefit of independence; (b) negotiate a slower exit to try to recoup more value over time; (c) explore a structured buyback to control the optics of the loss.
- What actually happened: The U.S. Treasury sold its final shares of GM common stock on December 9, 2013, ending the "Government Motors" era. The federal government ultimately recovered about $39 billion of the roughly $49.5 billion (some sources cite a TARP-specific loss of about $10.5 billion on the auto portion) it had invested to rescue GM — a final loss to taxpayers of roughly $10–11 billion on GM specifically.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** verbatim quote from a single named GM or Treasury official specifically on this transaction in this pass, though Treasury press materials and NPR/Bloomberg confirm the figures.
- Source: https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2013-12-09/gm-bailout-ends-as-u-s-sells-last-of-government-motors- ; https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2013/12/09/249815202/government-sells-last-shares-in-gm-loses-10-billion

### ERA C — Barra's GM: Crisis, Culture, and the Cut (2014–2019)

**16. DEC 10, 2013 / JAN 15, 2014 — "The First Woman to Run a Global Automaker"**
- CEO transition: Dan Akerson → Mary Barra
- Situation: GM's board named Mary Barra — a 33-year company engineer and most recently head of global product development — as the next CEO, the first woman ever to lead a major global automaker. She officially became CEO on January 15, 2014, just as an internal review was quietly escalating an old defect investigation.
- Alternatives: (a) promote from deep within engineering (Barra) to signal continuity and product focus; (b) bring in another outside turnaround executive as with Whitacre/Akerson; (c) promote a finance-oriented insider.
- What actually happened: The board chose Barra. Within roughly two weeks of her taking the job, GM's own contractor was placing rush orders for replacement ignition switches — the first visible motion of what would become the company's defining crisis of 2014.
- Quote: **NOT FOUND** specific Barra quote from the appointment announcement itself in this pass.
- Source: https://money.cnn.com/2014/11/10/news/companies/barra-recall/index.html

**17. FEB 7–14, 2014 — "The Recall Nobody Wanted to Name"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: GM notified NHTSA on February 7, 2014 of a recall covering 778,000 small cars (Chevy Cobalt, Saturn Ion, and others) for a faulty ignition switch that could slip out of "run" position, cutting power steering, brakes, and airbags — a defect GM engineers had known about, in some documented internal discussions, for roughly a decade. The recall went public February 14 and rapidly grew.
- Alternatives: (a) issue a narrow recall focused only on the originally identified vehicles; (b) proactively widen the recall immediately given evidence the defect existed in more models/years; (c) slow-walk the process pending further internal investigation (which is what had happened for the prior decade).
- What actually happened: The recall expanded rapidly to eventually cover about 2.6 million vehicles globally. GM commissioned an independent investigation by attorney Anton Valukas, whose report (delivered May 2014) detailed a culture of "incompetence and neglect" — calling it "the switch from hell" — that allowed the defect to go unaddressed for years.
- Quote: The Valukas Report described GM's internal culture and process failures, with investigators characterizing the defect's mishandling in scathing terms (widely reported as "the switch from hell" in coverage of the report); Barra herself told employees in a town hall that the report's findings were "deeply troubling."
- Source: https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001467858/000146785814000159/ex-99106052014prxvalukasre.htm ; https://www.autosafety.org/wp-content/uploads/import/GM%20Barra%20Town%20Hall.pdf

**18. APR 1, 2014 — "I Am Deeply Sorry"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: Barra testified before the House Energy and Commerce Subcommittee on Oversight and Investigations, in a hearing titled "The GM Ignition Switch Recall: Why Did It Take So Long?" — facing intense questioning about why GM took roughly a decade to recall a known defect.
- Alternatives: (a) accept full corporate responsibility publicly while declining to speculate on internal blame pending investigation; (b) deflect blame onto specific individuals/engineers; (c) emphasize legal/technical distinctions to minimize corporate liability.
- What actually happened: Barra apologized repeatedly, telling lawmakers "I am deeply sorry" and "I cannot tell you why it took years for a safety defect to be announced in that program, but I can tell you that we will find out" — declining to give specifics pending the Valukas investigation, which frustrated some senators who accused GM of a "culture of cover-up."
- Quote: Mary Barra: "I cannot tell you why it took years for a safety defect to be announced in that program, but I can tell you that we will find out." Also: "I am deeply sorry."
- Source: https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/economy/pressure-from-victims-investigators-mounts-on-gm-ahead-of-congressional-hearing/2014/04/01/7993fe4c-b9ae-11e3-9a05-c739f29ccb08_story.html ; https://money.cnn.com/2014/04/01/news/companies/barra-congress-testimony/ ; https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2014/04/02/298345621/gm-has-culture-of-cover-up-key-senator-says

**19. JUN 30, 2014 — "Pricing a Human Life"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: Rather than litigate thousands of individual claims, GM hired attorney Kenneth Feinberg (who had previously run the 9/11 and BP Deepwater Horizon compensation funds) to design and administer a victim compensation fund for the ignition-switch defect, with GM agreeing to place no cap on the total payout.
- Alternatives: (a) create an uncapped, no-fault compensation fund to resolve claims quickly and compassionately; (b) litigate claims individually through the courts, which would take years and risk much larger punitive verdicts but might pay out less in aggregate; (c) offer a small fixed settlement pool with a hard cap.
- What actually happened: The fund opened for claims August 1, 2014, with death claims starting at a $1 million baseline (plus additional amounts) and no aggregate cap. By the fund's close (final report issued December 2015), Feinberg's team had received about 4,343 claims, paid out on fewer than 10% of them, and distributed a total of roughly $594.5 million, covering 124 confirmed deaths and 275 injury claims. More than 90% of those offered compensation accepted.
- Quote: Kenneth Feinberg's final accounting confirmed "124 killed and another 275 received cash for injuries" — reported via CNN coverage of the fund's closure.
- Source: https://money.cnn.com/2015/12/10/news/companies/gm-recall-ignition-switch-death-toll/index.html ; https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/national/2015/12/10/391452.htm ; https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2014/06/30/326947185/feinberg-details-plan-to-compensate-victims-in-gm-recall

**20. 2014–2015 — "Never Put This Behind Us"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: With the immediate recall crisis subsiding, Barra had to decide how aggressively to use the scandal to reform GM's internal safety culture — famously rigid, hierarchical, and cost-focused — versus simply moving on once the legal and PR exposure was managed.
- Alternatives: (a) treat the crisis as a one-time legal/financial event to be resolved and then deprioritized; (b) use it as the forcing mechanism for a deep, permanent culture change (new "Speak Up for Safety" program, a new Global Product Integrity organization, empowering engineers to flag defects without fear); (c) make moderate process changes without broader cultural investment.
- What actually happened: Barra created a new Global Product Integrity organization, instituted a "Speak Up for Safety" reporting program, and told employees in internal communications that she never wanted to "put this behind us," wanting the experience to stay "permanently in our collective memories" as a safeguard against repeating it.
- Quote: Mary Barra, to employees: "I never want to put this behind us" — wanting it kept "permanently in our collective memories."
- Source: https://fortune.com/2015/09/18/mary-barra-gm-culture/ ; https://www.nbcnews.com/storyline/gm-recall/safety-more-important-cost-new-gm-ceo-barra-says-n69741

**21. MAR 6, 2017 — "Letting Go of Europe"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: After nearly 90 years and billions of dollars in cumulative losses in Europe, GM had to decide whether to keep fighting for Opel/Vauxhall or exit the market entirely. PSA Group (Peugeot) approached GM about acquiring the unprofitable European business.
- Alternatives: (a) sell Opel/Vauxhall outright to PSA and exit Europe; (b) continue investing in a turnaround as Akerson had bet on a few years earlier; (c) seek a different buyer or a joint-venture structure instead of a full sale.
- What actually happened: GM announced March 6, 2017 it would sell Opel/Vauxhall (plus GM Financial's European operations) to PSA Group for roughly €2.2 billion ($2.3 billion) — about $1.38 billion for the automotive business and $950 million for the financial unit, paid mostly in cash plus warrants for a 4.2% non-voting PSA stake. About 40,000 employees, six assembly plants, five component plants, and the Rüsselsheim engineering center transferred to PSA, making PSA Europe's #2 automaker behind VW.

**22. NOV 26, 2018 — "Unallocated"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: Facing a maturing North American truck/SUV-heavy product cycle, slowing car demand, and a desire to redirect billions toward EV and AV investment, GM announced a sweeping restructuring.

**23. SEP 15 – OCT 25, 2019 — "The 40-Day Strike"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: With the 2015 UAW-GM contract expiring and the 2018 "unallocation" plan still fresh, contract talks broke down over job security, wages, the use of temporary workers, and health-care cost-sharing. About 48,000 UAW members at roughly 50 GM plants across the U.S. walked out.
- Alternatives: (a) hold firm on cost discipline and risk a prolonged strike; (b) make early concessions to end the strike quickly and limit production losses; (c) attempt to operate plants with replacement/management labor (not seriously pursued).
- What actually happened: The strike ran 40 days, from September 15 to October 25, 2019 (workers ratified the new contract around October 25), the longest UAW national strike against GM in nearly 50 years. The final four-year deal included an $11,000 ratification bonus for full-time UAW workers ($4,500 for temps), a pathway for temps to convert to permanent status, and other job-security provisions. GM later said the strike cost the company between $3.8 billion and $4 billion for 2019, including about $1 billion in Q3 alone.

**25. OCT 2, 2023 — "The Dragging"**
- CEO (GM): Mary Barra; CEO (Cruise): Kyle Vogt
- Situation: At approximately 9:30 p.m. on October 2, 2023, near 5th and Market Streets in San Francisco, a pedestrian was first struck by a human-driven hit-and-run vehicle, thrown into the path of a Cruise robotaxi, which then braked but proceeded to pull over per its programmed protocol — dragging the pedestrian roughly 20 feet at up to about 7 mph while she was trapped underneath.
- Alternatives in the aftermath: (a) immediately and fully disclose the dragging footage and circumstances to California regulators; (b) report the incident in a way that emphasizes the vehicle's initial stop and omits the subsequent pullover/drag (which is what occurred in the initial report); (c) proactively ground the fleet pending review before being ordered to.
- What actually happened: A Cruise senior manager reported the incident to the California Public Utilities Commission on October 3 in a way that described the vehicle stopping upon impact but omitted the critical detail of the pullover maneuver that caused the dragging. Cruise did not show the California DMV the full dragging footage until October 13. On October 24, 2023, the California DMV suspended Cruise's robotaxi deployment and driverless testing permits, stating the vehicles "are not safe for the public's operation" and "may lack the ability to respond safely and appropriately during incidents involving a pedestrian." Cruise was later fined for withholding/incomplete reporting. On October 26–27, Cruise voluntarily suspended all driverless operations nationwide "to rebuild public trust." Kyle Vogt resigned as Cruise CEO on November 19, 2023.
- Quote: California DMV statement that Cruise vehicles "are not safe for the public's operation" and "may lack the ability to respond safely and appropriately during incidents involving a pedestrian so as not to unnecessarily put the pedestrian or others at risk of further injury."

**26. 2023–2024 — "How Much More Do We Spend on Cruise?"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: After the nationwide pause, Cruise had to rebuild trust with regulators and the public from zero, while GM had already sunk more than $8–10 billion cumulative investment into the unit since 2016 with no robotaxi revenue at meaningful scale. GM had to decide how hard to keep pushing the robotaxi business versus retrenching.
- Alternatives: (a) keep funding Cruise at full scale and push to relaunch robotaxi service as planned; (b) scale back funding and narrow Cruise's ambitions while still pursuing robotaxis eventually; (c) exit the robotaxi business entirely and redeploy the technology/team into personal-vehicle driver-assistance and autonomy features.
- What actually happened: GM initially supported a slower relaunch of limited Cruise testing through 2024, but by December 2024 concluded the "time and expense" required to scale robotaxis competitively (against Waymo and others) was too great. On December 10, 2024, GM announced it would stop funding Cruise's robotaxi development and fold Cruise's roughly 2,300 employees and technology into GM's broader technical teams, ending the standalone robotaxi business. GM's annual Cruise-related spend had been running around $2 billion, and the restructuring was expected to cut that by more than half (saving over $1 billion annually). GM said it would refocus autonomy investment on advanced driver-assistance and autonomous features for personal vehicles instead.
- Quote: Mary Barra cited the "time and expense" that would have been required to keep scaling the robotaxi business as the reason for exiting; GM stated it would "realign its autonomous driving strategy" toward personal-vehicle ADAS/autonomy.

**27. 2022–2024 — "The EV Targets That Didn't Land"**
- CEO: Mary Barra
- Situation: GM had publicly committed to aggressive North American EV production targets — including a goal to build roughly 400,000 EVs (on the Ultium platform plus existing Bolt) between early 2022 and mid-2023 — as part of a broader multi-billion-dollar EV/battery investment (Ultium Cells joint-venture battery plants with LG in Ohio, Tennessee, Michigan, etc.).
- Alternatives: (a) push through supply and automation bottlenecks to hit the original aggressive targets regardless of cost; (b) publicly delay and reset targets to more achievable levels while continuing the broader EV investment; (c) significantly scale back EV ambitions and capital spending in favor of continued ICE/hybrid profits.
- What actually happened: GM missed its original target, delivering only about 75,883 EVs in all of 2023 — and roughly 62,045 of those were soon-to-be-discontinued Chevy Bolts, leaving just about 13,341 Ultium-based EVs actually produced that year, due largely to battery-supply constraints at the Ohio Ultium Cells plant and problems with an automation equipment supplier. In 2023–2024 GM also paused EV van production at its CAMI plant in Ontario over battery shortages, and by late 2024 cut its 2024 EV production target to roughly 200,000 units (down from an earlier 250,000–300,000 goal) and slowed/delayed construction of additional battery cell plants — while publicly maintaining that Ultium remained the long-term strategy and that it had exited "production hell" by early 2024.
