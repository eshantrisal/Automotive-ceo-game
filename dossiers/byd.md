# BYD (Build Your Dreams) — Research Dossier

Founder/CEO/Chairman: **Wang Chuanfu** (in role continuously since 1995; remains Chairman & President as of 2026).

This dossier follows the same structure used for the Tesla and Mercedes-Benz/Daimler game data: a company snapshot with 3 candidate KPIs, 2–4 eras spanning company history, and within each era 8–15 real decision points with date, situation, alternatives, real outcome, quote (if verifiable), and sourcing.

---

## 1. Company Snapshot — Candidate KPI Dimensions

BYD's central strategic tension across its history is: **scrappy battery-chemistry engineer turned conglomerate, betting everything on vertical integration and volume, while quality, brand prestige, and geopolitics keep threatening to catch up with growth.** Three KPIs that fit this:

1. **Cash / Capital** (`cash`) — BYD has serially needed capital for audacious bets (auto pivot 2003, Blade Battery plants, overseas factories) while also being famous for thin margins and a 2023–2025 price war that crushed industry profitability. Maps to the "Cash Runway"-style KPI Tesla uses, but should also capture investor confidence in the stock (HK:1211 / SZSE:002594).
2. **Production / Vertical Integration Capacity** (`prod`) — BYD's distinguishing strategic choice (vs. Tesla and legacy OEMs) is owning nearly the entire stack: battery cells (Blade/LFP), semiconductors/IGBT chips, even ships to export cars. This KPI should track manufacturing scale, supply-chain self-sufficiency, and ability to avoid the chip-shortage/parts-shortage problems that hobbled rivals in 2021.
3. **Brand Trust / Quality & Geopolitical Standing** (`brand` or `trust`) — BYD has repeatedly won on price and volume but been dogged by quality complaints (F3DM-era, 2011 dealer crisis, 2025 quality complaints), and by 2023–2025 it faces EU/US tariff walls and Brazil labor-conditions lawsuits that threaten the "Made in China, sold to the world" growth story. This tracks both consumer trust and the company's standing with foreign governments/regulators — lose it and overseas expansion stalls regardless of how many cars roll off the line.

(Alternative third KPI to consider: "Global Reach" tracking overseas market access specifically, since tariffs/labor scandals are as much an expansion-KPI problem as a brand problem — a writer could split this into 4 KPIs if needed, but 3 is consistent with the Tesla/Mercedes format.)

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "The Battery Maker's Gamble" (1995–2008)
Wang Chuanfu, a battery chemist with no automotive experience, builds Shenzhen's BYD into China's largest rechargeable-battery maker by reverse-engineering Japanese designs and substituting cheap manual labor for expensive automated lines — then, against the explicit objections of investors and analysts, pivots the entire company into building gasoline cars from a near-bankrupt state factory. The central tension: is BYD a battery company opportunistically using cars as a new customer, or is Wang secretly building the only company on Earth positioned to make electric vehicles from the cell up? Quality is rough, the F3 is a Corolla-alike, but the volumes and the war chest grow fast enough that by 2008 a little-known American investor's right-hand man comes calling.

### Era B — "Buffett's Bet and the Crash" (2008–2014)
Berkshire Hathaway's investment via MidAmerican Energy in September 2008 turns Wang into a global story and BYD stock into a momentum trade — Wang briefly becomes China's richest man in 2009. But the hype outruns the company's actual execution: a bloated dealer network, inventory channel-stuffing, and quality problems collapse sales and profit by 2011–2012, forcing a humiliating public mea culpa from Wang and a multi-year rebuild. Underneath the carnage, the company quietly keeps funding EV and hybrid R&D (F3DM, e6 taxis, K9 buses) — the unglamorous, low-margin groundwork for everything that comes after.

### Era C — "Going All-In On Electric" (2015–2022)
Riding China's NEV subsidy boom, BYD scales electric buses, taxis and passenger cars, survives a 2019 subsidy-cut shock, and makes its highest-conviction technical bet yet: the Blade Battery (LFP, announced March 2020), packaged first in the flagship Han sedan as a direct shot at Tesla's Model S/3. In March 2022 Wang makes the symbolically enormous decision to stop building pure gasoline cars entirely — the first major global automaker to do so — completing the multi-decade thesis that batteries, not engines, were always the real business.

### Era D — "The World's Biggest EV Maker — and Its Backlash" (2023–2026)
BYD's volumes explode past Tesla's in quarterly, then annual, sales and revenue (2023–2024), powered by ultra-cheap models like the Seagull and an aggressive plug-in-hybrid lineup Tesla doesn't compete in. The company pushes hard into Europe, Southeast Asia, Brazil, and Mexico — and runs straight into EU anti-subsidy tariffs (2024), a 100% US tariff wall and a shelved Mexico plant (2024–2025), and a Brazilian forced-labor scandal at its Bahia factory (2024–2025). At home, BYD's own price-cutting ignites a destructive price war that draws a rare public rebuke from Chinese state media in 2025, and Wang has to manage the tension between "win at all costs growth" and the sustainability of the entire Chinese EV industry's margins — including his own.

## 3. Decision Points by Era

### ERA A — The Battery Maker's Gamble (1995–2008)

---

**1. FEB 1995 — "2.5 Million Yuan and a Borrowed Idea"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Founder/General Manager)
Situation: Wang, a metallurgy graduate who had run a state-owned battery research subsidiary (BAK-precursor) in Beijing, quits a secure post to found Shenzhen BYD Battery Co. with roughly CNY 2.5 million ($300K) raised mostly from a cousin. China's battery market is dominated by Japanese imports (Sony, Sanyo) protected by patents and expensive automated production lines BYD cannot afford.
Alternatives on the table: (a) stay in the safe state-research job; (b) try to license Japanese battery tech properly and pay royalties; (c) reverse-engineer the chemistry and substitute cheap manual labor/jigs for the expensive robotic assembly lines the Japanese used.
What actually happened: Wang chose (c) — he broke down Sony/Sanyo nickel-cadmium battery designs and re-engineered the manufacturing process around inexpensive semi-manual production, cutting unit costs roughly 5–6x versus Japanese rivals. BYD grew into China's largest NiCd/NiMH battery maker within a few years and a top global cellphone-battery supplier (Motorola, later Nokia) by the early 2000s.
Quote: Not independently verified with a primary-sourced direct quote for this specific moment; secondary sources (Britannica, BBNTimes profile) describe the cost-cutting strategy but a verbatim founding-era quote from Wang was not found in searchable sources — flag as unverified if a quote is needed here.

**2. JUL 2002 — "Going Public in Hong Kong"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD Company Limited lists on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange (HK:1211) on 31 July 2002, by then established as a major rechargeable-battery manufacturer with global cellphone-OEM customers. The IPO gives Wang a public capital-markets currency and outside shareholders — for the first time, decisions face public-market scrutiny.
Alternatives: (a) stay private and self-fund growth via bank debt and customer prepayments; (b) seek a strategic-investor private placement instead of a public listing; (c) IPO in Hong Kong to access international capital and prestige.
What happened: BYD chose the HKEX listing (option c). It proved to be the funding vehicle that, a year later, gave Wang the currency and credibility to attempt the auto-industry pivot.
Source: https://www.fool.com/investing/2025/11/18/is-byd-stock-a-millionaire-maker (confirms July 31, 2002 HKEX IPO date)

**3. JAN 2003 — "Buying a Bankrupt Car Factory Nobody Wanted"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: Wang announces BYD will acquire 77% of Tsinchuan (Qinchuan) Automobile, a struggling state-owned car manufacturer in Xi'an, Shaanxi, for roughly CNY 269 million — pivoting a pure battery company into car manufacturing with zero automotive engineering pedigree. This was not disclosed in BYD's listing prospectus, and it blindsided investors.
Alternatives: (a) stay a pure battery/electronics supplier and avoid capital-intensive, low-margin car manufacturing; (b) partner with or supply batteries to an existing automaker rather than buying one; (c) acquire Tsinchuan outright and build the car business from scratch.
What happened: Wang chose (c) despite the blowback — a Hong Kong fund manager reportedly threatened to dump all BYD stock the next day if the deal went through. BYD's Hong Kong-listed shares fell sharply (reports cite a roughly 20%+ single-day drop around the announcement) as institutional investors revolted. Wang proceeded anyway, declaring his intent to spend "the rest of my life" in the auto industry — specifically the new-energy-vehicle industry, even though BYD's first cars (the F3, 2005) would be conventional gasoline sedans.
Quote: Wang Chuanfu: "I will focus on the automotive industry for the rest of my life, and it will be the new energy vehicle industry." (As reported by 36Kr's retrospective on Wang's 30-year history; treat as a paraphrase/translation from Chinese-language reporting rather than a contemporaneously recorded English quote — flag as moderately, not fully, verified.)
Source: https://eu.36kr.com/en/p/3041711421649161 ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Auto

**4. 2005 — "The F3: Win Volume First, Fix Quality Later"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD launches its first mass-market sedan, the F3 — styled closely enough after the Toyota Corolla that comparisons were immediate — priced aggressively under roughly $10,000 in China. The strategic question: compete on engineering quality against Japanese/Korean incumbents, or compete purely on price and feature-for-the-money, accepting a "cheap copy" reputation?
Alternatives: (a) invest more upfront in original design/engineering to avoid copycat criticism; (b) lead with rock-bottom pricing and Corolla-adjacent styling to win volume fast; (c) skip passenger cars and focus on commercial/fleet vehicles where styling matters less.
What happened: BYD chose (b). The F3 became the best-selling sedan in China by 2009 and sold over 1.6 million units globally by the time production ended (2021), but it cemented an early reputation for derivative styling and so-so build quality that BYD would spend the next decade fighting to shed — particularly once the plug-in-hybrid F3DM (2008) drew criticism for "crude transitions" between electric and gasoline power.
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_F3 ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_F3DM

---

**5. DEC 2008 — "BYD F3DM: The World's First Mass-Production Plug-in Hybrid"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD launches the F3DM (Dual Mode), claimed as the world's first mass-produced plug-in hybrid, ahead of GM's Volt and Toyota's plug-in Prius. The car is rough — reviewers cite poor build quality and awkward switching between electric and gasoline power — but it is a genuine technology first from a company most of the world still associates with cheap gas cars.
Alternatives: (a) wait until the technology and build quality were polished before launching, ceding the "world's first" claim to a rival; (b) launch early and imperfect to claim the milestone and start the learning curve; (c) abandon the plug-in-hybrid approach and focus only on pure EVs.
What happened: BYD launched early and imperfect (b) — initially selling F3DMs mainly to government and corporate fleets in China rather than retail consumers, given the rough edges, while iterating toward later, much-improved DM-series hybrids that would become central to BYD's 2020s sales boom.
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_F3DM

**6. SEP 2008 — "Buffett's Man Comes Calling"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: Charlie Munger, having learned of BYD through a contact, becomes convinced Wang Chuanfu is an extraordinary operator and pushes Warren Buffett to invest — despite Buffett's famous aversion to tech/auto bets and to China. Berkshire Hathaway, through subsidiary MidAmerican Energy Holdings, moves to buy roughly 10% of BYD for about $232 million (225 million shares).
Alternatives: (a) decline the Western strategic capital and remain a primarily Chinese/Asian-investor company; (b) accept a smaller, less prominent partner; (c) accept the Berkshire/MidAmerican investment and the global spotlight it brings.
What happened: BYD took the deal (c). The investment, finalized in September 2008, was explicitly described by Berkshire insiders as a bet on Wang personally ("a bet on the horseman rather than the horse," per reporting on Munger's reasoning) more than on any specific product. It transformed BYD's global profile overnight and made Wang briefly China's richest man by 2009. Berkshire/MidAmerican held the stake for 17 years, beginning to sell down in 2022 and fully exiting by September 2025 — a holding that grew more than 20-fold in value over its life.
Quote: Wang Chuanfu, on the announcement: "We are extremely pleased and grateful that Berkshire Hathaway and MidAmerican will be our long-term investor and partner, as we bring our electric vehicles and other environmental protection measures to the global space." (CNBC, Sept 27, 2008)
Source: https://www.cnbc.com/2008/09/27/warren-buffett-invests-in-chinese-company-developing-green-cars.html ; https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/22/investing/warren-buffet-berkshire-hathaway-exits-byd

**7. 2009 — "The Battery-Fluid Demonstration"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: During a visit by Buffett's representatives/scouts evaluating BYD ahead of or around the investment, Wang — eager to prove the safety of BYD's battery electrolyte — reportedly drank a sample of it in front of visitors to demonstrate its non-toxicity.
Alternatives: (a) rely on lab safety data sheets and third-party certifications alone; (b) stage a dramatic personal demonstration to make the safety claim viscerally memorable.
What happened: Wang chose the dramatic personal demonstration (b), drinking the battery fluid. The anecdote became a widely repeated piece of BYD/Wang lore, foreshadowing the company's later (2020) Blade Battery nail-penetration safety demonstrations as a recurring marketing tactic: prove safety theatrically, not just statistically.
Note on verification: This anecdote is widely reported (Autoblog, citing earlier press) but the exact date and which Buffett-affiliated individual witnessed it are not pinned down precisely in available sources — treat the date/year as approximate (commonly cited as around the time of the Berkshire deal, 2008–2010) rather than a confirmed single date.

### ERA B — Buffett's Bet and the Crash (2008–2014)

---

**8. 2009 — "China's Richest Man, Briefly"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: On the strength of the Buffett-driven stock surge and BYD's expanding battery/auto/solar businesses, Wang Chuanfu's net worth briefly makes him the richest person in mainland China in 2009, per Hurun and Forbes-style rankings — a dramatic ascent for a battery engineer from a poor farming family in Anhui.
Alternatives: this is more an inflection point than a binary choice, but the strategic question facing Wang was whether to use the moment (and BYD's now sky-high valuation/profile) to raise more capital and accelerate expansion aggressively, or to consolidate and tighten operations given that the underlying business (dealer network, quality systems) had not caught up to the hype.
What happened: BYD chose rapid expansion — adding dealerships and sales targets aggressively through 2009–2010 — which set up the channel-stuffing and quality crisis that hit in 2011–2012 (see next entries). In hindsight, this is the clearest "the spotlight outran the fundamentals" moment in BYD's history.
Source: https://spectrum.ieee.org/byd-owner-is-chinas-richest-man

**9. 2010 — "The Daimler Joint Venture"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: Daimler AG — itself separately an early investor in Tesla — approaches BYD to form a 50/50 joint venture to develop a new electric-vehicle brand for the Chinese market, eventually branded Denza. This gives BYD a chance to learn premium-brand engineering and design discipline from a 120-year-old luxury automaker, but on Daimler's terms, with significant compromise on BYD's own brand visibility within the JV.
Alternatives: (a) decline and keep all EV development fully in-house under the BYD brand; (b) accept the JV to gain access to Daimler engineering/quality know-how and a credibility halo; (c) seek a similar partnership with a different, less control-seeking partner.
What happened: BYD accepted the JV (b); Denza was established and launched its first model years later (2014). The partnership ran for roughly a decade with limited commercial success before BYD eventually bought out Daimler's stake (2021–2022) and relaunched Denza as a wholly BYD-owned premium sub-brand, which became commercially successful only after that change in control.
Source: General industry reporting on the Denza joint venture; note — specific deal-value figures for the original 2010 JV formation were not independently re-verified line-by-line in this research pass and should be checked against a primary Daimler/BYD press release before being used as a hard fact in the game.

**10. 2011 — "The Crash: Profits Down 88%"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: After several years of breakneck dealership expansion and aggressive sales targets, BYD's auto business hits a wall in 2011: first-half profit plunges roughly 88.6% year-on-year. Dealers, having been pushed to take on more inventory than they could sell, start revolting; quality complaints mount; China also rolls back some EV-purchase tax incentives around this period, removing a tailwind.
Alternatives: (a) keep pushing volume targets and wait for the market to absorb the inventory; (b) publicly acknowledge the overexpansion and quality problems and reset the dealer network and sales targets; (c) quietly trim targets without public acknowledgment.
What happened: The crisis deepened into 2012 before BYD chose (b). Sales VP Xia Zhibing resigned amid the fallout, sales-department layoffs followed, and Chinese media ran scathing pieces (reported headlines translate to things like "The Great Defeat of BYD"). Net profit for full-year 2012 fell roughly 94% to about CNY 81.4 million, down from CNY 1.38 billion the prior year.
Quote: Wang Chuanfu, acknowledging the crisis: "In the past, we pursued expansion of quantity and scale one-sidedly, neglecting to improve brand quality." He identified three specific failure areas: dealership networks, media relations, and quality issues. (As reported in retrospective Chinese business-media coverage; treat as a paraphrase-level translation, high confidence on substance, lower confidence on exact original wording.)
Source: https://www.benzinga.com/news/earnings/13/03/3442138/byd-2012-net-profit-down-94 ; https://medium.com/@guangxizhang1207/the-legendary-life-of-wang-chuanfu-the-founder-of-chinas-trillion-dollar-car-company-3-9541ae20d8b5

**11. 2012 — "Rebuilding Trust: Dealer Network Reform"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: With dealers in revolt and inventory bloated, BYD must decide how to rebuild its sales channel — the same network whose collapse pattern would recur a decade later in 2025 (see Era D).
Alternatives: (a) cut dealer count sharply and rebuild a smaller, healthier network; (b) prop up the existing network with discounts and incentives; (c) shift toward direct/fleet sales (taxis, government vehicles) to reduce dependence on the dealer model.
What happened: BYD pursued a mix of (a) and (c) — trimming the weakest dealers while leaning harder into fleet sales (the e6 electric taxi program in Shenzhen, K9 electric buses) which didn't depend on a healthy private-retail dealer network. This fleet/commercial-vehicle ballast helped BYD survive the lean 2012–2014 stretch while it rebuilt passenger-car credibility.
Source: General context corroborated across multiple retrospective profiles (Medium/Roger Zhang piece; Just-Auto "power of nightmares" feature) — https://www.just-auto.com/features/emerging-markets-analysis-byd-and-the-power-of-nightmares/

**12. 2013–2014 — "Shenzhen e6: Betting on Taxi Fleets"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: With private passenger-car sales still recovering from the 2011–2012 crash, BYD pushes its e6 pure-electric vehicle into Shenzhen's taxi fleet as a flagship use case — a high-utilization, high-visibility proving ground for EV reliability at a time when private buyers remained skeptical of electric cars' range and durability.
Alternatives: (a) wait for private consumer demand to mature before pushing EVs hard; (b) use captive/government fleet deployments (taxis, buses) to generate volume, real-world reliability data, and government goodwill while the private market caught up.
What happened: BYD chose (b). Shenzhen's e6 taxi fleet became one of the most-cited proof points globally for EV taxi viability, and the K9 electric bus program expanded into dozens of cities domestically and eventually abroad — establishing the commercial-vehicle/government-fleet beachhead that subsidized BYD's passenger-EV R&D through the recovery years.
Source: Corroborated by multiple industry retrospectives on BYD's NEV ramp; treat specific fleet-size figures as approximate without a freshly pulled primary BYD or Shenzhen-government source.

### ERA C — Going All-In On Electric (2015–2022)

---

**13. JUN 2019 — "The Subsidy Cliff"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: Beijing cuts national NEV purchase subsidies sharply — by roughly 45–60% for vehicles with shorter range — effective late June 2019, ending years of subsidy-fueled growth across the Chinese EV industry. BYD's July 2019 sales drop 38% month-on-month and 12% year-on-year; pure-EV sales of popular long-range models (which lost the most subsidy) fall by roughly half in a single month.
Alternatives: (a) cut prices to offset the lost subsidy and protect volume, eating the margin hit; (b) hold prices and accept lower volume; (c) accelerate cost-reduction (battery/parts vertical integration) to absorb the subsidy loss without cutting margin or volume as much.
What happened: BYD leaned into (c) as a multi-year strategy — doubling down on in-house battery and semiconductor production to drive down underlying costs — while taking some near-term volume pain in 2019. This subsidy shock is widely viewed as a forcing function that pushed BYD's vertical-integration strategy (already underway since the mid-2000s with battery and chip subsidiaries) into higher gear ahead of the 2020s EV boom.
Source: https://qz.com/1683042/beijings-subsidy-cut-is-starting-to-its-largest-ev-maker-byd

---

**14. 2020–2021 — "Riding Out the Global Chip Shortage on In-House Chips"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: A global semiconductor shortage beginning in late 2020 cripples automakers worldwide — Ford, GM, Toyota and others idle plants for lack of chips. BYD, however, had been developing its own automotive-grade IGBT (insulated-gate bipolar transistor) chips through subsidiary BYD Semiconductor since 2005, reaching IGBT 4.0/5.0 generations by 2018–2020.
Alternatives: (a) rely on the open chip market like most automakers and absorb shortage-driven production cuts; (b) lean on in-house IGBT production to insulate at least the power-electronics portion of the supply chain; (c) spin off or sell the semiconductor unit to focus capital on vehicle assembly.
What happened: BYD chose (b), and the payoff was visible: while rivals lost millions of units of production industry-wide during 2021–2022, BYD's production kept running and its sales surged over 200% in 2022. BYD Semiconductor did raise prices on IGBT/IPM products by at least 5% in July 2021 to reflect higher input costs, but kept supply flowing to BYD's own lines — turning a crisis that hurt every competitor into a competitive advantage.
Source: https://kr-asia.com/byd-semiconductor-bumps-prices-by-5-as-global-chip-shortage-persists ; https://www.nomadsemi.com/p/byd-semiconductor-deep-dive

**15. JUL 2020 — "The Han: A Direct Shot at Tesla"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD launches the Han EV, a flagship sedan roughly the size of a Tesla Model S, priced from about $32,800 to $40,000 depending on trim, and built as the debut showcase vehicle for the new Blade Battery. After 15 years of being associated with cheap, derivative cars, this is BYD's first deliberate attempt at a premium, technology-forward flagship meant to be cross-shopped against Tesla.
Alternatives: (a) keep targeting the budget segment where BYD's cost advantages were strongest; (b) build a genuine flagship to change the brand's market positioning and prove the Blade Battery and BYD's own engineering at the top of the lineup.
What happened: BYD chose (b). The Han, capable of 0–62mph in as little as 3.9 seconds in AWD form and rated around 376 miles of range in its long-range trim, became a strong seller in China and a signal — confirmed at scale three years later — that BYD intended to compete with Tesla across the price spectrum, not just underneath it.
Source: https://www.greencarreports.com/news/1128931_byd-han-ev-luxury-sedan-arrives-in-china-tesla-model-s-sized-about-40k ; https://carbuzz.com/news/the-byd-han-ev-is-teslas-worst-nightmare/

**16. MAR 2020 — "The Blade Battery and the Nail Test"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD's battery subsidiary FinDreams unveils the Blade Battery — a lithium iron phosphate (LFP) cell in a thin, structural "blade" format — explicitly marketed on safety, at a moment when nickel-manganese-cobalt (NMC/ternary) chemistry, used by Tesla and most premium EV makers, offered higher energy density but a worse safety/thermal-runaway reputation. The strategic bet: trade some energy density for dramatically better safety and lower cost, using LFP at a time most premium automakers were still chasing NMC's range numbers.
Alternatives: (a) follow the industry toward higher-energy-density NMC chemistry to maximize range and the marketing "range race"; (b) commit to LFP/Blade and market safety and structural efficiency (via CTP — cell-to-pack — packaging that improved space utilization by roughly 50%) as the differentiator; (c) pursue a dual-chemistry strategy across different vehicle tiers.
What happened: BYD released a viral nail-penetration test video showing a ternary (NMC) cell catching fire immediately with surface temperatures exceeding 500°C, a standard LFP cell not burning but exceeding 200°C, and the Blade Battery producing no smoke or fire and staying in a 30–60°C range — cool enough to touch. BYD committed hard to LFP/Blade as both a safety-marketing platform and a cost lever, a bet validated industry-wide over the following years as LFP adoption (including by Tesla itself, for standard-range models) grew sharply.
Source: https://www.electrive.com/2020/03/30/byd-reveals-blade-battery-focussed-on-safety/ ; https://evreporter.com/byd-blade-battery-what-makes-it-ultra-safe/ ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Blade_battery

**17. JUN 2021 — "First Steps Into Europe: Norway"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD ships its first 100 European-specification Tang SUVs to Norway — its first deliberate entry into the European passenger-vehicle retail market (as distinct from earlier bus/commercial-vehicle exports) — choosing Norway specifically because of its uniquely EV-friendly market (near-100% EV market share in new sales) and a partnership with local dealer RSA for sales, service, and parts.
Alternatives: (a) enter Europe through a larger, more central market (Germany, UK, France) to maximize volume potential immediately; (b) enter through a small, EV-friendly beachhead market to learn European retail, service, and certification requirements at low risk before scaling; (c) delay European entry until the home market fully stabilized post-2019-subsidy-cut.
What happened: BYD chose the low-risk beachhead approach (b), planning to deliver roughly 1,500 Tang EVs in Norway in 2021 before expanding model lines and country count across Europe in subsequent years — a deliberate "learn small, then scale" playbook that preceded the much larger 2023–2025 European expansion (and the tariff fight that followed).
Source: https://cnevpost.com/2021/06/08/byds-first-100-tang-evs-begin-shipping-to-norway-delivery-expected-in-august/ ; https://www.electrive.com/2021/08/13/byd-completes-first-tang-deliveries-in-norway/

**18. APR 2022 — "Killing the Internal Combustion Engine"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: On April 3, 2022, BYD announces it will stop producing pure gasoline (internal-combustion-only) vehicles, becoming the first major global automaker to do so completely, focusing entirely on battery-electric and plug-in-hybrid vehicles going forward. By this point BYD's shift was already well underway commercially: it built 106,658 NEVs in March 2022 alone, versus 21,492 in the same month a year earlier — a roughly 5x year-on-year jump signaling the gas-car wind-down was catching up to, not getting ahead of, where demand already was.
Alternatives: (a) keep a gasoline-vehicle line as a hedge / cash-flow stabilizer while EV/PHEV demand matured further; (b) commit fully and publicly to ending ICE-only production, betting that demand momentum (and government policy direction) would continue; (c) phase out ICE production quietly without a symbolic public announcement.
What happened: BYD chose the full public commitment (b). It continued making and supplying ICE vehicle parts and after-sales service for existing customers but built no new pure-gasoline models from that point forward — a milestone widely covered as a symbolic bellwether for the broader industry's EV transition, two years before Western legacy automakers (GM, Ford, Mercedes, etc.) would walk back or soften their own EV-only target dates.
Source: https://www.electrive.com/2022/04/04/byd-quits-the-combustion-engine/ ; https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202204/1257571.shtml ; https://en.byd.com/news/byd-discontinues-gas-only-auto-line-to-focus-on-phev-and-pure-electric-tech/

### ERA D — The World's Biggest EV Maker — and Its Backlash (2023–2026)

---

**19. MAR 2023 — "Thailand: The First Wholly-Owned Overseas Passenger Plant"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD breaks ground (March 10, 2023) on a $491 million (17.9 billion baht), 150,000-unit-capacity factory in Rayong, Thailand — its first wholly BYD-owned overseas passenger-car manufacturing plant, as distinct from the licensed-export/local-partner approach used in earlier markets.
Alternatives: (a) continue exporting fully-built cars from China to growing markets like Thailand, accepting import duties and shipping costs/time; (b) build a wholly-owned local factory to localize production, avoid tariffs/duties, and establish a regional export hub for the rest of Southeast Asia; (c) license production to a local partner rather than invest directly.
What happened: BYD chose full ownership (b). The plant was completed in just 16 months and inaugurated July 4, 2024, coinciding with BYD's 8-millionth global NEV rolling off the line; it became central to BYD capturing roughly half of Thailand's EV market and, alongside other Chinese makers, around 85% of the Thai EV segment by 2025 — with BYD overtaking Honda as Thailand's #2 best-selling car brand outright by June 2025.

**21. EARLY 2024 — "The Seagull: A $9,700 Car as a Weapon"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD cuts the price of its tiny Seagull city EV by roughly 5% to a starting price of 69,800 yuan (about $9,700) in early 2024, as part of a much broader wave of price cuts across BYD's lineup (e.g., nearly 12% off the Yuan Plus crossover) that ignites a sector-wide price war Tesla itself had effectively started in China the prior year.

What happened: BYD chose (b), repeatedly. The Seagull's price would fall further still, to roughly 55,800 yuan (~$7,750) by May 2025 — a cumulative ~20%+ cut from its already rock-bottom 2024 starting price.

**22. JUL 2024 — EU Provisional Tariffs**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: Following a nine-month European Commission anti-subsidy investigation into Chinese EV imports, the EU imposes provisional countervailing tariffs on Chinese-made EVs from early July 2024, with BYD facing a provisional rate around 17.4% — on top of the EU's standard 10% car import duty. The strategic choice: fight the tariffs through trade diplomacy/legal channels, absorb them and stay export-based, or accelerate local European manufacturing to sidestep them entirely.
Alternatives: (a) contest the tariffs through WTO/legal channels and trade negotiations while continuing to export from China; (b) absorb the tariff cost into pricing and stay export-based; (c) accelerate building EU-based manufacturing (Hungary, later Turkey) to manufacture "in the wall" and avoid the tariffs on those volumes.
What happened: BYD pursued a combination, but most consequentially committed to (c): pressing ahead with a €4 billion factory in Szeged, Hungary (300,000-unit annual capacity), while later (2025) shifting emphasis toward an even larger Turkey plant (targeting 500,000 units/year, lower labor costs, and tariff-free EU export under Turkey's customs arrangements) as the Hungary plant's mass-production timeline slipped to 2026. BYD and other Chinese makers like MG also began selling plug-in hybrids in Europe specifically because PHEVs were not subject to the same EV-targeted tariffs.
Source: https://www.electrive.com/2025/07/22/byd-shifts-focus-from-hungary-to-turkey-for-european-ev-production/ ; https://www.electrive.com/2025/08/18/byd-and-mg-sidestep-eu-tariffs-with-plug-in-hybrids/

**23. OCT 2024 — Definitive EU Duties, and Choosing Diplomacy Over Defiance**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: On October 4, 2024, the European Council adopts definitive countervailing duties on Chinese-made battery-electric vehicles, ranging from 7.8% to 35.3% depending on manufacturer, taking effect October 29–30, 2024 under Implementing Regulation 2024/2754. BYD's definitive rate comes in at 17.0% (a slight reduction from the 17.4% provisional rate) — notably lower than some rivals because BYD was judged to have cooperated more fully with the EU's subsidy investigation. Germany and Hungary, among five member states, vote against the tariffs; ten states including France and Italy vote in favor.
Alternatives: (a) retaliate rhetorically/diplomatically and threaten reciprocal Chinese tariffs on EU goods (a path the Chinese government itself partly pursued separately, e.g., on EU brandy/pork); (b) cooperate with the EU investigation to minimize the company-specific rate and preserve the relationship for future localization plans; (c) abandon the European market rather than absorb tariffs.
What happened: BYD's relatively cooperative posture (b) paid off in a lower individual tariff rate than several rivals, while the company simultaneously pressed ahead with localizing production (Hungary/Turkey) to make the tariff question moot for an increasing share of its European volumes over time.
Source: https://www.clearytradewatch.com/2024/10/definitive-duties-adopted-by-the-eu-on-chinese-battery-electric-vehicles-to-counteract-subsidies-to-apply-by-october-30/

**24. 2024–2025 — "The US Tariff Wall and the Mexico Retreat"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: In May 2024 the Biden administration announces it will raise tariffs on Chinese EVs imported to the US to 100% (effective later that year), making the US market commercially closed to direct Chinese EV exports. BYD had been evaluating Mexico as a manufacturing base specifically because vehicles meeting USMCA regional-content rules could cross into the US tariff-free — a potential backdoor.
Alternatives: (a) press ahead with a Mexico plant on the existing timeline, betting trade tensions would ease; (b) pause/slow the Mexico search pending clarity on the 2024 US election outcome and likely tariff trajectory; (c) abandon the US/North American market ambition entirely and focus capital elsewhere.
What happened: BYD chose (b): Bloomberg reported in mid-2025 that BYD had stopped actively scouting Mexican sites (it had been evaluating three) to await clarity from the US political situation. Following Donald Trump's return to the White House and continued aggressive tariff rhetoric/policy toward both China and Mexico-based manufacturing, BYD effectively shelved the major Mexico production plan, redirecting that capital and attention toward Europe (Hungary/Turkey), Brazil, and Southeast Asia instead.

**25. DEC 2023–2025 — "Brazil: The Bahia Factory and the Forced-Labor Scandal"**
Top job: Wang Chuanfu (Chairman)
Situation: BYD acquires a former Ford manufacturing site in Bahia, Brazil (the sale completing around December 2023, after Ford ceased Brazilian manufacturing) for a planned $1.28 billion EV factory complex — its first major Latin American manufacturing investment. Construction, subcontracted partly to Chinese firm Jinjiang Group, draws Brazilian labor-ministry scrutiny: in December 2024, authorities report finding roughly 220 Chinese nationals working in conditions described as "analogous to slavery," including passport confiscation, illegal wage deductions, and degrading housing; 163 workers are reported freed from these conditions.
Alternatives: (a) deny responsibility and let subcontractors absorb the legal/reputational fallout; (b) cooperate fully with Brazilian investigators while contesting the characterization of the violations; (c) halt the project pending an independent labor-conditions review.
What happened: BYD pursued (b) — issuing statements that it had cooperated with investigations "since the start" while it and Jinjiang Group denied the most severe allegations. Brazilian prosecutors nonetheless sued BYD and two contractors (China Jinjiang Construction Brazil and Tecmonta) in May 2025, seeking roughly 257 million reais (~$50 million) in damages, and added BYD to a government "slave labor" employer registry — a serious reputational setback for BYD's Latin American expansion narrative even as construction and plans to begin local assembly continued.
