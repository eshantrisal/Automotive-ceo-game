## 1. Company Snapshot — Candidate KPI Dimensions

Mahindra's central strategic tension, repeated across eras: a farm-equipment cash machine subsidizing a perennially scrappy, second-tier automotive business that periodically makes big, expensive, high-conviction bets (SUVs, SsangYong, EVs) under a chairman (Anand Mahindra) who prizes global ambition and a string of professional-manager MDs/CEOs who must make the numbers work. Three KPI candidates:

1. **Cash/Capital Discipline** (label idea: "Capital Discipline") — Mahindra is not Tesla-style cash-starved; it's a profitable conglomerate, but its automotive bets (SsangYong, Ford JV, INGLO/EV platform investment of $1B+) are funded by surplus from the farm equipment business and can be cut off by the board/promoter family when losses mount (SsangYong 2020 funding refusal is the clearest example). This KPI tracks whether the player overextends auto ambitions against group financial discipline.
2. **SUV/Brand Leadership** (label idea: "SUV Credibility") — Mahindra's identity is "SUV specialist for India" (Jeep CKD heritage → Scorpio → XUV500 → Thar → BE EVs). This tracks product credibility, waiting lists, and brand heat versus Maruti, Tata, Hyundai, and now BYD/Tesla in EVs.
3. **Global/Future Readiness** (label idea: "Global & EV Readiness") — Tracks the company's bets beyond its protected home turf: SsangYong/global SUV ambitions, the Ford JV, and the EV transition (Reva, e2o, INGLO platform, BE brand). This is the riskiest, most volatile dimension — wins (Tivoli, BE 6e demand) and losses (SsangYong bankruptcy, Ford JV collapse) both register here.

These three (Capital Discipline / SUV Credibility / Global & EV Readiness) parallel the Tesla cash/production/confidence and Mercedes margin/brand/future structures while fitting Mahindra's actual recurring dilemma: a domestic farm-equipment fortress funding global automotive swings for the fences.

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "The SUV Specialist Is Born" (1991–2010)
**Framing:** Post-liberalization India opens to competition for the first time since 1947; Mahindra, until then a Jeep-CKD assembler and tractor maker, must decide whether to become a real, independent automotive engineering company or stay a licensed assembler living off government protection. Under R&D chief (and later MD) Pawan Goenka, the company makes a contrarian bet: build its own SUV in-house rather than license another foreign design, betting the entire company's relevance on the Scorpio (2002) and then the XUV500 (2011). The era closes with Mahindra also experimenting — and largely failing — at small-car and passenger-sedan diversification via Renault (Logan/Verito), even as the SUV core compounds.

### Era B — "Going Global, Going Electric" (2010–2016)
**Framing:** Flush with cash and confidence after Scorpio/XUV500, Chairman Anand Mahindra pushes the company onto the world stage for the first time: a landmark $463M acquisition of bankrupt South Korean automaker SsangYong (2010–11), and an early, contrarian bet on electric vehicles by buying control of Bangalore's REVA (2010) years before EVs were fashionable in India. This era is defined by audacious, expensive bets whose payoff is uncertain — SsangYong is a turnaround project, not a sure thing, and EVs are commercially almost a novelty act (e2o sells in the low thousands).

### Era C — "SUV Boom, Korean Bust, Pandemic Reckoning" (2016–2021)
**Framing:** Domestically, Mahindra rides a genuine SUV boom (new XUV500 generations, the 2018 Marazzo, and especially the wildly successful 2020 second-generation Thar) while internationally its SsangYong bet curdles into crisis — capped by a flashy, ultimately aborted joint venture with Ford (2019–2021) meant to share EV and SUV development costs. COVID-19 then forces brutal capital-allocation choices: the board pulls the plug on further SsangYong funding (April 2020), SsangYong collapses into bankruptcy (December 2020), and the era ends with a generational leadership change — Pawan Goenka, the "father of the Scorpio," retires as MD & CEO in April 2021, handing the keys to professional manager Anish Shah.

### Era D — "Born Electric" (2021–2026)
**Framing:** Under new CEO Anish Shah and auto/farm sector head Rajesh Jejurikar, Mahindra finally exits its costliest legacy liability (selling its SsangYong stake to Korea's KG Group, completed 2022) and pivots hard into a dedicated EV strategy: a new "BE" sub-brand, a bespoke INGLO EV platform, and a committed investment exceeding $1 billion, racing Tata Motors and global entrants (Tesla's India entry, Chinese EV makers) for the next decade of Indian SUV leadership — while fending off recurring speculation that the auto and farm-equipment businesses should finally be split apart.

## 3. Decision Points

### ERA A — The SUV Specialist Is Born (1991–2010)

**1. JUL 1991 — "The Licence Raj Ends"**
- Situation: India's 1991 economic liberalization ends decades of import/license restrictions that had protected Mahindra's Jeep-CKD-derived utility vehicle business from real competition. Foreign automakers (Suzuki already in via Maruti since 1981, and now potentially GM, Ford, Toyota) can enter more freely. Mahindra's vehicles (the CJ-340/CJ-500 Jeep derivatives, the MM540, MM550) are dated, low-volume, and built on 1940s-era Willys architecture.
- Top job: Group chaired by Keshub Mahindra; Anand Mahindra is Deputy Managing Director (from 1991) under his uncle.
- Alternatives on the table: (a) continue licensing/assembling foreign designs (the historical comfort zone), (b) build a genuine in-house R&D and engineering capability to design original vehicles, (c) exit utility vehicles and focus purely on tractors, which were already the more profitable, less competitive business.
- What actually happened: Mahindra chose to build in-house engineering capability. In 1993 it hired Pawan Goenka — a GM (Detroit) veteran with a PhD from Cornell — as General Manager of R&D, explicitly to build a domestic product-development organization rather than continue depending on foreign licensors. This decision is the seed of the Scorpio program a decade later.
- Quote: Unable to verify a contemporaneous (1991–93) quote from Anand Mahindra or Goenka specifically about this strategic choice; Goenka has since described the period as spending "the first 4-5 years... putting basics in place" before product development began (paraphrase, not exact quote — see source).
- Source: https://www.autocarpro.in/feature/dr-pawan-goenka-%E2%80%98you-have-to-be-the-master-at-whatever-you-do%E2%80%99-78911 ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pawan_Kumar_Goenka

**2. 1995–1997 — "Build the Scorpio In-House, From Scratch"**
- Situation: With basic R&D infrastructure in place, Goenka's team has to decide how ambitious to be. Indian carmakers at the time typically badge-engineered or licensed Japanese/Korean designs. Mahindra instead commits to a from-scratch, indigenously engineered SUV — eventually the Scorpio — at an eventual development cost reported around ₹600 crore over roughly five years.
- Top job: Keshub Mahindra (Chairman), Anand Mahindra (Managing Director from 1997), Pawan Goenka leading the program as the project's chief architect — he is widely credited as "father of the Scorpio."
- Alternatives: (a) license an existing Korean/Japanese SUV platform to move faster and cheaper, (b) build the Scorpio fully in-house with an integrated design-and-manufacturing team, (c) focus resources on facelifting existing Jeep-derived models instead.
- What actually happened: Mahindra committed to full in-house design via an integrated team, eventually unveiling a prototype at the 2002 Auto Expo and launching commercially on June 20, 2002, starting at Rs 5.99 lakh, with a 109hp 2.6L diesel and Renault-sourced 116hp 2.0L petrol option. Tellingly, Mahindra's own marketing avoided calling it an "SUV," branding it simply a "car" to signal the leap into mainstream passenger vehicles.
- Outcome: The Scorpio won "Car of the Year" (Business Standard Motoring) and "Best SUV of the Year"/"Best Car of the Year" (BBC World Wheels), and became the foundation of Mahindra's modern SUV identity.
- Source: https://www.autocarindia.com/car-news/sting-operation-the-mahindra-scorpio-origin-story-424570 ; https://www.carblogindia.com/2002-2022-mahindra-scorpio-history-details/ ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahindra_Scorpio

**3. JUN 2002 — "The Scorpio Launch Gamble"**
- Situation: The Scorpio is Mahindra's first vehicle aimed at both domestic and export markets and effectively a referendum on the company's decision to engineer its own products. Failure would validate skeptics who said Indian engineering couldn't deliver a competitive modern SUV.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (MD), Pawan Goenka (R&D/program lead).
- Alternatives: launch conservatively priced to minimize risk vs. launch at a confident price point that signals premium positioning vs. delay further for additional refinement.
- What actually happened: Launched June 20, 2002 at Rs 5.99 lakh ex-showroom — aggressive value pricing relative to its capability — and it succeeded commercially and critically immediately.
- Source: https://www.cartoq.com/blast-from-the-past-what-the-mahindra-scorpio-looked-like-when-first-launched-in-2002/ ; https://www.autocarindia.com/car-news/sting-operation-the-mahindra-scorpio-origin-story-424570

**4. 2005–2007 — "Partner With Renault for a Sedan"**
- Situation: The Scorpio proved Mahindra could do SUVs, but the company has no presence in the passenger sedan segment, which is far larger in volume. Renault wants an India entry without building from scratch. The two agree a joint venture (Mahindra Renault Ltd, 51% Mahindra / 49% Renault) to build the Renault Logan in India.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD).
- Alternatives: (a) build an independent Mahindra-badged sedan, (b) joint-venture with Renault on the Logan, (c) skip sedans entirely and stay SUV/UV focused.
- What actually happened: The JV launched the Logan in 2007. It sold roughly 28,000 units in its first year (2008) but collapsed to about 5,000 units by the end of 2009, hurt by criticism of its dated, boxy design and by India's 2006 tax restructuring that penalized cars marginally over 4 meters in length — a bracket the Logan's length fell into, creating a severe tax disadvantage versus sub-4m competitors.
- What actually happened next: Renault exited the JV in April 2010; Mahindra bought out Renault's stake and rebranded the car the "Verito," taking full control.
- Quote/source headline: "Renault ends Indian car alliance over Logan flop" (France24, April 16, 2010).
- Source: https://www.france24.com/en/20100416-renault-ends-indian-car-alliance-mahindra-logan ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahindra_Renault ; https://www.autocarpro.in/feature/day-2010-mahindra-bought-renault-stake-joint-venture-11156

**5. MAR 2007 — "Buying a Wounded Rival: Punjab Tractors"**
- Situation: Punjab Tractors, owner of the respected "Swaraj" tractor brand, had fallen from the #2 tractor maker in India to #5 due to poor management, despite a loyal customer base built since 1974. Actis Group and the Burman family investment vehicles hold a large stake and are looking to sell.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD), tractor/farm equipment business under its own divisional leadership.
- Alternatives: (a) acquire Punjab Tractors to add the Swaraj brand and consolidate India's tractor market, (b) let a rival (e.g., a foreign entrant) acquire it instead, (c) compete it into irrelevance organically.
- What actually happened: Mahindra & Mahindra agreed on March 9, 2007 to acquire a 43.3% stake in Punjab Tractors at Rs 360/share, a deal valued around Rs 1,489 crore, also picking up a 14% indirect stake in Swaraj Mazda. Punjab Tractors was later merged into Mahindra & Mahindra outright.
- Source: https://www.mahindra.com/news-room/press-release/en/mahindra-and-mahindra-agrees-to-acquire-43-point-3-percent-stake-in-punjab-tractors-limited ; https://www.businesstoday.in/magazine/case-study/story/case-study-punjab-tractors-success-after-mahindra-took-it-over-35408-2012-12-06 ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab_Tractors_Ltd.

**6. SEP 2011 — "The XUV500: India's First Indigenous Monocoque SUV"**
- Situation: Nine years after the Scorpio, Mahindra needs a more modern, globally credible SUV to keep momentum and to support export ambitions. The decision is to build the country's first indigenously developed monocoque (unibody) SUV with a transverse engine — a significant engineering step up from the Scorpio's body-on-frame design — entirely in-house at the new Mahindra Research Valley (Chennai).
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD), Pawan Goenka (overseeing R&D as President, Automotive & Farm Sectors).
- Alternatives: (a) license a foreign monocoque platform to move faster, (b) continue refining the Scorpio's body-on-frame architecture instead of investing in a new platform, (c) build a wholly new in-house "Mahindra Global SUV platform."
- What actually happened: Mahindra unveiled the XUV500 in Pune on September 28, 2011 (pricing/strategy detailed September 29), built on its own new global SUV platform, featuring a distinctive "pouncing cheetah" design language gathered from customer input across global markets.
- Outcome: The XUV500 became one of Mahindra's most important models of the decade, reinforcing Mahindra's "SUV specialist" identity domestically and internationally.
- Source: https://www.drivespark.com/four-wheelers/2011/23-mahindra-confirms-xuv-500-launch.html ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahindra_XUV500 ; https://www.cars24.com/article/evolution-of-xuv500-explained/

### ERA B — Going Global, Going Electric (2010–2016)

**7. MAY 2010 — "Buying an Electric Car Company Nobody Wants Yet"**
- Situation: Electric vehicles are commercially marginal in India in 2010 — REVA, a small Bangalore EV maker founded in 1994 as a JV between the Maini Group and US-based AEV LLC, is a niche product. Mahindra has to decide whether to make an early, speculative bet on EVs years before any real market or policy support exists.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD); decision executed under the auto sector leadership with Pawan Goenka taking the chair of the new entity's board.
- Alternatives: (a) acquire REVA outright and commit engineering resources to EVs, (b) watch and wait for the EV market to mature before investing, (c) develop EV technology purely in-house without acquiring REVA.
- What actually happened: In May 2010, Mahindra acquired 55.2% equity in REVA Electric Car Company through a mix of buying out promoters' shares and a fresh equity infusion of over Rs 45 crore (~$10 million). The company was renamed Mahindra REVA Electric Vehicle Company. Dr. Pawan Goenka became Chairman of the new board; REVA co-founder Chetan Maini stayed on as Chief of Technology & Strategy.
- Source: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/mahindra-enters-high-growth-electric-car-segment-acquires-majority-stake-in-reva-94910379.html ; https://www.motoroids.com/news/breaking-mahindra-acquires-majority-stake-in-reva-enters-electric-car-market/

**8. NOV 2010–FEB 2011 — "The SsangYong Gamble"**
- Situation: South Korea's SsangYong Motor — a SUV-focused automaker that had gone through repeated ownership changes and a 2009 bankruptcy under prior Chinese owner SAIC — is being auctioned by creditors. Mahindra, with no prior international manufacturing presence, considers becoming the controlling owner of a foreign automaker for the first time.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD), with the deal driven by group corporate development; Pawan Goenka was a key architect of Mahindra's broader 2007–2011 acquisition spree (which also included Punjab Tractors, Satyam Computer Services — IT, not auto — REVA, two-wheeler maker Kinetic, and now SsangYong).
- Alternatives: (a) acquire a controlling stake in SsangYong to gain SUV engineering, diesel technology and an entry into Korean/European/Russian markets, (b) pursue a smaller technology-licensing or supply partnership instead of ownership, (c) skip international M&A and stay focused on the Indian domestic market.
- What actually happened: Mahindra signed a definitive agreement to acquire a 70% stake in SsangYong for $463 million, subscribing to $378 million of new shares plus $85 million of corporate bonds; the deal closed after Korean creditor approval by early 2011 (some sources cite the agreement as November 2010, completion around March 2011). Mahindra later raised its stake further (e.g., to ~73% in 2013, ~74.65% in 2019).
- Source: https://www.business-standard.com/article/companies/mahindra-buys-70-in-ssangyong-110112400046_1.html ; https://www.businesstoday.in/magazine/cover-story/story/anand-mahindra-mandm-company-acquisitions-26671-2011-09-14 ; https://www.team-bhp.com/news/mahindra-sell-majority-stake-ssangyong

**9. 2011–2014 — "Rebuild SsangYong's Product Line: Bet on the Tivoli"**
- Situation: Having bought a distressed automaker, Mahindra/SsangYong management must decide where to spend scarce development capital for the brand's post-acquisition comeback. They commit to an all-new compact crossover, the Tivoli, as the first clean-sheet model since the Mahindra takeover.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman, Mahindra Group); SsangYong's own CEO ran day-to-day Korean operations (a sequence of Korean executives, e.g. Lee Yoo-il era) under Mahindra board oversight.
- Alternatives: (a) invest heavily in one flagship new model (the eventual Tivoli) to re-establish credibility, (b) spread limited capital across refreshing multiple existing aging models, (c) deprioritize new product and focus purely on cost-cutting/restructuring.
- What actually happened: SsangYong spent roughly 350 billion Korean won and 42 months developing the Tivoli, launched January 2015 — the first new SsangYong model since the Mahindra buyout. Anand Mahindra called it "a significant new milestone in the growing partnership between Mahindra and Ssangyong" (per company/press coverage, exact wording per source below — treat as a close paraphrase rather than a verified word-for-word transcript).
- Outcome: SsangYong targeted 35,000 Tivoli units in 2015 and exceeded it — about 64,000 Tivolis were built/sold globally in 2015, becoming SsangYong's most successful new model in years, though brand awareness remained a persistent weakness limiting it from becoming the runaway hit hoped for.
- Source: https://www.wardsauto.com/industry/tivoli-key-piece-of-ssangyong-s-turnaround-plan ; https://www.domain-b.com/companies-organisations/firms-companies/ssangyong-to-launch-its-first-car-tivoli-since-mahindra-buyout ; https://www.autocarpro.in/news-international/mahindra-plans-rescue-ssangyong-7544

**10. 2012–2013 — "How Much More Korean Stock to Buy?"**
- Situation: Having taken control at 70%, Mahindra must decide how much further to consolidate ownership of SsangYong as the turnaround unfolds — full ownership offers more control but ties up more group capital in a still-unproven foreign subsidiary.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman & MD).
- Alternatives: (a) raise the stake further to deepen control and signal long-term commitment, (b) hold steady at 70%, (c) reduce exposure pending clearer turnaround signs.
- What actually happened: Mahindra & Mahindra raised its SsangYong stake to 73% by February 2013 (and continued increasing it over subsequent years, reaching roughly 74.65% by 2019).
- Source: https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/companies/m-m-to-raise-stake-in-ssangyong-unit-to-73-113021400393_1.html ; https://www.business-standard.com/article/pti-stories/m-m-hikes-stake-in-ssangyong-motor-to-74-65-pc-picks-shares-worth-rs-316-cr-119011600447_1.html

### ERA C — SUV Boom, Korean Bust, Pandemic Reckoning (2016–2021)

**11. OCT 2019 — "The Ford Alliance: Sharing the EV Burden"**
- Situation: Developing new SUV platforms and electric powertrains is increasingly expensive, and Mahindra wants scale partners to share the cost, particularly for emerging-market and electric vehicle programs. Ford, struggling for relevance and profitability in India, is looking for a way to stay in the market without bearing full cost alone.
- Top job: Anand Mahindra (Chairman); Pawan Goenka (MD, overseeing the deal alongside Group CFO functions) on the Mahindra side; Ford's global and India leadership on the other.
- Alternatives: (a) form a joint venture with Ford to co-develop SUVs (including electric variants) and share suppliers/powertrains/technology, (b) go it alone on EV/SUV platform development, (c) seek a different (e.g., Asian) partner instead of Ford.
- What actually happened: Ford and Mahindra entered a definitive agreement in October 2019 to form a $275 million joint venture — Mahindra holding 51%, Ford 49% — to develop and sell vehicles in India and export to emerging markets, including at least three new SUVs and electric variants under the Ford brand.
- Source: https://www.malaymail.com/news/money/2019/10/01/ford-mahindra-agree-us275m-deal-for-india-emerging-markets/1796165 ; https://www.thenewsminute.com/money/mahindra-and-ford-call-proposed-joint-venture-140639

**12. SEP–OCT 2020 — "The Thar Reborn"**
- Situation: Mahindra's Thar — descended directly from the original Jeep CKD lineage — is overdue for a generational redesign that can compete on modern road manners and features while keeping its rugged, off-road identity intact, in a market where lifestyle SUVs are surging.
- Top job: Pawan Goenka (MD & CEO, Mahindra & Mahindra), Rajesh Jejurikar (then heading Mahindra's auto sector) overseeing product strategy.
- Alternatives: (a) launch an all-new, more refined and feature-rich Thar aimed at a broader lifestyle audience while retaining true off-road capability, (b) keep the Thar a niche, barebones off-roader to avoid diluting its identity, (c) delay the redesign given pandemic uncertainty.
- What actually happened: The new-generation Thar was unveiled in early August 2020 and launched commercially on October 2, 2020. It received roughly 9,000 bookings within the first four days, crossed 20,000 bookings within a month, and ultimately surpassed 50,000 bookings, with waiting periods stretching to 7–11 months in various cities — later worsened by the global semiconductor shortage.
- Source: https://www.rushlane.com/2020-mahindra-thar-production-waiting-period-12381396.html ; https://gulfnews.com/auto/news/indias-mahindra-unveils-all-new-2021-thar-suv-1.1597736630887 ; https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/markets/m-m-gains-4-as-all-new-thar-suv-booking-crosses-9-000-mark-120100600445_1.html

**13. MAR–APR 2020 — "COVID Hits: Halt Production, Conserve Cash"**
- Situation: The COVID-19 pandemic forces an unprecedented nationwide lockdown in India. Mahindra must decide how aggressively to shut down operations and how to triage cash across its businesses — including its loss-making Korean subsidiary SsangYong, which was already in financial distress before the pandemic.
- Top job: Pawan Goenka (MD & CEO).
- Alternatives: (a) suspend manufacturing entirely and prioritize liquidity preservation across the group, (b) continue limited operations to protect market share, (c) keep funding SsangYong regardless of pandemic-driven group cash pressure.
- What actually happened: Mahindra suspended manufacturing at its Nagpur plant and halted production at Chakan (Pune) and Kandivali (Mumbai) in March 2020, alongside Maruti and Honda, as part of the broader Indian auto-industry shutdown. M&M's stock hit its lowest level since April 2010, tanking 51% in a month (per Business Standard), and group net profit reportedly plummeted roughly 85% with no near-term revival in sight, per contemporaneous reporting.
- Source: https://www.pressreader.com/india/millennium-post/20200323/282063394053333 ; https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/markets/m-m-hits-lowest-level-since-april-2010-stock-tanks-51-in-a-month-120032500288_1.html ; https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/pti-stories/mahindra-net-profit-plummets-85-doesn-t-see-revival-soon-120021101438_1.html

**14. APR 2020 — "Cutting SsangYong Loose: The Board Says No"**
- Situation: SsangYong needs a fresh equity injection (reported around $406 million / Rs 3,078 crore) to stay solvent, just as Mahindra itself is reeling from COVID-19's hit to its core Indian business. The board must decide whether to keep bailing out the Korean subsidiary or to finally cut the cord.
- Top job: Pawan Goenka (MD & CEO, Mahindra & Mahindra), with the decision made at the M&M board level.
- Alternatives: (a) approve the fresh equity injection to keep SsangYong alive, (b) refuse further investment and seek alternative investors/buyers for SsangYong, (c) provide partial/bridge funding while a longer-term solution is found.
- What actually happened: On April 4, 2020, Mahindra & Mahindra's board rejected the plan to inject roughly Rs 3,078 crore (~$406 million) of fresh equity into SsangYong, with the company stating: "The magnitude of the unfortunate and unforeseen crisis created by Covid-19 has compelled us to take the difficult decision." Mahindra signaled it would not fund SsangYong further and asked it to seek outside investment instead.
- Quote (sourced): "The magnitude of the unfortunate and unforeseen crisis created by Covid-19 has compelled us to take the difficult decision" — Mahindra & Mahindra company statement, April 2020.
- Source: https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/automobile/m-m-board-rejects-plan-to-pump-in-fresh-equity-in-ssangyong-motor-120040400054_1.html ; https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/news-ani/m-m-refuses-rs-3-078-crore-fresh-equity-injection-in-ssangyong-120040400369_1.html

**15. DEC 2020 — "SsangYong Files for Bankruptcy"**
- Situation: Without fresh capital from Mahindra and unable to meet loan repayments (about 60 billion won) or persuade creditors to reschedule, SsangYong is forced into court receivership.
- Top job: Pawan Goenka (MD & CEO, Mahindra & Mahindra) overseeing the group's response; SsangYong's own Korean management filed the petition.
- Alternatives at this point were narrow: (a) let SsangYong go through court receivership/bankruptcy protection while Mahindra searches for a new investor, (b) attempt an eleventh-hour rescue financing despite the board's earlier refusal, (c) abandon the subsidiary entirely and write it off immediately.
- What actually happened: SsangYong filed for court receivership/bankruptcy on December 21, 2020, after missing the loan repayment. By mid-2021, Mahindra confirmed it was willing to relinquish its position as SsangYong's largest shareholder if a suitable new investor could be found, while ruling out further capital injection itself.
- Source: https://paultan.org/2020/12/22/ssangyong-motor-in-dire-straits-files-for-bankruptcy/ ; https://www.rushlane.com/mahindra-ssangyong-bankruptcy-filed-12387461.html ; https://www.koreaherald.com/article/2597179

**16. DEC 31, 2020 / JAN 2021 — "Letting the Ford JV Die"**
- Situation: The Ford-Mahindra joint venture's definitive agreement carried a December 31, 2020 deadline. Pandemic-driven shifts in both companies' global strategies (Ford was simultaneously restructuring its international operations) made the original deal's economics look outdated.
- Top job: Pawan Goenka (MD & CEO, Mahindra & Mahindra) in his final months in the role; Ford's global leadership under CEO Jim Farley (who took over Ford in October 2020).
- Alternatives: (a) renegotiate the JV's terms to reflect new conditions, (b) let the agreement lapse and formally end the partnership, (c) extend the deadline while continuing talks.
- What actually happened: Ford and Mahindra mutually determined they would not complete the joint venture after the December 31, 2020 deadline passed; the companies formally confirmed in early January 2021 (with a fuller joint statement around April 1, 2021) that they would not collaborate on any of the previously agreed JV projects, citing "fundamental changes in global economic and business conditions... caused, in part, by the global pandemic."
- Quote (sourced, paraphrase of joint statement language): "...driven by fundamental changes in global economic and business conditions... over the past 15 months" — Ford/Mahindra joint statement.
- Source: https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2021/1/1/bb-fordreverses-out-of-mahindra-car-making-joint-venture-in-india ; https://www.cnbc.com/2021/04/01/ford-and-mahindra-to-end-collaboration-on-all-projects-in-india.html ; https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/37996/000003799620000075/exhibit99.htm

**17. MAR 2021 — "Pawan Goenka Hands Over the Keys"**
- Situation: After 27 years at Mahindra and roughly the final several years as MD & CEO (steering the Scorpio/XUV era, the SsangYong acquisition's aftermath, the Ford JV, and the pandemic crisis), Goenka's tenure as the auto/farm business's chief executive comes to an end, coinciding with broader Mahindra Group leadership renewal.
- Top job (outgoing): Pawan Goenka, MD & CEO, Mahindra & Mahindra. (incoming): Dr. Anish Shah, then Deputy MD and Group CFO.

- Situation: With SsangYong in court receivership and Mahindra no longer willing to fund it, Mahindra/SsangYong's creditors run a bid process. A consortium led by Korean EV startup Edison Motors emerges as the winning bidder.
