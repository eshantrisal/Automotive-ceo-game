# RESEARCH DOSSIER: Ford Motor Company

Prepared for "Be an Automotive Industry CEO" — narrative strategy game data build.
All facts below are sourced from web research conducted June 2026. Citations are inline. Where a fact, quote, or figure could not be verified to a primary or major-press source, this is flagged explicitly with **[UNVERIFIED]**.

## 1. Company Snapshot — Candidate KPI Dimensions

Ford's central strategic tension across 2006–2026 is: **a 120-year-old, capital-intensive legacy manufacturer trying to fund a self-disruptive transition (EV/software) without losing the cash-generating combustion/truck business that pays for it all, all while carrying enormous fixed costs (UAW labor, plants, pension/legacy liabilities) that nimbler rivals (Tesla, Chinese EV makers) don't carry.**

Recommended 3 KPIs:

1. **Liquidity / Cash (key: `cash`)** — Automotive gross cash and access to credit. Ford's defining 2006 decision was borrowing against literally everything it owned specifically to survive a future cash crisis. Cash determines whether Ford can fund restructuring, EV losses, or a UAW contract without going to Washington or bankruptcy court.
2. **Core Profitability / Truck-and-Pro Cash Engine (key: `core`)** — Health of the legacy combustion, truck (F-Series) and Ford Pro commercial business. This is the "cash cow" that must keep generating $10B+ EBIT a year to fund the EV pivot. A stand-in label could be "F-150 / Pro Margin."
3. **Future Readiness / EV-Tech Competitiveness (key: `future`)** — Ford's competitiveness on EVs, software-defined vehicles, and autonomy vs. Tesla and Chinese EV makers (BYD, etc.). Tracks the existential question: can a 1903 company become a software company before its profitable present runs out of road?

(Alternative/4th candidate if the format wants something closer to Tesla's "Confidence": **Labor & Stakeholder Trust** — UAW relations, dealer network, and Ford-family/board confidence — given how much of Ford's history pivots on labor contracts and boardroom trust in the CEO.)

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "Bet the Company" (2006–2014)
Bill Ford Jr. concedes he isn't the right CEO for a crisis and recruits an outsider, Alan Mulally, who immediately does two unthinkable things: mortgages essentially all of Ford's tangible and intangible assets — plants, equipment, patents, even the Blue Oval trademark — for a $23.6B credit facility *before* anyone knew a financial crisis was coming, and simultaneously sheds the luxury-brand empire (Aston Martin, Jaguar, Land Rover, Volvo) built by his predecessor to refocus capital and attention on the core Ford/Lincoln business. The bet pays off explosively: when credit markets freeze in 2008 and GM and Chrysler are forced into bankruptcy/bailout, Ford alone has the liquidity to survive on its own terms — a singular, era-defining gamble that becomes a Harvard Business School case study.

### Era B — "The Lost Decade of Slow Pivot" (2014–2017)
Mulally hands off to insider Mark Fields just as the industry's center of gravity begins shifting toward EVs, software, ride-sharing, and autonomy — exactly while Ford's stock stagnates despite record profits from trucks and SUVs. Fields talks about Ford becoming "an auto and a mobility company" but investors see a company that looks, prices, and behaves like old Detroit. The tension: how much do you reinvest record truck profits into unproven future bets when Wall Street is valuing you on quarterly truck/SUV margins, not a 10-year thesis? The era ends abruptly when the board loses patience.

### Era C — "Fitness, Then Fight" (2017–2020)
Outsider-again pick Jim Hackett (from office-furniture maker Steelcase) is installed to make Ford "fit" — cutting $11B of costs, killing nearly the entire North American sedan lineup to double down on trucks/SUVs, and slow-walking a long-promised reinvention around EVs and autonomy that investors feel is moving too slowly relative to Tesla's stock-market ascent. The COVID-19 pandemic hits mid-era, simultaneously gutting production and forcing Ford to prove it can pivot operations (ventilators, masks) on a dime. The strategic tension: cut your way to "fitness" without cutting away your future.

### Era D — "Electric Reckoning" (2020–2026)
Jim Farley, a career "car guy," takes over and moves fast: he commits $30B+ to electrification, splits Ford into three units (Ford Blue for combustion, Ford Model e for EVs, Ford Pro for commercial) to expose the EV business's true profit-and-loss picture, launches the F-150 Lightning and Mustang Mach-E into a genuine demand boom — and then, as EV demand cools and Model e racks up billions in annual losses, has to publicly reverse course: killing a three-row electric SUV, delaying a next-gen electric truck plant, doubling down on hybrids, and betting the next phase of EVs on a secretive low-cost "universal EV platform" team led by a former Tesla engineer. The tension: having just proven Ford *could* build compelling EVs at scale, can it make them profitable before the capital runs out or the board's patience does?

## 3. Decision Points

### ERA A — "Bet the Company" (2006–2014)

---

**JUL–SEP 2006 — "The Outsider"**
- **Situation:** Ford lost $1.4B in Q2 2006 alone and was on pace for a record annual loss. Bill Ford Jr., the founder's great-grandson, had been CEO since 2001 but concluded publicly that the company needed someone with turnaround experience he didn't have. He quietly approached Alan Mulally — then head of Boeing Commercial Airplanes, who had just led Boeing through its own post-9/11 crisis and 787 development — first contacting him in July 2006.
- **CEO at the time:** Bill Ford Jr. (outgoing); Alan Mulally (incoming).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Promote an internal Ford lifer to project stability and institutional knowledge; recruit another auto-industry executive (a "known quantity"); Bill Ford stays on as CEO and brings in a strong COO/CFO instead of ceding the top job entirely.
- **What actually happened:** On September 5, 2006, Ford announced Mulally as President and CEO, with Bill Ford staying on as Executive Chairman. It was a stunning outside-the-industry hire — Mulally had never worked in cars.
- **Quote:** Bill Ford on the hire (joint interview), describing the rationale for going outside the industry — see Bloomberg interview below. **[Note: a precise word-for-word quote from this specific announcement was not independently re-verified beyond the Bloomberg interview transcript; treat short paraphrases with care.]**
- **Source:** [CNN Money, "Ford names Alan Mulally as new CEO," Sept 5, 2006](https://money.cnn.com/2006/09/05/news/companies/ford/); [Bloomberg, "An Interview with Bill Ford and Alan Mulally," Sept 6, 2006](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2006-09-06/an-interview-with-bill-ford-and-alan-mulally); [Directors & Boards, "How the Ford board recruited Alan Mulally"](https://www.directorsandboards.com/board-composition/director-recruitment/singlehow-ford-board-recruited-alan-mulally/).

---

**JAN 2006 — "The Way Forward" (pre-Mulally restructuring)**
- **Situation:** Before Mulally arrived, Ford under Bill Ford unveiled "The Way Forward" on January 23, 2006: a plan to close 14 plants (7 assembly, 7 parts) and cut up to 30,000 jobs in response to collapsing North American market share and overcapacity.
- **CEO at the time:** Bill Ford Jr.
- **Alternatives on the table:** A smaller, slower restructuring; protecting plants via attrition only; an even more radical immediate shutdown of underperforming brands.
- **What actually happened:** The plan was announced January 2006 and then, after Mulally's arrival, accelerated in September 2006 — Mulally pushed the targeted job cuts and capacity reductions to be completed years earlier than originally planned (by 2008 rather than 2012), adding two more plants to the closure list (Maumee Stamping, Ohio; Essex Engine, Ontario).
- **Outcome:** By 2008, Ford's assembly capacity had been cut by roughly 26%.
- **Source:** [Wikipedia, "The Way Forward"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Way_Forward); [WSWS, "Ford to slash 44,000 jobs," Sept 2006](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2006/09/jobs-s16.html); [Cornell Catherwood Library archive on plant closings](https://catherwood.library.cornell.edu/?p=39518).

---

**NOV–DEC 2006 — "Mortgage the Blue Oval"** (the marquee decision point)
- **Situation:** Within roughly two months of taking the job, Mulally proposed something no major automaker had done: borrowing against virtually all of Ford's tangible and intangible North American assets — plants, equipment, accounts receivable, inventory, intercompany notes, equity in foreign subsidiaries, and U.S. intellectual property and trademarks (explicitly including the Blue Oval) — to build a multibillion-dollar liquidity cushion in case of "unexpected events," at a time when no recession had yet been declared. Ford's own Nov 29, 2006 investor presentation literally itemizes the Blue Oval trademark as loan collateral on its "Eligible Value" / "Borrowing Base" table.
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally (3 months into the job).
- **Exact terms (verified via Ford's SEC 8-K filing, Nov 29, 2006):** An $8 billion 5-year secured bank revolver, a $7 billion 7-year secured Term Loan B, plus additional unsecured capital markets transactions (including convertible debt) bringing total new funding to roughly $18 billion in secured facilities and approximately $23.5–23.6 billion in total new liquidity once combined with existing cash and credit lines. Collateral included U.S. plant/property/equipment, U.S. receivables and inventory, a 100% pledge of stock in primary U.S. subsidiaries (including Ford Credit), 66–100% pledge of first-tier foreign subsidiary stock, and U.S. intellectual property and trademarks (independently valued for the deal). The facility closed and funded on December 15, 2006.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Raise smaller, unsecured amounts and preserve flexibility/credit rating (Ford's ratings were already junk-grade — S&P "B," Moody's "B3," both negative — so unsecured borrowing would have been far more expensive or unavailable at scale); wait and raise capital opportunistically as needed rather than all at once; pursue asset sales first and borrow only the remaining gap.
- **What actually happened:** Ford executed the full financing as described, becoming famous in retrospect for "betting the company" — committing essentially all its assets as collateral over a year before Lehman Brothers collapsed. The decision is widely credited as the single reason Ford avoided the fate of GM and Chrysler in 2008–2009.
- **Outcome with dates:** Facility priced/marketed at a Nov 29, 2006 lenders' meeting, final commitments due Dec 7, 2006, closed and funded Dec 15, 2006. By 2008, when the financial crisis hit and credit markets froze, Ford already had its liquidity locked in — GM and Chrysler had to seek emergency funding from a hostile Congress and ultimately filed Chapter 11 in 2009; Ford did neither.
- **Quote:** Mulally was reportedly mocked by some bankers in the room — narrative detail repeated across retrospectives (e.g., Motley Fool) describing how he "gathered more than 400 bankers into a ballroom" to pitch the deal, though the precise number and a verbatim Mulally quote from that meeting could not be independently confirmed in primary sources found. **[Use with caution — treat the "400 bankers" detail as secondary-source color, not confirmed primary fact.]**
- **Source (primary):** [Ford Motor Co. SEC Form 8-K Exhibit 99.1, "Secured Financing Presentation," filed Nov 29, 2006](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000037996/000114036106017120/ex99_1.htm) — contains the exact collateral schedule, tranche amounts, and closing timetable. Secondary: [Motley Fool, "Alan Mulally: The Savior of Ford," 2015](https://www.fool.com/investing/general/2015/05/16/alan-mulally-the-savior-of-ford.aspx); [TIME, "How Ford Earned its Blue Oval Back," 2012](https://business.time.com/2012/05/25/how-ford-earned-its-blue-oval-back/).

**2006–2008 — "Selling the Crown Jewels" (Premier Automotive Group breakup begins)**
- **Situation:** Mulally inherited the Premier Automotive Group (PAG) — Aston Martin, Jaguar, Land Rover, and Volvo, plus Lincoln (which had already been folded back into Ford's direct control in 2002) — assembled in the late 1990s under CEO Jacques Nasser as a luxury-brand bet. By the mid-2000s PAG brands were a capital drain: Jaguar in particular had not been reliably profitable. The "One Ford" philosophy demanded resources be refocused on the core Ford and Lincoln brands and shared global platforms rather than a portfolio of disparate luxury marques.
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Keep investing in PAG and try to fix Jaguar's profitability; sell only the weakest brand (Jaguar) and keep Land Rover and Volvo; sell everything at once versus a phased divestiture to maximize sale prices.
- **What actually happened:** Ford sold Aston Martin in March 2007 for $925 million to a consortium of investors (Ford retained a small minority stake initially). It then sold Jaguar and Land Rover together to India's Tata Motors, announced June 2007 / completed June 2008, for approximately $2.3 billion. Volvo was sold last, to China's Geely Holding Group, for $1.8 billion, announced 2009 and completed August 2010.
- **Outcome:** The divestitures, completed just before and during the financial crisis, both raised cash and simplified Ford's capital allocation exactly when liquidity mattered most — though Jaguar Land Rover went on to become highly profitable under Tata in the early-to-mid 2010s, a frequently cited irony.
- **Source:** [Wikipedia, "Premier Automotive Group"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_Automotive_Group); [Bloomberg Opinion, "Ford Is Left Standing By Its Supercharged Rejects," 2018](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2018-09-11/ford-is-left-standing-by-its-supercharged-rejects); [Jalopnik, "Why Did Ford Sell Jaguar And Land Rover? It's Complicated"](https://www.jalopnik.com/why-did-ford-sell-jaguar-and-land-rover-it-s-complicat-1851753235/).

**DEC 4, 2008 — "The Hearing Room"**
- **Situation:** With GM and Chrysler publicly pleading for a federal bailout, Congress summoned all three Detroit CEOs to testify before the Senate Banking Committee. Ford did not need money, but its fate was tied to its suppliers and dealer networks, many shared with GM and Chrysler — if either collapsed, Ford's supply chain could collapse too. The optics question: how do you ask for help (or decline it) without looking either greedy or naive?
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Request a standby line of government credit "just in case" even though Ford didn't need it immediately (which Ford in fact did request, as a precaution, alongside GM and Chrysler); refuse any government assistance on principle; lobby quietly for industry-wide supplier-stabilization help without personally testifying.
- **What actually happened:** Mulally testified that Ford did not anticipate a liquidity crisis in 2009 and stated the company had "no desire to access the government money," while supporting government assistance for GM and Chrysler to prevent supply-chain contagion. Ford did request access to a contingent credit line as a precaution but never drew on TARP/bailout funds, and avoided Chapter 11.
- **Quote:** Mulally: "From Day 1, we had no desire to access the government money." — per CNBC retrospective citing his comments.
- **Source:** [CNBC, "How Ford Avoided the Meltdown that Hit GM, Chrysler," April 2009](https://www.cnbc.com/2009/04/09/how-ford-avoided-the-meltdown-that-hit-gm-chrysler.html); [Mulally's written Senate testimony, Dec 4, 2008, Senate Banking Committee](https://www.banking.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/Mulally0Ford12408FinalWrittenTestimony.pdf).

---

**2009 — "The Lone Survivor's Dividend"**
- **Situation:** With GM and Chrysler in bankruptcy and under government control, Ford had a once-in-a-generation opportunity: be the only Detroit brand that hadn't taken a bailout or filed Chapter 11, at the exact moment American consumers were deciding which of the Big Three to trust again.
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Run a marketing campaign explicitly contrasting Ford's no-bailout status against GM/Chrysler (some "Ford didn't take your money" ad concepts existed in the industry conversation); stay quiet and let the financial results speak; aggressively cut prices to capture share from weakened rivals.
- **What actually happened:** Ford leaned into the "we didn't take a bailout" narrative in its marketing and investor communications. The company posted a $2.7 billion net profit in 2009 — its first annual profit since 2005 — and gained U.S. market share for the first time since 1995, ending the year near 15% share, about a full point higher than 2008.
- **Outcome:** Ford's surprise 2009 turnaround set up the broader Mulally-era recovery; 2010 brought continued share and earnings growth as Ford differentiated itself from its bailed-out rivals in consumer perception.
- **Source:** [CNN Money, "Ford: First profitable year since '05," Jan 28, 2010](https://money.cnn.com/2010/01/28/news/companies/ford_results/index.htm); [Just-Auto, "US: Ford makes $2.7bn net profit in 2009"](https://www.just-auto.com/news/us-ford-makes-2-7bn-net-profit-in-2009/).

---

**2012 — "Earning Back the Blue Oval"**
- **Situation:** By 2012, Ford's financial recovery was strong enough that the company could consider paying down or refinancing the 2006 secured debt and reclaiming the Blue Oval trademark and other assets pledged as collateral.
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Keep the cheap secured debt in place as long as possible since the rates were favorable; pay it down aggressively to remove the symbolic and practical overhang of having mortgaged the company; do a partial paydown.
- **What actually happened:** Ford's credit rating was upgraded back to investment grade by Moody's in May 2012, reflecting the company's deleveraging and recovery — effectively "earning back" the Blue Oval symbolically, even as some of the underlying facilities continued to roll off over subsequent years.
- **Source:** [Automotive News, "Ford's credit rating raised to investment grade by Moody's," May 2012](https://www.autonews.com/article/20120522/OEM/120529973/ford-s-credit-rating-raised-to-investment-grade-by-moody-s); [TIME, "How Ford Earned its Blue Oval Back," 2012](https://business.time.com/2012/05/25/how-ford-earned-its-blue-oval-back/).

### ERA B — "The Lost Decade of Slow Pivot" (2014–2017)

---

**APR–JUL 2014 — "The Handoff"**
- **Situation:** Mulally, 68, had led Ford for nearly 8 years through the crisis and recovery and signaled it was time to retire. The board needed to decide between continuing outside hires (the formula that had worked) or promoting from within, where COO Mark Fields — a 25-year Ford veteran who had also run Ford of Europe and Ford Americas — was the clear heir apparent.
- **CEO at the time:** Alan Mulally (outgoing); Mark Fields (incoming).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Recruit another outside turnaround specialist as a hedge against complacency; have Mulally stay on longer given strong results; name Fields but with a longer transition runway.
- **What actually happened:** Ford announced May 1, 2014 that Mulally would retire and Fields would become President and CEO, with the transition completed July 1, 2014 — about six months earlier than originally anticipated, on Mulally's own recommendation that Ford's leadership team (Fields included) was ready sooner than planned.
- **Source:** [CSMonitor, "Ford CEO Alan Mulally to retire, be replaced by Mark Fields," May 2014](https://www.csmonitor.com/Business/In-Gear/2014/0501/Ford-CEO-Alan-Mulally-to-retire-be-replaced-by-Mark-Fields); [Detroit Bureau, "Ford's Mulally, Fields Make Early, Informal CEO Transition," June 2014](https://www.thedetroitbureau.com/2014/06/fords-mulally-fields-make-early-informal-ceo-transition/).

---

**2015–2016 — "Mobility Talk, Truck Money"**
- **Situation:** Under Fields, Ford generated record profits — driven almost entirely by trucks and SUVs (F-150, Explorer) — while publicly repositioning itself as "an auto and a mobility company," investing in Ford Smart Mobility, ride-sharing pilots, and autonomous vehicle research (a 2016 pledge to have a Level 4 autonomous vehicle in commercial operation by 2021). The strategic tension: do you reinvest record near-term truck profits into unproven mobility bets at a scale that satisfies Silicon Valley-style investors, or protect the dividend and margins that satisfy traditional auto investors?
- **CEO at the time:** Mark Fields.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Spend aggressively and visibly on autonomy/mobility to compete with the Tesla narrative in investor perception; stay disciplined on core-business capital allocation and let the mobility bets be smaller/slower; pursue M&A (acquiring a tech/mobility startup outright) instead of organic R&D.
- **What actually happened:** Ford made the 2021 self-driving pledge and created a dedicated Ford Smart Mobility LLC subsidiary (2016) but was perceived by investors as moving cautiously and incrementally relative to Tesla's stock-market momentum and GM's higher-profile Cruise/Lyft moves. Ford's stock continued to lag despite strong underlying profits.
- **Outcome:** Ford shares fell roughly 37% during Fields's nearly three-year tenure, with Ford's market capitalization at one point dropping below Tesla's, even though Tesla was far smaller in revenue and unprofitable.
- **Source:** [NPR, "Ford Replaces CEO Mark Fields In Management Shake-Up," May 2017](https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2017/05/22/529459034/ford-replacing-ceo-mark-fields-in-management-shakeup); [Motley Fool, "Why Ford's Board Ousted CEO Mark Fields," May 2017](https://www.fool.com/investing/2017/05/22/ford-ousts-ceo-mark-fields-in-dramatic-overhaul.aspx).

---

**MAY 2017 — "Excised"**
- **Situation:** Ahead of Ford's 2017 annual shareholder meeting, investors openly criticized management over the stock's poor performance despite strong profits. The board, led by Executive Chairman Bill Ford, had to decide whether to back Fields through the long-term mobility transition or act on shareholder pressure immediately.
- **CEO at the time:** Mark Fields (until ouster); Jim Hackett (incoming, previously head of Ford Smart Mobility, and formerly CEO of office-furniture maker Steelcase 1994–2014).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Give Fields more time/runway given the long-cycle nature of auto investment; make incremental management changes below the CEO level instead; replace Fields with another internal candidate rather than Hackett, who came from outside the auto industry himself (echoing the 2006 Mulally playbook).
- **What actually happened:** On May 22, 2017, Ford abruptly replaced Fields with Hackett. Fields, 56, "retired" (with a reported exit package potentially worth up to $57.5 million depending on calculation), and Hackett — who had no prior automotive operating experience before joining Ford's board and then leading Ford Smart Mobility — was elevated to CEO.
- **Quote:** Bill Ford Jr., to shareholders: "Look, we're as frustrated as you are by the stock price," adding that for the Ford family, "most of our net worth is tied up in the company." On the leadership change: "This is a time of unprecedented change... A time of great change requires a transformational leader, and thankfully we have that in Jim."
- **Source:** [Automotive News, "Ford fires CEO Mark Fields, hires Jim Hackett," May 2017](https://www.autonews.com/article/20170522/OEM02/170529994/fields-departs-in-shake-up-ex-steelcase-head-jim-hackett-named-ceo); [Spokesman-Review, "Ford names Hackett CEO as stock drop prompts Fields' ouster"](https://www.spokesman.com/stories/2017/may/22/ford-names-hackett-ceo-as-stock-drop-prompts-field/); [IndustryWeek, "Ford's Fields May Walk Away With $57.5 Million After Ouster"](https://www.industryweek.com/leadership/companies-executives/article/22017736/fords-fields-may-walk-away-with-575-million-after-ouster).

### ERA C — "Fitness, Then Fight" (2017–2020)

---

**JUL 2018 — "The $11 Billion Redesign"**
- **Situation:** Hackett had spent months talking about improving Ford's organizational "fitness" — cost discipline, speed, accountability — but investors and media grew impatient for specifics. The company needed a concrete restructuring plan with real numbers attached.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Hackett.
- **Alternatives on the table:** A smaller, faster-to-execute cost-cutting plan focused only on overhead; a much larger, multi-year transformation touching products, plants, and headcount; delay the announcement further to refine details (which is effectively what had already been happening and drawing criticism).
- **What actually happened:** In July 2018, Ford detailed a restructuring expected to cost roughly $11 billion in EBIT charges and about $7 billion in related cash costs, executed over several years, eventually touching global operations (especially struggling European and South American units), salaried headcount, and the U.S. product portfolio.
- **Outcome:** By October 2018 / into 2019, the plan resulted in major reductions to the ~70,000-person global salaried workforce (roughly 7,000 salaried job cuts confirmed by May 2019) and a wholesale restructuring of Ford's product lineup (see next entry).
- **Quote:** TheStreet characterized Wall Street's reaction as skeptical, noting Hackett himself called the restructuring "a massive undertaking" that had to be "very thoughtfully orchestrated."
- **Source:** [TheStreet, "Ford CEO Jim Hackett's Restructuring Plans Provoke Wall Street Skepticism"](https://www.thestreet.com/investing/ford-restructuring-plans-receives-mixed-reviews-14663958); [TechCrunch, "Ford will slash 7,000 salaried jobs by August," May 2019](https://techcrunch.com/2019/05/20/ford-will-slash-7000-salaried-jobs-by-august).

---

**APR 25, 2018 — "Killing the Sedan"**
- **Situation:** Ford's North American car lineup (Fiesta, Focus, Fusion, C-Max, Taurus) was increasingly unprofitable as American buyers shifted en masse to trucks, SUVs and crossovers, which already made up the bulk of Ford's North American profit. Ford had to decide whether to keep funding the next generation of sedans for a shrinking, low-margin segment.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Hackett.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Continue investing modestly in redesigned sedans to preserve brand breadth and fleet/rental relationships; phase out only the weakest nameplates (e.g., C-Max) while keeping Fusion and Focus; exit cars entirely except performance/halo models.
- **What actually happened:** On April 25, 2018, Ford announced it would stop selling nearly every traditional car in North America, keeping only the Mustang and a single crossover-like "Focus Active," and redirect investment toward trucks, SUVs, and a new category blending car and utility attributes. Current-generation Focus production ended May 2018, Taurus in March 2019, Fiesta in May 2019.
- **Outcome:** By the announcement, Ford said roughly 90% of its North American product portfolio by volume would be trucks, utilities, and commercial vehicles.
- **Source:** [TechCrunch, "Ford to stop selling every car in North America but the Mustang and Focus Active," April 2018](https://techcrunch.com/2018/04/25/ford-to-stop-selling-every-car-in-north-america-but-the-mustang-and-focus-active/); [NPR, "Ford To Phase Out 'Traditional Ford Sedans'," April 2018](https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2018/04/26/605971051/ford-to-phase-out-traditional-ford-sedans-such-as-fusion-and-taurus-in-the-u-s); [Ford 8-K press release, April 2018](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000037996/000003799618000033/exhibit991pressreleaseforapr.pdf).

---

**NOV 17, 2019 — "The Electric Mustang Gamble"**
- **Situation:** Ford needed a flagship product to prove it could compete in EVs, where Tesla had a multi-year head start and growing cultural cachet. Internally, the company debated whether to build a conventional, low-risk electric crossover under a new nameplate, or take the enormous brand risk of attaching the Mustang name — Ford's most emotionally loaded badge — to a four-door electric SUV.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Hackett (Bill Ford Jr. as Executive Chairman was the most visible voice at the reveal).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Launch a no-name-recognition-risk EV under an entirely new sub-brand; badge it as a Ford Edge/Escape EV variant; delay the EV crossover further to refine range/performance before any reveal.
- **What actually happened:** Ford revealed the Mustang Mach-E on November 17, 2019, in Hawthorne, California — explicitly trading on the Mustang name for a 5-seat electric crossover with 210–300 miles of range, backed by a previously announced $11.5 billion electrification investment plan through 2022.
- **Quote:** Bill Ford Jr.: "We are really pushing our chips in on the table with this vehicle."
- **Source:** [NBC News, "Ford's electric horse: US carmaker bets big on new Mustang"](https://www.aljazeera.com/ajimpact/ford-electric-horse-carmaker-bets-big-mustang-191118054234745.html); [Electrek, Mach-E overview](https://electrek.co/guides/ford-mach-e/).

---

**APR 2019 — "Backing a Pickup Startup"**
- **Situation:** Ford wanted EV and "skateboard platform" technology faster than it could build alone, and identified Michigan-based startup Rivian — then developing an electric pickup and SUV — as a potential partner/acquisition target for shared platform development.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Hackett (Bill Ford Jr. led the deal announcement).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Build the platform entirely in-house; acquire Rivian outright rather than taking a minority stake; partner with a different EV startup or supplier (e.g., battery-focused partners) instead of a vehicle-platform startup.
- **What actually happened:** Ford announced a $500 million equity investment in Rivian in April 2019, intending to co-develop a new Ford battery-electric vehicle using Rivian's "skateboard" platform.
- **Outcome:** The joint vehicle-development plan was cancelled in November 2021 as Ford pivoted to developing the F-150 Lightning and other EVs on its own platforms; Ford later substantially sold down its Rivian equity stake (disclosed February 2023), realizing investment gains from Rivian's 2021 IPO before the relationship ended.
- **Quote:** Bill Ford, Ford's Executive Chairman: "We are excited to invest in and partner with Rivian."
- **Source:** [Electrek, "Ford invests $500 million in electric pickup startup Rivian," April 2019](https://electrek.co/2019/04/24/ford-invests-rivian-electric-pickup-truck/); [CNBC, "Ford and Rivian cancel plans to jointly develop an electric vehicle," Nov 2021](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/11/19/ford-and-rivian-cancel-plans-to-jointly-develop-an-electric-vehicle.html); [Electrek, "Ford and Rivian go their separate ways," Feb 2023](https://electrek.co/2023/02/10/ford-and-rivian-go-their-separate-ways/).

---

**AUG 2020 — "Hackett Steps Aside"**
- **Situation:** Three years into Hackett's "fitness" restructuring, Ford's stock had actually fallen further under Hackett than it had under Fields, even as the foundational restructuring and EV product pipeline (Mach-E, Lightning in development) were taking shape. The board had to decide whether Hackett should see the EV pivot through to launch or hand off to COO Jim Farley, a "car guy" seen as a stronger product and brand operator.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Hackett (outgoing); Jim Farley (incoming).
- **Alternatives on the table:** Let Hackett complete the EV product launches (Mach-E, Lightning) before transitioning; bring in another outside executive; promote a different internal candidate over Farley.
- **What actually happened:** Ford announced August 4, 2020 that Hackett would retire and Farley — Ford's COO since February 2020 and previously head of Ford's global markets and Lincoln brand — would become President and CEO effective October 1, 2020. Hackett stayed on as a special advisor through the transition until March 2021.
- **Source:** [CNN Business, "Ford CEO Jim Hackett to retire and be replaced by COO Jim Farley," Aug 2020](https://www.cnn.com/2020/08/04/cars/ford-ceo-jim-hackett-retire/index.html); [Ford 8-K, Aug 2020](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000037996/000003799620000059/exhibiu99.htm); [Ford Authority, "Ford Stock Price Fell Further Under CEO Jim Hackett Than His Predecessor"](https://fordauthority.com/2020/03/ford-stock-price-fell-further-under-ceo-jim-hackett-than-his-predecessor/).

### ERA D — "Electric Reckoning" (2020–2026)

---

**MAY 26, 2021 — "Ford+" and the $30 Billion Bet**
- **Situation:** Eight months into his tenure, Farley needed to convince Wall Street that Ford could be a credible EV and software company, not just a truck company dabbling in electrification, while Tesla's market cap dwarfed all of Detroit combined.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Maintain the prior, smaller electrification budget (~$22 billion through 2023) and grow it incrementally; spend even more aggressively to match or exceed GM's public EV commitments; focus capital primarily on Ford Pro's commercial/software opportunity instead of consumer EVs.
- **What actually happened:** At Ford's "Delivering Ford+" Capital Markets Day on May 26, 2021, Farley raised planned EV/electrification spending to more than $30 billion through 2025 (up from ~$22 billion), targeted 40% of global vehicle volume being all-electric by 2030, and announced a new in-house battery development effort and the standalone Ford Pro commercial unit.
- **Quote:** Jim Farley: "This is our biggest opportunity for growth and value creation since Henry Ford started to scale the Model T."
- **Source:** [TechCrunch, "Ford's $30B investment in electric revs up in-house battery R&D," May 2021](https://techcrunch.com/2021/05/26/fords-30b-investment-in-electric-revs-up-in-house-battery-rd/); [Bloomberg, "Ford's Farley Stokes Stock With His $30 Billion Bid in EV Race," May 2021](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-05-26/ford-boosts-spending-on-electric-cars-batteries-to-30-billion); [Ford 8-K, May 2021](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000037996/000003799621000035/exhibit99tomay262021ford.htm).

---

**MAY 19, 2021 — "The Lightning Strikes"**
- **Situation:** Ford's best-selling, most profitable product on Earth is the F-150 pickup. Electrifying it was both the biggest opportunity (the truck buyer base is enormous) and the biggest risk (any stumble damages Ford's single most important franchise).
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Launch the electric truck under a different, risk-isolating sub-brand rather than the F-150 name; launch a smaller-volume/halo electric truck first to test demand; wait for battery costs and charging infrastructure to mature further before committing the flagship nameplate.
- **What actually happened:** Ford unveiled the F-150 Lightning on May 19, 2021. Demand exceeded expectations dramatically: roughly 20,000 reservations on day one, 44,000 within 48 hours, and nearly 200,000 reservations by December 2021 — described by Ford as roughly three years of backlog at then-planned production rates. Production began April 26, 2022, with initial 2022 output of around 15,000 units, targeting 55,000 in 2023 and 80,000 in 2024.
- **Outcome:** Despite the historic demand signal, actual realized sales later undershot the reservation hype as EV demand growth slowed industry-wide in 2023; Ford repeatedly cut Lightning production targets in 2023–2024 and idled the plant at points (e.g., a production pause in early 2023, restart announced for March 13, 2023).
- **Source:** [Electrek, "Ford says it now has nearly 200,000 reservations for F-150 Lightning," Dec 2021](https://electrek.co/2021/12/07/ford-f-150-lightning-reservations-3-years-backlog/); [Wikipedia, "Ford F-150 Lightning"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ford_F-150_Lightning); [CNBC, "Ford plans to restart production of the electric F-150 Lightning on March 13," 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2023/03/02/ford-f-150-lightning-production-restart.html).

**MAR 2, 2022 — "Splitting the Company in Three"**
- **Situation:** Investors and analysts couldn't tell how profitable (or unprofitable) Ford's EV business actually was because it was buried inside the same P&L as the highly profitable combustion business. Farley needed a structure that exposed the EV unit's true economics, both to discipline internal spending and to let Wall Street value the EV business the way it valued Tesla.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Keep a single unified P&L but improve internal reporting/disclosure; spin off the EV business entirely as a separate public company (as some investors and analysts publicly suggested, similar to GM's Cruise structure); maintain the existing regional organizational structure (Ford North America, Ford Europe, etc.) rather than reorganizing by powertrain/business model.
- **What actually happened:** On March 2, 2022, Ford announced the creation of three distinct business units: Ford Blue (combustion and hybrid vehicles), Ford Model e (EVs and the software/digital platforms underpinning the whole lineup), and Ford Pro (commercial vehicles and services), each with separate financial reporting. Farley personally took the title of President of Ford Model e in addition to CEO.
- **Outcome:** The new segment reporting subsequently revealed (through 2022–2024 earnings) that Ford Model e was losing several billion dollars a year (cumulative EV losses became a major recurring disclosure through 2023–2024), while Ford Pro and Ford Blue remained strongly profitable — vindicating the decision to expose the numbers, even though the numbers themselves were politically uncomfortable.
- **Source:** [Tux Reports / Ford 8-K coverage, "Ford Blue and Ford Model e Businesses Announced," March 2022](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000037996/000003799622000015/march2exhibit99.htm); [Autoblog, "Ford's reorganization means specialization everywhere"](https://www.autoblog.com/carbuying/ford-reorganization).

**SEP–OCT 2023 — "The UAW Standoff"**
- **Situation:** The UAW's national contract with Ford, GM, and Stellantis expired at midnight September 14, 2023. The union, under new president Shawn Fain, demanded a 36% wage increase over four years among other gains, citing record automaker profits and prior concessions workers had made during the 2009 crisis. Ford had to weigh labor cost increases against the capital it needed for the EV transition.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Hold a hard line on cost given EV investment needs and risk a prolonged strike; offer an early, generous settlement to avoid production losses and reputational damage; seek a multi-tier agreement that protects EV-plant labor costs differently from legacy-plant costs.
- **What actually happened:** The UAW struck Ford, GM, and Stellantis simultaneously starting Sept 15, 2023 in a novel "stand-up strike" targeting specific plants, then escalated by expanding strikes against Ford and GM (sparing Stellantis, citing bargaining progress) on Sept 29, 2023. Farley and Fain publicly traded barbs — Farley called the union's expansion "grossly irresponsible" given what he characterized as Ford's already-record offer. A tentative agreement was reached October 25, 2023, including a 25% base wage increase over the contract term (part of what both sides described as a "record" deal), ending the nearly six-week strike.
- **Outcome:** After ratification, Farley said the new contract added "significant cost" to Ford's structure and that the company would need to find offsetting productivity and efficiency gains to remain competitive — directly linking the labor settlement to the EV-affordability problem that dominated 2024.
- **Quote:** Jim Farley, on the UAW's strike expansion: called it "grossly irresponsible," and said "if the UAW's goal is a record contract, they have already achieved this." Post-ratification: the deal added "significant cost."
- **Source:** [Detroit News, "UAW, Ford announce tentative agreement," Oct 25, 2023](https://www.detroitnews.com/story/business/autos/2023/10/25/ford-uaw-set-to-announce-tentative-agreement-with-25-percent-raise/71320883007/); [BBC, "UAW strike: Ford and union agree record pay rise in tentative deal"](https://feeds.bbci.co.uk/news/business-67224476); [AOL/wire, "As UAW officially ratifies Detroit 3 contract, Ford CEO says deal adds 'significant cost'"](https://www.aol.com/news/uaw-officially-ratifies-detroit-3-164013363.html); [Autoblog, "Farley reacts as UAW expands strike against Ford, GM"](https://www.autoblog.com/2023/09/29/uaw-set-to-expand-strike-at-ford-gm-assembly-plants/).

**AUG 21, 2024 — "Retreat from the Three-Row EV"**
- **Situation:** Ford's EV unit (Model e) was losing billions annually as EV demand growth slowed across the industry in 2023–2024. A planned three-row electric SUV could not, per Ford's own profitability discipline (no launch without a path to profitability within the first 12 months), pencil out given battery and manufacturing cost structure at the time.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Launch the three-row EV anyway at a loss to maintain market presence and EV credibility; delay the SUV by a year or two while re-engineering costs down; cancel the EV three-row entirely and substitute a hybrid three-row instead (which is what happened).
- **What actually happened:** On August 21, 2024, Ford announced it was cancelling the three-row electric SUV in favor of three-row hybrid models, delaying the next-generation electric pickup truck and its associated new manufacturing plant (Tennessee "BlueOval City" battery/assembly complex), while still expecting to start battery cell production at the Tennessee site in 2025. Ford disclosed a roughly $400 million non-cash special charge for the write-down of product-specific manufacturing assets tied to the change, with the possibility of up to a further $1.5 billion in charges in subsequent quarters.
- **Quote:** Ford CFO John Lawler: the cost structure of a three-row electric SUV could not meet Ford's requirement of profitability within the first 12 months of launch.
- **Source:** [CNBC, "Ford delays new EV plant, cancels electric three-row SUV as it shifts strategy," Aug 21, 2024](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/08/21/ford-delays-new-ev-plant-cancels-electric-three-row-suv.html); [NBC News, same story](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/autos/ford-delays-new-ev-plant-cancels-electric-three-row-suv-shifts-strateg-rcna167541); [Globe and Mail, "Ford slows its EV plans, delaying pickup and axing three-row"](https://www.theglobeandmail.com/drive/article-ford-changes-its-ev-plans-to-delay-next-generation-truck-axe-three-row/).

**FEB 2024 — "The Skunkworks Bet on a $30,000 EV"**
- **Situation:** Having concluded that scaling up existing-architecture EVs (Mach-E, Lightning) the same way legacy automakers build combustion cars was structurally too expensive to compete with Tesla and Chinese EV makers on cost, Ford needed a fundamentally different, lower-cost EV engineering and manufacturing approach — essentially admitting its first-generation EV strategy needed a clean-sheet redo.
- **CEO at the time:** Jim Farley.
- **Alternatives on the table:** Continue iterating on the existing Mach-E/Lightning platforms to reduce costs incrementally; license or partner with a Chinese or other low-cost EV platform provider; exit the affordable-EV segment entirely and concentrate on premium/commercial EVs only where margins were less brutal.
- **What actually happened:** Ford revealed (reported February 6–7, 2024) that it had quietly built a small, secretive "skunkworks" engineering team — about two years old by then, based in Irvine, California — tasked with designing a new, low-cost EV from scratch alongside an entirely new manufacturing system. The team is led by Alan Clarke, a former Tesla Director of New Program Engineering, alongside Anil Paryani (founder of EV-power startup Auto Motive Power, acquired by Ford in November 2023, and a former multi-year Tesla colleague of Clarke's).
- **Outcome:** The effort produced the "Ford Universal EV Platform" and "Universal EV Production System," whose first product — a midsize, four-door electric pickup to be built at Ford's Louisville Assembly Plant — was targeted for a 2027 launch, with Ford disclosing roughly $5 billion committed to the new platform, assembly process, and midsize truck program.
- **Source:** [TechCrunch, "Ex-Tesla exec leading Ford skunkworks project to develop low-cost EV," Feb 6, 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2024/02/06/ex-tesla-exec-leading-ford-skunkworks-project-to-develop-low-cost-ev/); [Tech Times, same story](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/301416/20240207/former-tesla-exec-leads-ford-skunkworks-project-develop-affordable-ev.htm); [autoevolution, "Everything We Know About Ford's $30,000 Electric Pickup and the Universal EV Platform"](https://www.autoevolution.com/news/everything-we-know-about-ford-s-30000-electric-pickup-and-the-universal-ev-platform-266017.html); [Barchart, "Ford's $5B Bet on America"](https://www.barchart.com/story/news/34050032/fords-5b-bet-on-america-innovation-meets-efficiency-in-new-ev-platform-assembly-process-and-midsize-truck).
