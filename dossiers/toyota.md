## 1. Company Snapshot — Candidate KPI Dimensions

Toyota's central strategic tension across its modern history is: **how do you stay the world's most disciplined, highest-volume, highest-trust manufacturer while a) a self-inflicted quality/trust crisis threatens that trust, and b) the entire industry's technology base shifts toward EVs/software, an area where Toyota has deliberately hedged rather than sprinted?** Three KPI dimensions that capture this:

1. **Trust / Quality (Brand Trust)** — Toyota's entire identity is "quality, durability, reliability." This KPI tracks public/regulatory trust, recall exposure, and the reputational account that gets spent during the 2009-2011 recall crisis and the 2023-2024 certification scandals, and rebuilt through quality reforms and sales records.
2. **Production & Supply Resilience** — Toyota pioneered just-in-time/lean manufacturing (Toyota Production System), and that same lean system proved fragile to the 2011 earthquake/tsunami and the 2020s chip shortage. This KPI tracks manufacturing output, supply-chain robustness, and global #1-by-volume status.
3. **Future Readiness (Powertrain Bet)** — Toyota's multi-decade hybrid bet (Prius, 1997) and hydrogen bet (Mirai, 2014) versus the global pivot to battery EVs led by Tesla/BYD/Chinese rivals. This KPI tracks Toyota's competitiveness in the electrification/software race versus its profitable, conservative hybrid-centric core.

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "The Reckoning" (2009–2013)
Akio Toyoda becomes president in June 2009 at the worst possible moment: Toyota has just posted its first operating loss in nearly six decades amid the global financial crisis, having overtaken GM as the world's largest automaker only months earlier on the back of a breakneck "Global 30" expansion strategy that critics say outran Toyota's own quality culture. Within his first year, Toyota is engulfed in the unintended-acceleration recall crisis — ultimately covering roughly 10 million vehicles worldwide — culminating in Toyoda's own contrite congressional testimony in February 2010. The era's central tension: can the founder's great-grandson re-anchor a company that grew too fast around its founding obsession with quality, while Wall Street, Washington, and the press treat every subsequent stumble (including the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake's supply-chain shock) as proof Toyota's invincibility was a myth?

### Era B — "Hybrid Conviction, Hydrogen Bet" (2014–2020)
Having stabilized after the recall crisis and the 2011 earthquake, Toyota leans into the two bets that define its philosophy: doubling down on hybrid technology it pioneered with the Prius, and pushing a genuinely contrarian bet on hydrogen fuel cells with the Mirai (launched late 2014). Meanwhile Tesla, BYD and Chinese EV makers begin building the case that pure battery-electric vehicles are the inevitable future. Toyota's leadership — convinced that hybrids and hydrogen are the more pragmatic, infrastructure-realistic path to decarbonization — resists going all-in on BEVs, drawing increasing criticism that the industry's most profitable, most disciplined manufacturer is "behind." The era's tension: profitable conviction versus the risk of mistiming the single biggest technology transition in automotive history.

### Era C — "The EV Reckoning and the Succession" (2021–2023)
Pressure peaks. Toyota unveils a $35 billion, 30-model BEV electrification plan in December 2021 even as Akio Toyoda publicly argues EVs are "overhyped" and the industry needs a "multi-pathway" approach. The chip shortage tests (and partly validates) Toyota's supply-chain reforms. The bZ4X — Toyota's first dedicated global BEV — launches in 2022 only to be recalled within weeks for a literal wheel-detachment risk, a gift to critics. In January 2023, Akio Toyoda steps down as President/CEO (effective April 2023), handing day-to-day control to Koji Sato while becoming Chairman — a deliberate generational and strategic reset. The tension: managing a leadership transition and an EV-strategy reversal at the same time, in full public view.

### Era D — "Scandal and the Empire's Scale" (2023–2026)
Even as Toyota notches record global sales (#1 worldwide for a fifth consecutive year in 2024), a wave of certification and safety-test falsification scandals rips through the Toyota Group: Daihatsu admits to 30+ years of rigged safety tests (Dec 2023), Hino Motors is found to have falsified emissions/fuel economy data back to 2003 (2022, with a $1.6B US settlement reached in 2025), and Toyota Industries admits forklift-engine emissions cheating (Jan 2024). Meanwhile Koji Sato and Chairman Toyoda recalibrate the EV strategy again — trimming 2026 BEV targets while doubling down on hybrids and teasing solid-state battery breakthroughs for 2027-2028. The tension: can the group police its own manufacturing integrity across a sprawling, decentralized empire of subsidiaries while still being trusted as the engineering gold standard — and can it commercialize next-gen battery tech before the EV market consolidates around rivals?

## 3. Decision Points

### ERA A — The Reckoning (2009–2013)

---

**JAN 2009 — "The Crown Nobody Wanted"**
Situation: Toyota's 2008 global sales of 8.97 million vehicles edge out GM's 8.36 million, making it the world's #1 automaker for the first time in 77 years — but the announcement lands during the deepest global auto downturn since the Depression, and Toyota's own sales fell 4% (its first decline in a decade). The "Global 30" expansion plan (targeting 15% global market share, up from 11% in 2005) that won the crown is increasingly blamed for diluting Toyota's quality culture.
Top job: Katsuaki Watanabe, President (until June 2009).
Alternative paths: (a) celebrate the milestone and continue expansion momentum; (b) quietly slow expansion and reinvest in quality systems; (c) treat the crown as incidental and refocus messaging entirely on cost control given the crisis.
What actually happened: Toyota publicly downplayed the achievement given the economic backdrop and the following month posted a forecast for its first operating loss in 71 years. Watanabe announced he would step down; the board moved toward installing a Toyoda family member for the first time since 1995.
Quote: "Toyota is suffering from trying to get too big, too fast" — Paul Ingrassia, Pulitzer Prize-winning auto journalist, quoted in retrospective coverage of the period. [Washington Times]
Sources: https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2009/01/21/AR2009012101216.html ; https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2010/feb/10/toyotas-bumpy-ride-began-with-race-for-growth/

**JUN 2009 — "The Family Returns"**
Situation: Akio Toyoda, 53, great-grandson of founder Kiichiro Toyoda, is named President, the first family member to lead the company since 1995. He inherits a company that just posted an $8.6 billion net loss for fiscal year 2009 — its first loss in nearly six decades — after the financial crisis forced it to idle factories built during a decade of breakneck expansion. Toyota was worth $115 billion at the time.
Top job: Katsuaki Watanabe → Akio Toyoda (effective June 2009).
Alternative paths: (a) bring in an outside professional manager to signal a clean break from the expansion era; (b) promote from the existing non-family executive bench; (c) install a Toyoda family member as a symbolic "return to founding values."
What actually happened: The board chose Akio Toyoda. He pledged to steer the company "back to the basics" of building cars that made customers happy and to bring greater transparency to Toyota's sprawling culture.
Quote: Toyoda pledged to take the company "back to the basics" of building cars that made people happy. [CNN Money / Fortune interview, June 2009]
Sources: https://money.cnn.com/2009/06/23/autos/akio_toyoda_toyota_new_president.fortune/index.htm ; https://www.autonews.com/article/20090120/GLOBAL02/301209682/akio-toyoda-named-toyota-president-amid-global-crisis/

**OCT 2009 — "3.8 Million Floor Mats"**
Situation: Following the fatal August 28, 2009 crash of a Lexus ES350 near San Diego that killed CHP officer Mark Saylor and three family members (the 911 call captured the car accelerating uncontrollably to over 100 mph), Toyota traces the cause to floor mats trapping accelerator pedals and recalls 3.8 million vehicles on October 5, 2009 — the largest recall in company history to that point. Internally, Toyota also has unresolved information about a separate, mechanical "sticky pedal" defect.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) recall broadly and immediately for all plausible causes, including the not-yet-confirmed sticky-pedal mechanism; (b) recall narrowly for the confirmed floor-mat cause only, citing insufficient evidence on other causes; (c) resist a recall and characterize incidents as driver error pending further study.
What actually happened: Toyota recalled narrowly for floor mats only. The Department of Justice later concluded Toyota knew weeks before its public statements that other vehicles had similar pedal entrapment risk and that some accelerators could stick in a partially depressed position — and that Toyota's public assurance that floor mats were the "root cause" was misleading. This became the central allegation in the 2014 $1.2B DOJ settlement.
Sources: https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-criminal-charge-against-toyota-motor-corporation-and-deferred ; https://www.autosafety.org/major-recalls-toyota-sudden-acceleration/

**JAN 2010 — "The Sticky Pedal Recall"**
Situation: On January 16, 2010, Toyota informs NHTSA that CTS-brand accelerator pedals have a "sticking" defect — pedals can become harder to depress, slower to return, or stuck in a partially depressed position. Toyota recalls 2.3 million vehicles on January 21, then under NHTSA pressure expands the recall by another 1.1 million vehicles on January 27. Toyota also takes the extraordinary step of briefly halting sales and production of eight models in the US.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) recall and halt sales/production immediately and broadly across all affected models; (b) recall but continue sales/production while a fix is engineered; (c) push back on NHTSA's demand for a wider recall.
What actually happened: Toyota recalled the additional 1.1 million vehicles and, unusually, suspended sales of new vehicles using the CTS pedal as well as halting production at five North American plants for about a week — a step rarely taken by any automaker for a single supplier defect.
Sources: https://www.autosafety.org/major-recalls-toyota-sudden-acceleration/ ; https://www.transportation.gov/testimony/toyota%E2%80%99s-recalls-and-government%E2%80%99s-response

**FEB 24, 2010 — "Toyoda Goes to Washington"**
Situation: The House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform holds a hearing titled "Toyota Gas Pedals: Is the Public at Risk?" Toyota has by now recalled roughly 8.5 million vehicles worldwide for the floor-mat/sticky-pedal issues, with allegations the defects were linked to as many as 89 deaths. Akio Toyoda personally travels to testify — an unusual move for a Japanese company president, and one his US team reportedly debated at length.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) send Toyota's North American CEO/US executives to testify instead of the president personally; (b) have Toyoda testify but stick to narrow technical/legal points via counsel; (c) have Toyoda testify personally, apologize directly, and accept responsibility even while defending Toyota's engineering.
What actually happened: Toyoda testified personally and apologized repeatedly. He stated: "We pursued growth over the speed at which we are able to develop our people and our organization, and I am deeply sorry for any accidents that Toyota drivers have experienced." He also told the panel he was "absolutely confident" there was no electronic defect, maintaining the company's position that sudden acceleration stemmed from floor mats or sticking pedals. Lawmakers were unmoved by the apology and continued to press him; the hearing was broadly seen as bruising for Toyota's image despite the contrition.
Quote: "We pursued growth over the speed at which we are able to develop our people and our organization, and I am deeply sorry for any accidents that Toyota drivers have experienced." — Akio Toyoda, testimony to House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform, Feb 24, 2010.
Sources: https://www.industryweek.com/the-economy/public-policy/article/21937531/akio-toyodas-testimony-to-u-s-congress ; https://www.npr.org/2010/02/24/124052202/toyota-chief-apologizes-for-recalls ; https://oversightdemocrats.house.gov/committee-activity/hearings/full-committee-hearing-toyota-gas-pedals-is-the-public-at-riskd

**MAR 2010 — "Building a Quality Czar Structure"**
Situation: With the recall crisis still raging and Toyota facing accusations that its sprawling global structure let safety concerns get lost between regions and Japan headquarters, Toyota convenes the first meeting of a new Special Committee for Global Quality, chaired personally by Akio Toyoda.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) centralize all quality decisions in Toyota City, Japan; (b) create regional Chief Quality Officer (CQO) positions empowered to escalate concerns directly, without going through the existing regional sales hierarchy; (c) outsource quality auditing to a third party for credibility.
What actually happened: Toyota created CQO positions for each major region (North America, Europe, Asia/Oceania, China, etc.), each sitting on the new global quality committee, plus "customer first" training centers and a faster information-escalation process. This became the template for Toyota's post-crisis quality governance.
Sources: https://pressroom.toyota.com/toyota-motor-corporation-president-akio-toyoda-announces-global-quality-task-force/ ; https://www.toyota-global.com/company/history_of_toyota/75years/text/leaping_forward_as_a_global_corporation/chapter5/section3/item2_a.html

**FEB 2011 — "The Exoneration Nobody Believed"**
Situation: After a 10-month joint investigation, NASA engineers and NHTSA conclude there is no electronic-based cause of unintended high-speed acceleration in Toyota vehicles; the only confirmed causes remain the mechanical floor-mat and sticky-pedal defects already recalled. Transportation Secretary Ray LaHood states publicly that the study found no electronic flaw. The finding vindicates Toyota's central technical defense — but plaintiffs' lawyers and some independent researchers contest the report's independence, given Toyota's own outside expert (Exponent) was involved in aspects of the investigation.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) use the exoneration aggressively in PR and litigation to rehabilitate Toyota's image; (b) accept the finding quietly without triumphalism, given ongoing litigation and the (separate, already-admitted) regulatory disclosure failures; (c) push for further independent review to remove any doubt.
What actually happened: Toyota welcomed the finding publicly but the controversy did not fully die — Toyota still faced thousands of lawsuits and, three years later, a $1.2B federal settlement over how it handled disclosure (not over whether electronics caused the defect). NHTSA's framing also shifted blame partly toward "pedal misapplication" by drivers for many of the reported incidents.
Sources: https://www.transportation.gov/briefing-room/us-department-transportation-releases-results-nhtsa-nasa-study-unintended-acceleration ; https://www.greencarcongress.com/2011/02/ua-20110209.html

**MAR 2011 — "The Earthquake Breaks the Machine"**
Situation: The March 11, 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami devastate northeastern Japan, wiping out tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers of critical components (especially semiconductors and specialty parts) that Toyota's famously lean, just-in-time production system depended on. Toyota's global output falls 78% year-over-year in April 2011; the company halts operations at all 12 of its main domestic assembly plants, and the disaster ultimately costs roughly 150,000+ units of lost production in the immediate aftermath.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) maintain the pure just-in-time model and absorb the disruption as a one-off; (b) begin building strategic safety-stock inventory and multi-sourcing critical components even at higher carrying cost; (c) vertically integrate critical chip/component production to reduce future exposure.
What actually happened: Toyota began a multi-year shift toward "multi-sourcing" for critical parts and started carrying higher safety-stock inventories of key components, a change credited a decade later with helping Toyota weather the 2021 chip shortage better than rivals.
Sources: https://www.supplychaindive.com/news/toyota-semiconductor-shortage-earthquake-inventory-ihs-gartner-forecast-2022/600193/ ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0925527314002278

**APR 2010 — "Selling the Cathedral"** *(crossover scenario with Tesla dossier — Toyota's side of the same decision)*
Situation: Toyota's 70%-owned NUMMI joint venture with GM in Fremont, California — a 5.5 million sq ft plant built for 500,000+ cars/year — is being abandoned by GM (which announced its withdrawal in June 2009 amid its own bankruptcy). Toyota must decide what to do with its half of a plant that once employed thousands of UAW workers, just as it is fighting recall fires at home.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) keep operating NUMMI alone, absorbing GM's exit; (b) close the plant entirely and write off the investment; (c) sell or lease the facility to a third party, including the option of a small EV startup (Tesla) that approached Toyota directly.
What actually happened: Toyota closed NUMMI on March 31, 2010, idling roughly 4,700 UAW jobs — but the same year, in May 2010, Toyota invested $50M in Tesla and sold the Fremont plant to Tesla for $42M, a deal Akio Toyoda personally championed after an enthusiastic test of the Tesla Roadster.
Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NUMMI ; https://money.cnn.com/2010/04/01/news/companies/Toyota_GM_joint_venture_closing/index.htm

**MAR 2014 — "Paying the Price for Silence"**
Situation: The DOJ's multi-year investigation into Toyota's recall conduct concludes. Prosecutors find that in fall 2009 Toyota told consumers and NHTSA it had identified and addressed the "root cause" of unintended acceleration (floor mats) while internally aware that other vehicles, with different pedal designs, posed similar risk from a separate sticky-pedal mechanism it had not yet disclosed.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) fight the wire fraud charge in court; (b) settle and admit the misleading conduct in a deferred prosecution agreement; (c) settle without admissions, paying a fine but disputing the characterization.
What actually happened: On March 19, 2014, Toyota agreed to a $1.2 billion payment and a deferred prosecution agreement, admitting it had "misled U.S. consumers by concealing and making deceptive statements about two safety-related issues affecting its vehicles" — at the time the largest criminal penalty ever imposed on an automaker in the US.
Sources: https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-criminal-charge-against-toyota-motor-corporation-and-deferred ; https://edition.cnn.com/2014/03/19/us/toyota-settlement-justice-department

### ERA B — Hybrid Conviction, Hydrogen Bet (2014–2020)

---

**NOV 2014 — "Betting on Hydrogen, Not Just Batteries"**
Situation: Toyota unveils the Mirai — a hydrogen fuel-cell sedan — at the Los Angeles Auto Show, with Japan sales starting December 2014 at ¥6.7 million (~$57,400) and US sales beginning October 2015. Toyota commits to building only 700 units globally in the first year, a deliberately small, expensive halo project, even as Tesla's Model S is proving battery-electric vehicles can be desirable, profitable-trending products. Toyota frames hydrogen — built on three decades of in-house fuel-cell R&D dating to the early 1990s — as a complementary path to a "hydrogen society," not just a car technology.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) commit fully to battery-electric development instead, given Tesla's market validation; (b) launch hydrogen as a low-volume halo/R&D project while still scaling hybrids as the mass-market core; (c) pursue both hydrogen and BEVs aggressively and simultaneously at scale.
What actually happened: Toyota chose (b) — Mirai launched as a small-volume technology showcase while Prius/hybrid technology remained the actual sales volume driver, and full BEV investment stayed comparatively modest through the rest of the decade.
Sources: https://global.toyota/en/newsroom/toyota/22740159.html ; https://www.fleetnews.co.uk/news/2014/11/19/toyota-to-launch-mirai-hydrogen-fuel-cell-car/

**2015-2016 — "The Disruption the Rivals Saw Coming"** *(context scenario — useful as a comparison beat to Mercedes' 2015 "Disruption Memo")*
Situation: Tesla's Model S and Model X are reframing what an EV can be: fast, desirable, software-updatable. Rivals including Daimler and BMW begin announcing dedicated EV sub-brands and multi-billion-euro electrification plans (Daimler's "CASE" strategy and EQ brand, announced 2016). Toyota, by contrast, continues prioritizing hybrid volume (the Prius has sold millions of units) and hydrogen R&D, without announcing a comparable flagship BEV program.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) match rivals with an immediate, large-scale BEV sub-brand announcement; (b) continue the hybrid+hydrogen path and wait for BEV battery economics/charging infrastructure to mature; (c) accelerate BEV R&D quietly without a public splashy announcement.
What actually happened: Toyota largely pursued (b)/(c) — continuing hybrid dominance and incremental BEV R&D (e.g., later partnerships with Panasonic on batteries, and small-scale BEV trials in China) without matching the scale of German rivals' public EV pledges until 2019-2021.
Note: This is a framing/context point rather than a single dated decision; treat as background, not a hard "hist:" citation point. **Partially unverifiable as a discrete decision date** — use only as connective narrative, not as a scenario with a specific real quote.

**2017 — "China Demands an EV, Right Now"**
Situation: China's New Energy Vehicle (NEV) credit mandate forces all automakers selling in China to produce or source a quota of plug-in/EV credits or pay penalties, creating sudden pressure on Toyota — whose China lineup is hybrid-heavy — to either build compliant BEVs or partner for credits.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) rapidly localize a dedicated BEV for China, even as a low-margin compliance product; (b) partner with a Chinese automaker/battery maker to source NEV credits or co-develop a compliance EV; (c) lean harder into plug-in hybrids, which still partially qualify under some local rules, to delay full BEV commitment.
What actually happened: Toyota pursued partnerships (including with BYD and other Chinese players in subsequent years for battery and EV co-development) and accelerated localized BEV variants for China through the late 2010s, while continuing hybrid-first strategy elsewhere.

**SEP 2017 — "The Solid-State Promise"**
Situation: Toyota announces it is developing solid-state batteries — promising faster charging and greater range/safety than conventional lithium-ion — and targets commercialization in the early 2020s, a promise that recurs (and slips) for years afterward.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) announce an aggressive near-term solid-state commercialization timeline to reassure markets Toyota isn't ceding the EV future; (b) stay quiet about R&D timelines and let products speak when ready; (c) partner with an established battery maker rather than developing proprietary solid-state tech in-house.
What actually happened: Toyota made the announcement, generating significant press attention about a "game-changing" battery — but the commercialization date slipped repeatedly over the following years (later guidance: 2027-2028, per a 2023-2024 update with partner Idemitsu).

**2019-2020 — "COVID Tests Genchi Genbutsu"**
Situation: The COVID-19 pandemic disrupts global supply chains and forces remote work, directly challenging one of Toyota's foundational principles — genchi genbutsu ("go and see for yourself") — which assumes managers can physically visit the production floor or supplier site to solve problems. Toyota's profits decline sharply in this period.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) insist on maintaining genchi genbutsu in modified, distanced form; (b) openly question and adapt the principle for a remote-first crisis; (c) suspend the principle temporarily and revert once travel resumes.
What actually happened: Toyoda publicly mused that the pandemic was forcing a "fresh look at the assumptions of genchi genbutsu," a notable moment of a Toyota leader questioning a sacred Toyota Way tenet in public.
Quote: "We're taking a fresh look at the assumptions of genchi genbutsu" — Akio Toyoda, FY2020 financial results press conference, May 2020.
Sources: https://www.autonews.com/automakers-suppliers/lockdown-leads-toyota-chief-question-core-tenet ; https://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2020/05/13/business/corporate-business/coronavirus-akio-toyoda-toyota-japan/

### ERA C — The EV Reckoning and the Succession (2021–2023)

---

**AUG 2021 — "The Chip Shortage Test"**
Situation: The global semiconductor shortage, worsened by COVID-related factory shutdowns in Southeast Asia, forces Toyota — which had built a safety-stock buffer after the 2011 earthquake — to nonetheless cut global production by as much as 40% in some months (September 2021: planned ~900,000 units cut to ~540,000). Toyota's earlier resilience investments delayed but did not prevent significant pain.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) protect the safety stock and prioritize high-margin/high-demand models with available chips; (b) draw down all available inventory immediately to maximize near-term output regardless of model mix; (c) renegotiate aggressively with chip suppliers for guaranteed allocation at higher cost.
What actually happened: Toyota drew on its safety stock to outlast peers initially (faring comparatively better than GM/Ford in the shortage's first year), but by autumn 2021 was still forced into major production cuts as the shortage deepened and outlasted its buffer.
Sources: https://www.fleetnews.co.uk/news/manufacturer-news/2021/08/19/chip-shortage-forces-toyota-to-cut-global-production ; https://www.supplychaindive.com/news/toyota-semiconductor-shortage-earthquake-inventory-ihs-gartner-forecast-2022/600193/

---

**DEC 14, 2021 — "The $35 Billion Pivot"**
Situation: Akio Toyoda holds a major press conference unveiling Toyota's electrification plan: 30 new battery-electric vehicle (BEV) models by 2030, a target of 3.5 million annual BEV sales by 2030, and roughly $35 billion ($70B combined BEV+hybrid/electrified) in planned investment — a dramatically larger public BEV commitment than anything Toyota had previously announced, displayed with 16 EV/Lexus concept vehicles on stage.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) make a symbolic, modest BEV announcement and continue prioritizing hybrids; (b) announce a sweeping multi-billion-dollar BEV commitment to answer "behind on EVs" critics directly; (c) avoid a single big-bang announcement and let incremental product launches speak for themselves.
What actually happened: Toyota chose the sweeping announcement (b), staged with the 16-concept reveal, explicitly to counter the growing media narrative that Toyota was lagging in the EV race — while Toyoda simultaneously continued to defend hybrids and a "multi-pathway" approach in the same period.
Sources: https://www.kbb.com/car-news/surprise-toyota-lexus-show-off-16-ev-concepts ; https://pressroom.toyota.com/akio-toyoda-shares-toyotas-strategy-for-achieving-carbon-neutrality-through-battery-electric-vehicles/

**2022 — "Overhyped"**
Situation: As rivals race toward EV-only futures and press coverage intensifies around Toyota's "laggard" reputation (having launched only one BEV, the bZ4X, by 2023), Akio Toyoda pushes back publicly and repeatedly, arguing the auto industry's all-in EV consensus is mistaken and that internal combustion engines, hybrids, and hydrogen will all remain relevant.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President (until April 2023).
Alternative paths: (a) publicly defend the multi-pathway position despite criticism, accepting reputational cost; (b) soften public rhetoric while privately accelerating BEV investment; (c) abandon the multi-pathway message entirely and align public statements with the December 2021 BEV pledge.
What actually happened: Toyoda continued to defend the multi-pathway approach forcefully and personally, framing himself as an early, lonely voice that was later vindicated when Japan's government endorsed "multi-pathway" language at the 2023 G7 summit.
Quote: "All they say is that Toyota lags behind on BEVs." ... "For the three years before that, I was the only person in the auto industry to propose [multi-pathway decarbonization], and I took a lot of flak." — Akio Toyoda, reported in automotive press, 2022-2023.
Quote: Toyoda separately stated EVs would likely "cap at 30% of market" with engines "surely" remaining relevant alongside them.
Sources: https://carbuzz.com/news/toyota-chairman-akio-toyoda-thinks-the-need-for-evs-is-grossly-overhyped/ ; https://insideevs.com/news/705724/akio-toyoda-toyota-ev-cap/ ; https://insideevs.com/news/762583/akio-toyota-evs-hybrids-emissions/

**JUN 2022 — "The Wheels Came Off"**
Situation: Toyota's first dedicated global BEV, the bZ4X (and its Subaru-badged twin, the Solterra), is recalled within weeks of launch after Toyota discovers that hub bolts on all four wheels could loosen with repeated hard cornering/braking, in the worst cases causing a wheel to detach entirely while driving — confirmed in at least two real-world incidents. Only 2,700 units had been built/sold globally at the time. Toyota initially has no fix and tells owners not to drive the cars at all.
Top job: Akio Toyoda, President.
Alternative paths: (a) issue a stop-sale/stop-drive order immediately and pause all bZ4X production until a permanent fix exists; (b) issue a more limited advisory and continue limited sales while investigating; (c) attempt a quick interim fix (e.g., torque/inspection bulletin) without halting sales.
What actually happened: Toyota chose the most conservative path — a global stop-drive recall and a production/sales pause — even though only ~2,700 vehicles existed. Toyota engineers found and validated a fix (new wheel hub bolts/hardware) by October 2022, after which bZ4X production and sales resumed in the US.

**JAN 26, 2023 — "Toyoda Hands Off the Wheel"**
Situation: After nearly 14 years as President during which Toyota survived the recall crisis, the 2011 earthquake, and weathered "EV laggard" criticism while still becoming the world's top-selling automaker, 66-year-old Akio Toyoda announces he will step down as President and CEO effective April 1, 2023. His successor is 53-year-old Koji Sato, then president of Lexus and Gazoo Racing (Toyota's motorsports arm) — notably not a member of the Toyoda family, and notably someone with a performance/motorsport and brand background rather than a pure engineering or finance background.
Top job: Akio Toyoda → Koji Sato (effective April 1, 2023); Toyoda becomes Chairman of the Board.
Alternative paths: (a) remain as President/CEO and continue personally steering the EV strategy; (b) hand the presidency to another long-tenured insider/engineer; (c) hand the presidency to a younger executive seen as more credible on EVs/software/younger-generation branding, while Toyoda retains influence as Chairman.
What actually happened: Toyota chose (c) — Sato took over day-to-day operations and the EV portfolio, while Toyoda moved to Chairman (a role from which he continued to speak publicly and influentially on strategy, including continuing to defend hybrids).
Quote: Coverage characterized the move as Toyoda stepping aside to "become chairman" while Sato "takes over daily management," with Toyoda's role continuing as an influential elder voice rather than a clean exit.

**APR 2023 — "Sato's First Move: Double the EV Bet"**
Situation: In his first press conference as President/CEO, Koji Sato unveils an accelerated EV plan: 10 new BEV models by 2026 and a target of 1.5 million annual BEV sales, while also announcing a new dedicated organizational unit ("BEV Factory," established May 2023) to oversee next-generation EV development separate from Toyota's traditional engineering structure.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO.
Alternative paths: (a) continue Toyoda's measured multi-pathway messaging with only incremental changes; (b) announce an accelerated, more EV-forward strategy to signal a generational shift, while still preserving hybrids as the profit engine; (c) make a hard, near-total pivot away from hybrids/hydrogen toward EVs to match Tesla/BYD/Chinese rivals directly.
What actually happened: Sato chose (b) — a notably more EV-forward tone and a dedicated "BEV Factory" organization, while Toyota's hybrid sales (in practice) continued to dominate volume and revenue. Targets were later trimmed (EV sales projections cut by roughly 40% in a subsequent revision) as actual BEV demand growth slowed industry-wide.

**MAR 2023 — "Hino's Reckoning Begins"**
Situation: Hino Motors, Toyota's commercial-truck subsidiary, had admitted as early as March 2022 to systematically falsifying engine emissions and fuel-economy certification data — eventually traced back to 2003, affecting more than 640,000 vehicles. By 2023 the scope is clear enough that Toyota and partner organizations take punitive action.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO, Toyota Motor Corp (Hino is a separately listed but Toyota-controlled subsidiary).
Alternative paths: (a) keep Hino fully inside Toyota's commercial vehicle alliance and manage the cleanup internally; (b) expel Hino from the Toyota-led commercial vehicle alliance/JV as a punitive, trust-restoring signal; (c) absorb Hino more tightly under direct Toyota control to enforce compliance.
What actually happened: Toyota and partners (including Hino's truck alliance partners) took the "most severe step" available, expelling Hino from the joint commercial-vehicle partnership framework in 2022 following the scandal's escalation — among the most punitive internal actions Toyota Group has taken against one of its own subsidiaries.

**DEC 20, 2023 — "Daihatsu Shuts Down"**
Situation: An independent third-party investigative committee finds that Daihatsu, Toyota's small-car subsidiary, falsified safety certification test data across 64 models over roughly 30 years (with the oldest documented case dating to 1989), including 174 newly identified irregularity cases beyond an initial set already disclosed earlier in 2023. 88,000 vehicles built in Thailand and Malaysia in 2022-2023 alone were directly implicated in falsified crash tests; 24 of the 64 affected models carried the Toyota brand.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO, Toyota Motor Corp; Daihatsu's own president resigned amid the scandal.
Alternative paths: (a) suspend only the specific models with confirmed falsified tests; (b) suspend ALL Daihatsu domestic and international shipments pending a full safety re-certification, even at major revenue cost; (c) restructure Daihatsu's management/governance immediately while continuing shipments under enhanced oversight.
What actually happened: Daihatsu suspended all domestic and international vehicle shipments and halted all factory production in Japan from late December 2023, resuming only partial domestic production in February 2024 after government-supervised re-testing.
Sources: https://www.cnn.com/2023/12/27/business/daihatsu-japan-production-halt-safety-tests-intl-hnk/index.html ; https://www.daihatsu.com/news/2023/20231220-4.html ; https://www.malaymail.com/news/money/2024/02/12/toyotas-daihatsu-resumes-part-of-domestic-production/117620

**JAN 29, 2024 — "The Forklifts, Too"**
Situation: Toyota Industries Corporation (TICO) — the Toyota Group company that, among other things, makes engines used in Toyota's own forklifts — is found by a special investigation committee to have cheated on emissions certification testing for multiple gasoline and diesel forklift engine models over more than a decade, including data substitution and unauthorized parts swaps during testing.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO, Toyota Motor Corp; Toyota Industries has its own separate president/board.
Alternative paths: (a) limit corrective action to the specific non-compliant engine models; (b) suspend all affected engine shipments pending recertification and accept the financial/legal exposure, including likely US class-action litigation; (c) attempt a negotiated remediation with regulators without halting shipments.
What actually happened: TICO suspended shipments of the affected engines and, in the US, ultimately settled a related class-action lawsuit for $299.5 million (per Toyota Industries' own 2024 disclosure), separate from the Hino and Daihatsu matters but part of the same broader pattern of Toyota Group certification failures surfacing in 2022-2024.

**2024 — "Record Sales, Record Scandal — At the Same Time"**
Situation: Despite (or in spite of) the Daihatsu, Hino, and Toyota Industries scandals all becoming public within roughly a 24-month window, Toyota and Lexus combined post global sales of just over 10.8 million vehicles for 2024, retaining the #1 global automaker title for a fifth consecutive year — even as total Toyota Group sales (including Daihatsu/Hino) fell 3.7% due specifically to a 13.8% domestic sales drop in Japan caused by the certification-driven production halts.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO.
Alternative paths: (a) lead with the scandal response and accept a "lost year" narrative in messaging; (b) lead public communications with the sales record to reassure markets, while continuing scandal remediation in parallel; (c) attempt to separate "Toyota brand" messaging entirely from "Toyota Group subsidiary" scandals in investor and public communications.
What actually happened: Toyota's public messaging emphasized the continued global #1 sales position and the growth of electrified (hybrid) sales — up 23.2% year-over-year to 4.53 million units, 40.8% of global sales — while the domestic, Group-wide scandal fallout was acknowledged primarily in the financial disclosures explaining the Japan sales decline.
Sources: https://motorillustrated.com/toyota-sells-10-8-million-vehicles-in-2024-retains-global-sales-lead/148005/ ; https://autotechinsight.spglobal.com/news/5280208/toyota-remains-worlds-top-automaker-in-2024-despite-sales-dip

**2024-2025 — "Betting Again on Solid-State"**
Situation: Toyota and battery/energy partner Idemitsu announce a formal cooperation to begin mass production of all-solid-state batteries for BEVs, with Toyota projecting (per its own roadmap) commercialization between 2027 and 2028, and a flagship claim of ~750 miles (1,200 km) of range with roughly 10-minute charging times — a technology bet that, if delivered on schedule, could leapfrog current lithium-ion EV competitors including Tesla and Chinese rivals.
Top job: Koji Sato, President/CEO; Akio Toyoda, Chairman, remains a vocal advocate of the multi-pathway/long-term technology approach that underlies this bet.
Alternative paths: (a) make the solid-state bet the centerpiece of Toyota's public EV narrative going forward, accepting the risk of another timeline slip after 2017's unmet promises; (b) downplay solid-state publicly and focus messaging on near-term, deliverable hybrid and conventional-battery BEV products; (c) abandon in-house solid-state development and license/partner for next-gen battery tech from an outside leader instead.
What actually happened: Toyota chose (a), publicly committing to the Idemitsu partnership and the 2027-2028 commercialization target as a signature long-term differentiator, while continuing to scale conventional hybrids as the near-term sales and profit engine.
