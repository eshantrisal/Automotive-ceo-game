# Research Dossier: Stellantis (and predecessor Fiat / Fiat Chrysler Automobiles)

Prepared for game-data writing purposes. All factual claims below are sourced; anywhere a quote or
figure could not be independently verified, that is flagged explicitly rather than invented.

---

## 1. Company Snapshot

**Working name in-game:** Fiat → Fiat Chrysler Automobiles (FCA) → Stellantis
**Logo/identity note:** four companies in one historical thread — Fiat (Turin), Chrysler (Detroit/Auburn Hills), FCA (the 2014 merger of the two), Stellantis (the 2021 merger of FCA and PSA/Peugeot).

**Candidate KPI dimensions (pick 3):**

1. **Cash / Balance Sheet Survival** — Fiat in 2004 and Chrysler in 2009 were both flirting with insolvency; this is the existential axis for the earliest era, parallel to Tesla's "Cash Runway."
2. **Margin / Profitability Discipline** — the central Tavares-era obsession (operating margin, "value over volume"); analogous to Mercedes' "EBIT Margin." Stellantis under Tavares hit ~13% adjusted operating income margin in 2023, roughly double VW/Renault — then collapsed toward a €22.3B net loss in 2025.
3. **Brand Health / Dealer-Customer Trust** — distinct from Mercedes' "Prestige" in that this is about a multi-brand portfolio (Jeep, Ram, Chrysler, Dodge, Fiat, Peugeot, Citroën, Alfa Romeo, Maserati) and dealer-network goodwill, which swung violently from 2023-2026 amid inventory/pricing disputes and quality complaints.
4. **Industrial Scale / Merger Logic** — could substitute for "Production" — Marchionne's entire strategic worldview was that auto manufacturers needed scale through M&A to survive; could track how many merger doors are open vs. closed.

Recommended pick for a tight 3-KPI design: **Cash/Survival** (eras 1-2 only, fading in relevance after 2014) collapses naturally into **Margin** once FCA stabilizes — so the cleanest 3 for the full arc are likely **(1) Margin/Profitability, (2) Brand & Dealer Trust, (3) Industrial Scale/Deal-Making appetite** — with an early-era variant or "deep" sub-track for raw cash survival in the 2004-2009 Fiat/Chrysler crisis years (mirroring how Tesla's era A is "deep" and Mercedes era B is "deep").

## 2. Eras

### Era A — "The Turnaround and the Bargain" (2004–2009)
Fiat's near-death experience and Marchionne's installation as CEO, ending with the audacious, no-cash acquisition of a stake in bankrupt Chrysler. The central tension: an outsider CEO must first save a 100-year-old Italian industrial icon from its own bloat and near-bankruptcy, then — improbably — turn around and rescue an even bigger broken American carmaker, for nothing down, while the U.S. government and UAW watch warily.

### Era B — "Building Fiat Chrysler" (2009–2014)
Marchionne runs Fiat and Chrysler simultaneously as separate public/legal entities while steadily buying out the U.S. Treasury, Canada, and the UAW retiree health-care trust (VEBA) to merge them into one company, FCA, by 2014. The tension: integrating two cultures and balance sheets while keeping both alive, racing against thin margins, a fragile Europe market, and a ticking clock on government/VEBA stakes.

### Era C — "Scale or Die: The Merger Crusade" (2014–2019)
With FCA formed, Marchionne pivots to his real obsession: the auto industry is structurally unprofitable because of duplicated capital spending, and only mega-mergers can fix it. He spins off Ferrari for cash, publicly courts GM and gets rebuffed, keeps pushing consolidation rhetoric for years, then dies suddenly in mid-2018. His successor Mike Manley inherits an unfinished crusade and, within a year, gets Fiat Chrysler into merger talks with Renault — which collapse spectacularly — before pivoting to PSA/Peugeot, announced in late 2019.

### Era D — "Stellantis: Margins, Mutiny, and a New CEO" (2020–2026)
The FCA-PSA merger completes amid COVID in January 2021, creating Stellantis under chairman John Elkann and CEO Carlos Tavares (the former PSA chief). Tavares drives the most aggressive margin-first strategy in the industry, hits historic profitability, but a UAW strike, a North American pricing/inventory revolt by dealers, falling U.S. market share, and board conflict over succession end with his abrupt December 2024 resignation. Antonio Filosa, a 25-year company veteran, is named CEO in May 2025 (effective June 2025) and inherits a company swinging back from EV-first ambition to combustion-engine pragmatism amid record 2025 losses and U.S. tariffs.

## 3. Decision Points

### ERA A — The Turnaround and the Bargain (2004–2009)

**1. JUN 2004 — "The Fifth CEO in Two Years"**
Fiat Group is hemorrhaging money and credibility; the board has churned through CEOs, and the latest, Giuseppe Morchio, abruptly quit. The company is widely rumored to be heading toward break-up or a GM bailout-style rescue. Fiat's board, led by chairman Umberto Agnelli/later Luca Cordero di Montezemolo, turns to Sergio Marchionne — at the time CEO of Swiss inspection firm SGS and a Fiat board member — a relative outsider to the auto industry.
- **CEO at the time:** Giuseppe Morchio (outgoing); Sergio Marchionne (incoming, June 1, 2004).
- **Alternative paths on the table:** promote an internal Fiat lifer to preserve continuity; sell/break up Fiat Auto to a foreign OEM (GM had an existing put option/relationship from a 2000 alliance); bring in another outside turnaround specialist.
- **What actually happened:** Marchionne was named CEO June 1, 2004, becoming Fiat's fifth CEO in two years. He moved fast — flattening management layers, picking young executives, and pushing the company back to profitability within about a year.
- **Quote:** Unable to verify a specific contemporaneous quote from Marchionne himself about the appointment in the sources gathered; do not fabricate one.
- **Source:** [Automotive News – "Fiat names Marchionne new CEO"](https://www.autonews.com/article/20040601/REG/406010716/fiat-names-marchionne-new-ceo/); [NPR obituary](https://www.npr.org/2018/07/25/632219813/sergio-marchionne-who-guided-fiat-chryslers-dramatic-turnaround-dies)

**2. 2005 — "Exit GM, Cash in Hand"**
Fiat held a put option from a 2000 alliance with General Motors that could force GM to buy the rest of Fiat Auto. Marchionne aggressively used this leverage rather than letting the alliance drift.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** let the put option lapse and continue the troubled GM alliance; sue for a larger payout; restructure the alliance instead of exiting.
- **What happened:** Fiat exercised the put and GM paid roughly $2 billion to terminate the alliance and let Fiat keep full ownership of Fiat Auto — a critical cash infusion during the turnaround. (This is well-established background in multiple retrospectives on Marchionne's tenure; treat the exact $2B figure as approximate pending a primary-source citation — flagged for verification.)
- **Source:** General background corroborated across multiple retrospectives (Fortune timeline, NPR obituary) — recommend the writer independently verify the exact GM payout figure before using it as a hard number in-game.

**3. 2006–2008 — "Profitable Again, But Europe Is Shrinking"**
By roughly 2006 Fiat had returned to profitability and Marchionne began an aggressive product renewal (new Fiat 500, Alfa Romeo revival plans) while European mass-market volumes stagnated. The strategic question: keep investing in product or hoard cash given storm clouds (which became the 2008 financial crisis).
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** slow capital spending and bank profits; continue the renewal at full speed; seek a partner/merger early (Marchionne reportedly had consolidation instincts even this early).
- **What happened:** Fiat kept investing in product (new 500 launched 2007) even as the 2008 crisis hit global auto sales hard, leaving Fiat — like nearly every automaker — searching for partners by late 2008/early 2009.
- **Source:** General industry retrospectives; specific figures not independently verified in this research pass — flagged.

**4. JAN 2009 — "Chrysler Is Dying, and Cheap"**
Chrysler, owned by private equity firm Cerberus Capital Management since 2007, is collapsing — burning cash, with no money for new products, heading toward a politically explosive bankruptcy as the Obama administration's auto task force (led by Steven Rattner) decides whether to rescue General Motors and Chrysler with federal funds. Marchionne, sensing opportunity, begins talks for Fiat to take a stake in Chrysler — with no cash, in exchange for small-car platforms, fuel-efficient engine technology, and global distribution access Chrysler lacks.
- **CEO at the time:** Sergio Marchionne (Fiat); Chrysler was run by Bob Nardelli as Cerberus's appointed CEO.
- **Alternative paths:** Fiat could have stayed out entirely and let Chrysler die or get absorbed by a domestic buyer; Fiat could have demanded a larger upfront equity stake with some capital contribution; Fiat could have pursued only a narrower technology-licensing deal without ownership.
- **What happened:** After tense negotiations described in Treasury auto-task-force chief Steven Rattner's memoir (Marchionne reportedly swore at underlings and once walked out to an Apple Store to cool off), the U.S., Canadian, and Ontario governments structured a deal giving Fiat a 20% stake in a reorganized Chrysler and management control — without Fiat putting up any cash — in exchange for technology and a path to additional stakes (up to 35%) tied to performance milestones.
- **Source:** [NBC News/Globe and Mail retrospective](https://www.theglobeandmail.com/business/article-how-sergio-marchionne-drove-fca-to-success/); described via Steven Rattner's account of the negotiations.

**5. APR–JUN 2009 — "42 Days in Bankruptcy"**
Chrysler files for Chapter 11 on April 30, 2009. The Fiat alliance is contingent on Chrysler successfully and quickly emerging from bankruptcy — a process many feared could drag on for years and destroy what was left of the brand and dealer network. Marchionne stakes Fiat's involvement on speed.
- **CEO at the time:** Bob Nardelli (outgoing Chrysler CEO); Sergio Marchionne (incoming).
- **Alternative paths:** a slower, traditional Chapter 11 reorganization preserving more creditor claims; full liquidation (advocated by some bondholders and conservative commentators who opposed the bailout); a sale to a different partner entirely.
- **What happened:** Chrysler's bankruptcy closed in just 42 days — among the fastest major industrial bankruptcies on record — emerging on June 10, 2009 as "new Chrysler," majority owned by a UAW retiree health trust (VEBA, ~55%), with the U.S. and Canadian governments holding minority stakes and Fiat holding 20% (rising with milestones) plus management control. Marchionne became CEO of Chrysler the same day, while remaining CEO of Fiat — running both companies simultaneously.
- **Source:** [PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/economy/business-jan-june09-chryslerfiat_06-10); multiple retrospectives confirming the "42 days" figure and June 10, 2009 closing date.

### ERA B — Building Fiat Chrysler (2009–2014)

**6. 2009–2010 — "Running Two Car Companies at Once"**
Marchionne is now simultaneously CEO of Fiat S.p.A. in Turin and Chrysler Group LLC in Auburn Hills, Michigan — two companies on different continents, different unions, different product cycles, legally separate but meant to share platforms and engineering. He famously commutes by private jet and is reported to sleep only a few hours a night.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne (both companies).
- **Alternative paths:** appoint a separate, dedicated Chrysler CEO and remain Fiat-only chairman; merge legal entities immediately rather than running parallel companies; slow product-sharing to avoid culture clashes.
- **What happened:** Marchionne ran both companies in parallel for nearly five years, gradually increasing Fiat's ownership stake in Chrysler as performance milestones were hit, while pushing shared platforms (e.g., the Dodge Dart on a Fiat Compact platform; the Jeep-Fiat 500X shared underpinnings) and a "World Class Manufacturing" quality push at Chrysler plants.
- **Source:** General retrospectives; [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/money/Sergio-Marchionne).

**7. JAN 2011 — "First Stake Increase: 20% to 25%"**
The first government performance milestone — building a fuel-efficient four-cylinder engine at Chrysler's Dundee, Michigan plant — is met.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** N/A — this was a contractual milestone, not a discretionary choice, but Fiat could have chosen to delay or under-invest in meeting it.
- **What happened:** Fiat's stake rose from 20% to 25% in January 2011 after meeting the engine-production milestone.
- **Source:** [AOL/Associated Press](https://www.aol.com/news/2011-01-10-fiat-boosts-stake-in-chrysler-to-25-after-hitting-business-mile.html)

**8. APR 2011 — "Buying Out the U.S. Treasury"**
The U.S. government wants out of its Chrysler stake as fast as politically possible, both to recoup taxpayer money and end the "government motors" stigma. Fiat has the opportunity — and a friendly counterparty eager to sell — to accelerate its path to majority ownership by directly buying out Treasury's remaining stake plus a loan repayment, well ahead of the original milestone schedule.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** wait for Treasury to exit via a public sale/IPO of its stake instead of a direct buyout; negotiate a lower price by waiting out political pressure on Treasury to sell; pursue a slower, milestone-only path.
- **What happened:** In spring 2011, Fiat agreed to pay roughly $1.3 billion-plus to retire Chrysler's remaining government loans and buy out the U.S. Treasury's stake, pushing Fiat's ownership toward the 46% and then 51% (majority) range within the year — years ahead of the original 2009 deal's slow-milestone timeline.
- **Source:** [Victoria Advocate/AP](https://victoriaadvocate.com/2011/04/21/fiat-to-spend-1-3b-to-boost-its-stake-in-chrysler/); [Automotive News](https://www.autonews.com/article/20110410/GLOBAL/110419999/fiat-said-to-raise-chrysler-stake-to-30-as-soon-as-this-week)

**9. 2012 — "The Dart and the Last Milestone"**
The final U.S. government performance milestone requires Chrysler to build the new Dodge Dart (on a Fiat-derived small-car platform) at an Illinois plant and prove it can hit 40 mpg combined fuel economy — a major bet that Fiat's small-car engineering can translate into an American compact sedan.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** delay the Dart launch to perfect it (risking missing the milestone window and the associated stake increase); rush it to market to hit the EPA target and stake bump even if the car isn't fully ready; abandon the Fiat-platform approach and build a more conventional American compact instead.
- **What happened:** Chrysler and Fiat formally committed the Dart to the Belvidere, Illinois plant and demonstrated the required fuel economy to the EPA, triggering the final 5% ownership increase. The Dart itself went on to underperform commercially and was discontinued by 2016 — a cautionary footnote to an otherwise successful milestone.
- **Source:** [Automotive News retrospective via search synthesis](https://www.autonews.com/article/20110410/GLOBAL/110419999/fiat-said-to-raise-chrysler-stake-to-30-as-soon-as-this-week) (Dart underperformance is widely reported industry knowledge; recommend independent confirmation of exact discontinuation date if used as a hard fact).

**10. 2011–2012 — "Buying Out the VEBA / UAW Retiree Trust"**
The remaining major outside shareholder is the VEBA — the UAW retiree healthcare trust that ended up holding the largest single stake (roughly 41-46% at various points) in new Chrysler after the bankruptcy. The VEBA wants liquidity for its retiree healthcare obligations; Fiat wants full ownership to finally merge the companies into one.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** let the VEBA sell its stake via a public Chrysler IPO instead of a private buyout (this was litigated — the VEBA's trustees pushed for an IPO route to maximize value); negotiate a lower private price and resist IPO pressure; do a partial buyout and leave VEBA as a minority holder indefinitely.
- **What happened:** This set up the next several years of tense, sometimes litigated negotiations between Fiat and the VEBA, including a contractual right for the VEBA to force a Chrysler IPO if Fiat didn't buy its stake — which became a major lever in the 2013-2014 endgame (see #12 below).
- **Source:** Widely reported in 2013-2014 financial press; corroborated in the CNBC 2014 Fiat-Chrysler full-buyout coverage.

**11. MAY 2013 — "Marchionne Wants It All"**
With Fiat's European business badly hurt by the eurozone crisis and Chrysler now Fiat's profit engine, Marchionne pushes publicly and aggressively to acquire full ownership of Chrysler from the VEBA — well before the VEBA is ready to sell on his terms.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** continue running two separately-owned companies indefinitely; let the VEBA force an IPO and buy shares on the open market over time; offer the VEBA a much higher price to close the deal immediately.
- **What happened:** Marchionne publicly stated his intent to acquire full Chrysler ownership in 2013, but the VEBA resisted his offered price, eventually forcing the matter toward Chrysler filing for a public stock offering (IPO) in late 2013 as leverage — a process that effectively forced Fiat to negotiate a final buyout price rather than let an IPO dilute control.
- **Source:** [Benzinga](https://www.benzinga.com/news/13/05/3624304/marchionne-looks-to-acquire-full-chrysler-ownership)

**12. JAN 2014 — "Full Ownership, FCA Is Born"**
After the VEBA's threatened IPO route forced the issue, Fiat and the VEBA reach final terms for Fiat to buy the trust's entire remaining stake.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** let the Chrysler IPO proceed and buy shares gradually afterward; hold out for an even lower price and risk the IPO actually pricing and diluting Fiat's control; structure a stock-swap instead of cash purchase.
- **What happened:** On January 1, 2014, Fiat announced a deal to acquire the VEBA's remaining roughly 41.5% stake in Chrysler for about $3.65–4.35 billion combined (cash plus a special Chrysler dividend to the trust), giving Fiat 100% ownership. The combined Fiat Chrysler Automobiles (FCA) entity was formally created later in 2014, with FCA shares beginning to trade and Marchionne now leading one unified global company for the first time since 2009.
- **Quote/context:** Italian unions worried publicly about the deal shifting Fiat's center of gravity toward the U.S.
- **Source:** [Fox News/AP](https://www.foxnews.com/world/fiats-3-65-billion-deal-to-acquire-chrysler-rises-stock-italian-unions-worry); [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2014/01/01/fiat-agrees-to-buy-remaining-stake-in-chrysler.html)

### ERA C — Scale or Die: The Merger Crusade (2014–2019)

**13. OCT 2014 — "Spin Off the Crown Jewel"**
FCA, now unified, is still capital-starved relative to its $60 billion product-investment ambitions. Marchionne proposes spinning off Ferrari — FCA's most profitable and prestigious brand — into a separately listed company, partly to raise cash and partly to let Ferrari trade at luxury-goods multiples (like Hermès) rather than auto-industry multiples.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne (FCA and Ferrari chairman).
- **Alternative paths:** keep Ferrari fully inside FCA as a profit anchor; sell Ferrari outright to a third party for a lump sum; spin off a different, less iconic asset (e.g., Maserati) instead.
- **What happened:** FCA announced the Ferrari separation plan in October 2014; Ferrari's IPO (10% of shares) priced on the NYSE in October 2015, with the rest distributed to FCA shareholders, raising capital for FCA's broader investment plan. Ferrari has since traded at a major valuation premium to FCA/Stellantis, vindicating the "luxury, not auto" multiple thesis.
- **Source:** [TheStreet](https://www.thestreet.com/opinion/fiat-chrysler-seeks-to-finance-its-own-future-from-ferrari-spinoff-13332808); [Business Standard/PTI](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/pti-stories/ferrari-readies-to-launch-ipo-on-wall-street-115072400002_1.html)

**14. APR–MAY 2015 — "Confessions of a Capital Junkie"**
Marchionne publishes/presents a 26-page deck titled "Confessions of a Capital Junkie," arguing the entire auto industry wastes tens of billions of dollars duplicating R&D and capital spending on largely redundant technology, and that consolidation (mergers) is the only cure. He directs the argument squarely at General Motors as his preferred partner.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne (FCA); Mary Barra (GM, since January 2014).
- **Alternative paths:** keep FCA independent and focus purely on internal cost discipline; pursue a smaller bolt-on acquisition instead of a mega-merger; approach a different target (e.g., a Japanese or Korean automaker) instead of GM.
- **What happened:** Marchionne released the presentation on April 29, 2015, calling it an "insider perspective on the cure for the industry's value-destroying addiction to capital." Investors reportedly found the argument "spot on," and FCA shares briefly rallied, but Marchionne directly proposed a merger to GM CEO Mary Barra by letter/email; GM's board reviewed and rejected the approach. Barra publicly stated in June 2015: "For the past few years, we've been merging with ourselves. We have scale and we're leveraging that. We're focused on that and not the other."
- **Source:** [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-05-26/fiat-ceo-s-auto-merger-confession-called-spot-on-by-investors); [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/cars/fiat-chryslers-sergio-marchionne-wanted-to-merge-with-gm-but-got-shot-down-by-mary-barra/2015/05/26/53d20208-03c7-11e5-93f4-f24d4af7f97d_story.html); [Bloomberg on Barra's board review](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-06-09/barra-says-gm-board-vetted-passed-on-marchionne-s-merger-call)

**15. 2015–2017 — "The Crusade Continues, Alone"**
Rebuffed by GM, Marchionne does not give up on consolidation logic; he continues making the public case for industry mergers for years, reportedly approaching other potential partners informally while FCA invests heavily in Jeep and Ram (its most profitable brands) and scales back small-car and sedan programs in North America.
- **CEO:** Sergio Marchionne.
- **Alternative paths:** abandon the merger crusade and focus solely on FCA's own brand portfolio; pursue technology-sharing alliances short of a full merger (e.g., with Google/Waymo, which FCA did pursue for self-driving minivans); double down on cutting passenger cars entirely in North America.
- **What happened:** FCA announced in 2016 it would stop building the Dodge Dart and Chrysler 200 sedans in the U.S. to focus capacity on Jeep/Ram trucks and SUVs — a hugely profitable bet during the SUV/truck boom — while Marchionne kept publicly floating merger ideas through at least 2017's Detroit auto show commentary ("Howes: Marchionne's merger mania lives, despite GM 'no'").
- **Source:** [Detroit News](https://www.detroitnews.com/story/business/columnists/daniel-howes/2015/07/27/howes-marchionnes-merger-mania-lives-despite-gm/30766303/)

**16. JUL 2018 — "The Sudden Succession"**
Marchionne, long known for grueling hours and chain-smoking, undergoes shoulder surgery in early summer 2018 and develops serious complications. FCA's board must improvise a succession plan in real time as his condition worsens.
- **CEO transition:** Sergio Marchionne (outgoing, incapacitated then deceased) to Mike Manley (incoming).
- **Alternative paths:** name an interim caretaker CEO while Marchionne recovers; split his roles (FCA, Ferrari, CNH Industrial) among several different executives; promote a different internal candidate (e.g., a regional or brand head other than Manley).
- **What happened:** As Marchionne's health failed, FCA's board, chaired by John Elkann, transferred his FCA duties to Mike Manley — the British executive who had built Jeep into a global success and led FCA's Asia-Pacific operations — even before Marchionne's death. Marchionne died on July 25, 2018, in Zürich, Switzerland, at age 66. Elkann announced: "Unfortunately, what we feared has come to pass. Sergio Marchionne, man and friend, is gone." Manley was confirmed as FCA CEO.
- **Source:** [Time](https://time.com/5348170/sergio-marchionne-fiat-chrysler/); [Carscoops](https://www.carscoops.com/2018/07/sergio-marchionne-former-fca-ceo-dies-age-66/); [CNN](https://money.cnn.com/2018/07/25/news/companies/sergio-marchionne-obit/index.html)

**17. MAY–JUN 2019 — "The Renault Deal That Almost Was"**
Manley revives Marchionne's consolidation logic less than a year into his tenure: on May 27, 2019, FCA proposes an all-stock 50/50 merger with Renault, which would create the world's third-largest automaker by volume (8.7 million combined annual sales) and promised roughly €5 billion/year in synergies.
- **CEO:** Mike Manley (FCA); Renault was led at the board level amid the post-Carlos Ghosn arrest turmoil, with the French state holding a 15% Renault stake.
- **Alternative paths:** structure the deal to give France/Renault's state stake special protections up front; pursue a narrower alliance (platform-sharing only) instead of a full merger; wait for Renault's internal governance crisis (post-Ghosn) to settle before proposing anything.
- **What happened:** The Renault board reportedly was on the verge of approving the merger — the two sides were "less than three hours" from a joint announcement — when the French government, citing job-security concerns and a desire to also fold in alliance partner Nissan, asked for a delay. FCA abruptly withdrew its offer on June 5–6, 2019, stating: "It has become clear that the political conditions in France do not currently exist for such a combination to proceed successfully."
- **Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/06/06/fiat-chrysler-withdraws-renault-merger-offer-french-meddling-blamed.html); [The Week UK](https://www.theweek.co.uk/101602/fca-renault-merger-deal-collapses-following-french-government-intervention)

**18. OCT–DEC 2019 — "Pivot to Peugeot"**
Within months of the Renault collapse, FCA opens talks with PSA Group (Peugeot, Citroën, Opel/Vauxhall), led by CEO Carlos Tavares — a deal that, unlike the Renault talks, does not involve a major state shareholder with veto leverage in the same way.
- **CEO:** Mike Manley (FCA); Carlos Tavares (PSA).
- **Alternative paths:** continue seeking other partners (e.g., a renewed approach to Renault once the French political situation calmed, or to a Japanese/Korean automaker); abandon the merger strategy and go it alone, focusing on North American truck/SUV profits.
- **What happened:** FCA and PSA confirmed merger talks publicly on October 30, 2019, for a deal valued near $50 billion, and on December 18, 2019, the two sides announced a binding "Combination Agreement" to merge as equals.
- **Source:** [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2019/10/30/fiat-and-peugeot-confirm-talks-over-potential-50bn-tie-up); [Stellantis press release](https://www.stellantis.com/en/news/press-releases/2019/december/groupe-psa-and-fca-agree-to-merge)

### ERA D — Stellantis: Margins, Mutiny, and a New CEO (2020–2026)

**19. JAN 2021 — "Stellantis Is Born"**
After shareholder approvals and a pandemic-disrupted process, the FCA-PSA merger formally closes. The combined company needs a name (chosen as "Stellantis," from the Latin "stellare," to brighten with stars) and a governance structure splitting power between the two legacy camps.
- **CEO/Chair:** Carlos Tavares becomes CEO; John Elkann (Exor/Agnelli family heir, FCA chairman) becomes Chairman.
- **Alternative paths:** retain separate FCA and PSA brand-management structures longer before full integration; appoint Manley (FCA's outgoing CEO) as overall CEO instead of Tavares; choose a more conventional combined name (e.g., "FCA-PSA" or retain "Fiat Chrysler" branding).
- **What happened:** Shareholders approved the merger; it completed January 16, 2021; shares began trading as Stellantis on Euronext Paris/Milan on January 18 and on the NYSE on January 19, 2021, under ticker STLA. The board, chaired by Elkann, named Tavares CEO — a notable outcome since PSA (the smaller-revenue partner in some measures) supplied the chief executive, reflecting Tavares's reputation for having engineered PSA's own dramatic margin turnaround in the 2010s.
- **Source:** [Stellantis press release — merger completed](https://www.stellantis.com/en/news/press-releases/2021/january/the-merger-of-fca-and-groupe-psa-has-been-completed); [Automotive News](https://www.autonews.com/automakers/fca-and-psa-complete-merger-become-stellantis/)

**20. MAR 2022 — "Dare Forward 2030"**
With integration underway, Tavares must set the long-term strategic plan for the newly merged 14-brand giant, balancing the EU's accelerating EV mandates against North America's still-truck-hungry market and Stellantis's own ambitious margin targets.
- **CEO:** Carlos Tavares.
- **Alternative paths:** prioritize a faster, more capital-intensive all-EV pivot regardless of near-term margin hit; keep brand strategies fully decentralized rather than setting one unified global target; focus capital on fewer "global" brands and wind down weaker ones (e.g., Chrysler, DS, Alfa Romeo) immediately rather than gradually.
- **What happened:** Stellantis unveiled "Dare Forward 2030" on March 1, 2022: targets included carbon net-zero by 2038 (50% reduction by 2030), 100% BEV sales mix in Europe and 50% in the U.S. by 2030, 5 million global BEV sales, doubling net revenue to €300 billion, and sustaining double-digit adjusted operating income margin throughout the decade. The plan became the central promise against which Tavares's later performance — and his 2024 fall — would be judged.
- **Source:** [Stellantis press release](https://www.stellantis.com/en/news/press-releases/2022/march/dare-forward-2030-stellantis-blueprint-for-cutting-edge-freedom-of-mobility)

**21. 2022–2023 — "Margin Over Volume"**
Tavares pushes a deliberate strategy of raising vehicle prices (especially in North America on Jeep and Ram) and cutting low-margin trims/fleet sales to drive Stellantis's adjusted operating margin to roughly 13% — among the best in the global industry, nearly double Volkswagen's or Renault's — even as it costs market share.
- **CEO:** Carlos Tavares.
- **Alternative paths:** match competitors' pricing/incentive moves to preserve volume and market share even at lower margin; selectively protect volume in core segments (e.g., Ram pickups) while raising prices only on niche trims; slow the price increases in response to early 2023 dealer pushback rather than continuing through 2024.
- **What happened:** Stellantis posted record profitability in 2023 (operating margin near 12-13%), but by seeking higher margins, Tavares lost core customers — fleet buyers, price-sensitive retail buyers — to rivals, setting up the U.S. market-share collapse and dealer revolt that followed in 2024.
- **Source:** [Reuters via Investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/seeking-higher-margins-stellantis-ceo-tavares-lost-some-core-customers-3748208)

**22. SEP–OCT 2023 — "The UAW Strike"**
As the UAW's national contract with the Big Three expires, Stellantis faces a coordinated "stand-up strike" strategy targeting plants across Ford, GM, and Stellantis simultaneously, demanding large wage increases, the restoration of cost-of-living adjustments, and job-security guarantees after years of plant idling and outsourcing concerns.
- **CEO:** Carlos Tavares; UAW President Shawn Fain led the union.
- **Alternative paths:** settle early with a smaller package to avoid prolonged plant shutdowns; take a harder line and risk a much longer strike; offer job guarantees without matching Ford's/GM's wage terms.
- **What happened:** After a strike lasting roughly six weeks (affecting Stellantis along with Ford and GM under the UAW's targeted strike strategy), Stellantis and the UAW reached a tentative agreement on October 28, 2023: roughly 25% in general wage increases over 4.5 years (compounding to about 33% with COLA), saving 5,000 jobs slated for elimination and committing to add 5,000 new jobs by April 2028.
- **Source:** [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2023/10/28/stellantis-uaw-strike-tentative-deal); [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2023/10/28/uaw-stellantis-tentative-contract-agreement/34a6a726-75b5-11ee-936d-7a16ee667359_story.html)

**23. SEP 2024 — "The Dealers Revolt"**
By mid-2024, Stellantis's North American sales have fallen sharply (roughly 20% year-over-year in Q3), unsold Jeep/Ram/Chrysler/Dodge inventory is piling up on dealer lots due to overpricing relative to rivals, and dealers blame Tavares-era decisions directly.
- **CEO:** Carlos Tavares; Kevin Farrish, president of the Stellantis National Dealer Council, represents the dealer body.
- **Alternative paths:** respond immediately with steep incentives and price cuts to clear inventory (sacrificing the margin story); replace North American leadership before the situation became public; engage dealers privately rather than let the conflict become a public letter-writing campaign.
- **What happened:** On approximately September 10, 2024, Farrish sent an open letter to Tavares stating: "The reckless short-term decision-making to secure record profits in 2023 has had devastating yet entirely predictable, consequences in the US market. Those consequences include the rapid degradation of our iconic American brands—brands like Jeep, Dodge, Ram, and Chrysler that have over a century of history in America." Dealers also blamed North American chief Carlos Zarlenga, alleging decisions were being made by European executives disconnected from the U.S. market. The UAW separately also criticized Tavares's cost-cutting and plant decisions around the same period.
- **Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/09/12/dealers-uaw-condemn-stellantis-ceo-cuts-sales-declines.html); [Detroit News](https://eu.detroitnews.com/story/business/autos/chrysler/2024/09/12/stellantis-dealers-uaw-find-common-foe-in-ceo-carlos-tavares/75157867007/)

**24. SEP–OCT 2024 — "The Emergency Inventory Cut"**
Facing the dealer revolt and collapsing North American shipments, Stellantis must decide how aggressively to slash production and inventory versus protect near-term revenue and margin guidance.
- **CEO:** Carlos Tavares.
- **Alternative paths:** maintain production and rely on heavier incentive spending to clear stock; cut production gradually over several quarters instead of an abrupt step-change; combine inventory cuts with an immediate management shake-up in North America.
- **What happened:** Stellantis revised its full-year 2024 guidance downward, targeting no more than 330,000 units of U.S. dealer inventory by year-end (cutting roughly 100,000+ units), with North American shipments down by more than 200,000 vehicles in the second half of 2024 alone, plus increased incentives on 2024-and-older model-year vehicles. U.S. dealer inventory was cut by roughly 50,000 units in Q3 2024 alone.
- **Source:** Stellantis Q3 2024 earnings press release (SEC Form 6-K, [sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001605484/000160548424000147/stellantisnvq32024pressrel.htm))

**25. DEC 1, 2024 — "Tavares Resigns"**
With North American sales and profits collapsing, a €/$-denominated profit warning already issued, and persistent disagreement with the board (particularly Chairman John Elkann) reportedly over strategy, accountability for North America, and succession planning, the situation reaches a breaking point.
- **CEO/Chair:** Carlos Tavares (outgoing); John Elkann (Chairman, overseeing the transition).
- **Alternative paths:** the board could have stripped some of Tavares's authority while keeping him as CEO through a transition; Tavares could have proposed his own succession timeline and stayed through a defined handover; the board could have ousted him for cause rather than accepting a resignation.
- **What happened:** Stellantis announced on December 1, 2024 (effective immediately) that its board, chaired by John Elkann, had accepted Carlos Tavares's resignation as CEO, citing "diverging views" between Tavares and the board. The board immediately formed an Interim Executive Committee chaired by Elkann; Antonio Filosa (already Jeep CEO) was given expanded responsibility as North America COO. A Special Committee of the board was tasked with finding a permanent CEO within the first half of 2025.
- **Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/12/01/stellantis-ceo-carlos-tavares-resigns.html); [Spokesman-Review/AP](https://www.spokesman.com/stories/2024/dec/02/stellantis-ceo-carlos-tavares-resigns-amid-differe/)

**26. MAY–JUN 2025 — "Filosa Gets the Job"**
After a roughly six-month global search by the board's Special Committee — reportedly considering both internal and external candidates — Stellantis must choose its next CEO at a moment of historic crisis: collapsing U.S. market share, EV strategy in doubt, incoming U.S. tariffs under the second Trump administration, and a demoralized dealer network.
- **CEO selection:** the board, chaired by John Elkann, with a Special Committee running the search.
- **Alternative paths:** hire an outside turnaround CEO from another automaker or industry; promote a different internal candidate from the PSA/European side of the business to balance Filosa's North America/Jeep background; install an extended interim leadership team rather than naming a permanent CEO quickly.
- **What happened:** On May 28, 2025, Stellantis announced Antonio Filosa — a more than 25-year company veteran who had been South America COO at Stellantis's 2021 founding, became Jeep brand CEO in 2023, North America COO in October 2024, COO of both North and South America in December 2024, and Chief Quality Officer in January 2025 — as the new CEO. He formally took up full authority as CEO on June 23, 2025, while Elkann remained Executive Chairman. On his first day, Filosa announced a new leadership team.
- **Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/28/auto-stellantis-appoints-north-america-boss-antonio-filosa-as-new-ceo.html); [Stellantis press release](https://www.stellantis.com/en/news/press-releases/2025/june/antonio-filosa-announces-new-stellantis-leadership-team); [SEMA](https://www.sema.org/news-media/enews/2025/23/stellantis-names-antonio-filosa-ceo-appoints-new-chief-quality-officer)

**27. JUL 2025 — "Tariffs and Reinstating Guidance"**
Filosa inherits a company that suspended its full-year financial guidance back in April 2025 due to uncertainty from new U.S. import tariffs under the Trump administration. He must decide whether and how to reinstate forward guidance amid an unresolved tariff regime.
- **CEO:** Antonio Filosa.
- **Alternative paths:** keep guidance suspended longer until tariff policy stabilizes; reinstate guidance but with wide ranges/heavy caveats; aggressively lobby the administration before making any guidance commitment.
- **What happened:** On July 29, 2025, Stellantis reinstated 2025 guidance, while disclosing that import tariffs would cost the company up to roughly $1.7 billion for the year (with about $350 million already booked), and that the second half of 2025 would show higher revenue than H1 but only a low-single-digit operating margin. Filosa said the company had been working with the Trump administration to "properly recognize the high American U.S. content in some vehicles" and aimed for "a gradual, sequential acceleration" in performance.
- **Source:** [CNBC](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/autos/stellantis-reinstates-guidance-flags-tough-decisions-17-billion-tariff-rcna221707); [Spokesman-Review](https://www.spokesman.com/stories/2025/jul/29/stellantis-reinstates-2025-guidance-as-new-ceo-pre/)

**28. JUL–DEC 2025 — "Killing the EV-First Strategy: V8s Come Back"**
Filosa confronts the core strategic question of his tenure: Dare Forward 2030's aggressive BEV targets (100% BEV mix in Europe, 50% in U.S. by 2030) are colliding with weak EV demand, especially in North America, while customers are abandoning Jeep/Ram/Dodge for rivals still offering big V8 trucks and muscle cars.
- **CEO:** Antonio Filosa.
- **Alternative paths:** hold the line on the BEV-first roadmap and wait for demand to catch up; pursue a hybrid-only middle path without reviving V8s; cut unprofitable brands/nameplates entirely rather than reinvesting in combustion engines.
- **What happened:** Filosa's turnaround plan, detailed through mid-to-late 2025, explicitly reintroduced the Hemi V8 (in mildly hybridized form) into Ram 1500 and other models, alongside new Jeep and Ram products and a revived Dodge Charger muscle car — reversing Tavares-era EV-first commitments. The reversal came with a massive financial reckoning: Stellantis booked approximately €22.2 billion in write-downs/charges in the second half of 2025 (about €25.4 billion for the full year) tied largely to scaling back BEV ambitions, contributing to a full-year 2025 net loss of roughly €22.3 billion (some reports cite a $26.3B-equivalent loss figure). Reported V8 Ram order volume reached approximately 50,000 units, with plans to triple production toward 100,000 units by 2026.
- **Source:** [MarketScreener](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/automaker-stellantis-books-22-2-billion-euro-writedowns-in-h2-2025-in-ev-pullback-ce7e5ad9d989f027); [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2025/07/30/stellantis-turnaround-plan-more-muscle-cars-future-of-mobility); [Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/stellantis-ceo-says-turnaround-plan-includes-new-jeeps-ram-trucks-and-return-of-the-hemi-v8-133116215.html); [ItalPassion](https://www.italpassion.fr/en/stellantis/stellantis-announces-a-22-billion-euro-cost-reduction-and-no-dividends-for-shareholders/)

**29. 2025 — "The Quality Reckoning: 2,000 New Engineers"**
Years of Tavares-era cost-cutting left persistent quality problems on Jeep, Ram, and Chrysler vehicles — including a major Jeep Grand Cherokee recall (400,000+ vehicles) over an airbag software fault — that Filosa must address as both an engineering and brand-trust problem.
- **CEO:** Antonio Filosa.
- **Alternative paths:** address quality issues model-by-model reactively as recalls arise; outsource quality auditing to third parties rather than hiring internally; focus quality investment only on the most profitable nameplates (Jeep, Ram) and let smaller brands lag.
- **What happened:** Stellantis announced the hiring of approximately 2,000 additional engineers dedicated to improving quality across Jeep, Dodge, and Ram, explicitly framed as part of Filosa's broader "reset" of the company following the Tavares era. The Jeep Grand Cherokee recall (covering 2022-2026 model years, 400,000+ vehicles, later compounded by a separate 91,787-vehicle PHEV hybrid-control-processor recall) became one of the most visible symbols of the quality problem Filosa inherited.
- **Source:** [AOL/quality hiring](https://www.aol.com/lifestyle/jeep-dodge-ram-deploy-2-161500401.html); [Dealership Guy newsletter on Grand Cherokee recall](https://news.dealershipguy.com/p/jeep-recalls-400k-grand-cherokees-as-stellantis-pushes-quality-overhaul); [Yahoo Finance recall coverage](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/stellantis-issues-recall-91-787-165752526.html)
