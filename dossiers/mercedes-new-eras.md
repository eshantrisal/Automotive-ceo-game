## 2. New Eras

### Era 0 — "The Chrysler Marriage and the Reckoning" (1998–2009)
*Framing:* In May 1998, Daimler-Benz chairman Jürgen Schrempp and Chrysler CEO Robert Eaton announce the "merger of equals" — at $36 billion, the largest cross-border industrial merger in history, creating DaimlerChrysler. The "equals" framing collapses almost immediately: Schrempp privately runs it as an acquisition, German managers take over Chrysler's leadership within two years, and by the early 2000s Chrysler is hemorrhaging billions while a parallel, equally disastrous turnaround bet on Mitsubishi Motors absorbs more capital and management attention. Dieter Zetsche, sent to Detroit in 2000 to fix Chrysler, becomes the public face of the rescue and rides it to the top job in 2006 — only to inherit a global financial crisis that forces Daimler to finally cut Chrysler loose (at a steep loss, to Cerberus Capital, in 2007) just before the 2008–09 crash hits Daimler's own balance sheet, forcing a German government aid debate, a foreign sovereign-wealth-fund rescue investment, and the company's first dividend cancellation in 14 years. The era's tension is hubris vs. discipline: scale-chasing global empire-building gives way, by 2009, to a leaner, chastened Daimler ready for the "Recovery & Expansion" era that follows.

### Era E — "All In, Then Walked Back" (2021–2026)
*Framing:* Ola Källenius, who succeeded Zetsche in 2019, opens this era by making Mercedes' boldest public pledge since the Schrempp years: going "all electric by 2030, where market conditions allow," with combustion R&D spend falling to "close to zero" by 2025. The EQ sub-brand (EQS, EQE) launches into a luxury-EV market that, in the US and Europe, fails to materialize at the pace forecast — EQ sales fall sharply through 2023–2024 even as Chinese rivals (BYD, NIO, and others) erode Mercedes' China margins and a softening Chinese luxury market drags down group profit. By February 2024 Källenius publicly walks the pledge back, promising combustion and hybrid models will be sold "well into the 2030s," doubling down instead on a flexible new architecture (MMA) that builds EV and combustion versions of the same car on the same line, an in-house software platform (MB.OS) to compete with Chinese software-defined-vehicle rivals, and — by 2024–2025 — a painful "Next Level Performance" cost-cutting and job-reduction program as China weakness and EV softness combine to produce a 28% profit decline and a bleak 2025 outlook. The era's tension is the inverse of BMW's: having bet boldly on a single electric future, Mercedes spends 2024–2026 managing the costly, public process of un-betting it while still trying to look like the technology leader against both Tesla and a new wave of Chinese rivals.

## 3. Decision Points by Era

### ERA 0 — The Chrysler Marriage and the Reckoning (1998–2009)

#### 0.1 Announcing the "Merger of Equals" (7 MAY 1998)
- **CEO:** Jürgen Schrempp (Daimler-Benz) and Robert Eaton (Chrysler), as Co-Chairmen/Co-CEOs of the new DaimlerChrysler
- **Situation:** Daimler-Benz, flush with cash and looking to break into the mass-market US car business, and Chrysler, a profitable but cyclically exposed Detroit automaker looking for global scale and currency diversification, negotiate a combination structured and marketed explicitly as a merger between equals rather than an acquisition by either side.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Pursue a straightforward acquisition of Chrysler by Daimler-Benz, with Daimler management and culture clearly in charge from day one; (2) structure and market the deal as a "merger of equals" with shared governance, preserving Chrysler's brand autonomy and US management; (3) walk away and pursue organic US market entry or a smaller joint venture instead.
- **What actually happened:** On 7 May 1998, at a press conference in London, Schrempp and Eaton announced a $36 billion "merger of equals" forming DaimlerChrysler, the world's fifth-largest automaker with combined revenues of roughly $130 billion and 420,000+ employees, explicitly promising no plant closures or layoffs as a result of the deal and citing $1.4 billion in targeted first-year savings.
- **Outcome:** The "equals" framing began dissolving within two years: Schrempp installed German management at Chrysler, and by autumn 2000 he told Germany's Handelsblatt (relayed by the Financial Times) that he had always intended Chrysler to become a mere "division," not a partner — language that detonated trust with American employees, dealers, and shareholders just as Chrysler's losses were mounting.
- **Quote:** Jürgen Schrempp, in an October 2000 Financial Times interview: the "merger of equals" framing was necessary for "psychological reasons" and Chrysler had always been intended as a division of the combined company, not an equal partner. (Widely reported across multiple outlets; treat as a real, attributed quote from a primary FT interview, relayed via secondary press at the time.)
- **Source:** Automotive News retrospective context — https://www.autonews.com/ ; Detroit News retrospective, "Howes: Decades on, 'merger of equals' a cautionary tale" — https://www.detroitnews.com/story/business/columnists/daniel-howes/2018/05/09/years-merger-equals-stands-cautionary-tale/34741549/ ; dieselnet.com news archive — https://dieselnet.com/news/1998/05daimler.php ; retailrelates Substack retrospective — https://retailrelates.substack.com/p/may-7-1998-daimler-and-chrysler-shake

#### 0.2 The Mitsubishi Motors Gamble (MAR 2000)
- **CEO:** Jürgen Schrempp
- **Situation:** Seeking a foothold in Asia to complement the new transatlantic Daimler-Chrysler combination, and with Mitsubishi Motors in financial distress, DaimlerChrysler negotiates to take a large minority stake — a second major, simultaneous cross-cultural integration bet on top of the still-unsettled Chrysler integration.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Take a large controlling-minority stake (34%) and commit management resources to a turnaround; (2) take only a small, passive financial stake without operational involvement; (3) decline Asia entirely and focus all integration resources on making the Chrysler merger work first.
- **What actually happened:** DaimlerChrysler signed a letter of intent in March 2000 and purchased a 34% controlling stake in Mitsubishi Motors for roughly ¥450 billion (about €2.1 billion), taking on a turnaround mandate at the same time as the Chrysler integration was deteriorating.
- **Outcome:** Mitsubishi's problems worsened rather than improved — a defect-coverup scandal and continuing losses (including a $454 million loss at Mitsubishi Motors North America) culminated in DaimlerChrysler's board refusing to fund a 2004 rescue plan; the stake was diluted to 12.4% in a 2005 recapitalization and DaimlerChrysler sold its remaining shares in November 2005 for about $1.1 billion — a steep loss on the original ¥450 billion investment, on top of the Chrysler losses unfolding in parallel.
- **Source:** Wikipedia, "DaimlerChrysler–Mitsubishi alliance" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DaimlerChrysler%E2%80%93Mitsubishi_alliance ; Automotive News, "DaimlerChrysler pulls plug on Mitsubishi Motors" — https://www.autonews.com/article/20040423/REG/404230702/daimlerchrysler-pulls-plug-on-mitsubishi-motors/ ; NBC News, "DaimlerChrysler sells Mitsubishi stake" — https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna10002232 ; The Truth About Cars, "Mitsubishi Motors: And Then There Were…" — https://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/2014/07/mitsubishi-motors-and-then-there-were/

#### 0.3 Replacing Chrysler's American Leadership With German Managers (NOV 2000)
- **CEO:** Jürgen Schrempp (group); Dieter Zetsche dispatched to head Chrysler Group
- **Situation:** Two American CEOs of the Chrysler Group, James P. Holden and (briefly) Thomas Stallkamp, are dismissed within roughly 19 months of the merger as Chrysler swings from a $2.5 billion first-half-1998 profit to a $2 billion loss in the second half of that same year, and losses approach $2 billion again in 2001–2002. The board must decide who runs the American operation going forward.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Continue searching for an American successor to preserve at least the appearance of a "merger of equals"; (2) install a trusted Daimler insider directly into Chrysler's top operating role; (3) centralize Chrysler's decisions entirely back to Stuttgart without a dedicated on-the-ground CEO.
- **What actually happened:** Schrempp sent Dieter Zetsche, a long-time Daimler-Benz engineer-executive, to Detroit in November 2000 as President and CEO of the Chrysler Group, beginning a major restructuring of the American operation.
- **Outcome:** Zetsche's restructuring produced results: Chrysler posted an adjusted operating profit of $111 million in Q1 2002, a sharp reversal from the prior year's losses, and by 2006 Chrysler was, per contemporary reporting, "perhaps the healthiest car company in Detroit" — a turnaround that built Zetsche's internal reputation and positioned him as Schrempp's eventual successor.
- **Source:** Encyclopedia.com, "DaimlerChrysler Corporation" — https://www.encyclopedia.com/economics/economics-magazines/daimlerchrysler-corporation ; The Case Centre, "Daimler-Chrysler to DaimlerChrysler: Dieter Zetsche's Restructuring Strategies" — https://www.thecasecentre.org/products/view?id=68190 ; European CEO, "Dieter Zetsche" profile — https://www.europeanceo.com/profiles/dieter-zetsche-mercedes/

#### 0.4 Schrempp's Exit and Zetsche's Promotion to Group Chairman (JUL 2005 – JAN 2006)
- **CEO transition:** Jürgen Schrempp → Dieter Zetsche
- **Situation:** Nearly eight years after the merger, with Chrysler's turnaround complete but shareholder patience over the "merger of equals" promise and DaimlerChrysler's depressed stock price exhausted, the supervisory board must decide on a change at the top.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Keep Schrempp in place to defend and continue his global empire-building strategy; (2) install Zetsche, the executive most credibly associated with fixing (rather than causing) the merger's biggest problem; (3) bring in an outsider with no ties to the original 1998 deal.
- **What actually happened:** Following a board decision on 28 July 2005, Dieter Zetsche succeeded Jürgen Schrempp as Chairman of the Board of Management of DaimlerChrysler, effective 1 January 2006; officially a voluntary departure, contemporary reporting characterized Schrempp's exit as effectively forced by sustained shareholder pressure.
- **Outcome:** Zetsche became the public face of both the Chrysler rescue and, soon, the decision to end the Chrysler experiment altogether — a role that placed Daimler's most US-savvy executive in charge just as the case for divesting Chrysler became unanswerable.
- **Source:** Wikipedia, Dieter Zetsche — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dieter_Zetsche ; aftermarketNews, "DaimlerChrysler CEO Schrempp to Step Down" — https://www.aftermarketnews.com/daimlerchrysler-ceo-schrempp-to-step-down/ ; Mercedes-Benz Group corporate history, Schrempp — https://group.mercedes-benz.com/company/tradition/ceos/schrempp.html

#### 0.5 Selling Chrysler to Cerberus Capital Management (14 MAY 2007)
- **CEO:** Dieter Zetsche
- **Situation:** Despite the early-2000s turnaround, Chrysler relapses — posting a $1.5 billion loss in 2006 — and continues to be a drag on group profitability and, in management's view, on DaimlerChrysler's stock valuation versus pure-premium rivals like BMW. Private equity firms begin circling the unit.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Keep Chrysler and fund another internal turnaround; (2) sell a controlling stake to a private equity buyer to remove the unit from Daimler's books while retaining some upside; (3) seek a strategic industrial buyer instead of a financial one.
- **What actually happened:** DaimlerChrysler announced the sale of an 80.1% stake in the Chrysler Group to private equity firm Cerberus Capital Management for $7.4 billion, a fraction of the original $36 billion deal value nine years earlier; the transaction closed on 3 August 2007. DaimlerChrysler also contributed an additional roughly €650 million to cover Chrysler's long-term liabilities as part of the deal.
- **Outcome:** The sale was estimated to cut DaimlerChrysler's 2007 net profit by roughly €3–4 billion. The company was renamed Daimler AG shortly after, formally ending the nine-year transatlantic experiment and freeing management to refocus entirely on the Mercedes-Benz and truck businesses.
- **Quote:** Chrysler CEO Tom LaSorda, on the transaction: "After months of uncertainty, then a period of transition, we are beginning a new chapter in Chrysler's proud history." (LaSorda's quote, not Zetsche's — flagged here for accuracy; no single comparably punchy verbatim Zetsche quote on the sale itself was independently located in this research pass.)
- **Source:** CNN Money, "Daimler dumps Chrysler on Cerberus, taking loss to do so" — https://money.cnn.com/2007/05/14/news/companies/chrysler_sale/ ; CNBC, "Chrysler to Be Acquired by Private-Equity Firm for $7.4 Billion" — https://www.cnbc.com/2007/05/14/chrysler-to-be-acquired-by-privateequity-firm-for-74-billion.html ; NBC News, "Cerberus takes control of Chrysler" — https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna20108727 ; Britannica Money, "Chrysler: From Daimler to Stellantis" — https://www.britannica.com/money/Chrysler/From-Daimler-to-Stellantis

#### 0.6 The Global Financial Crisis Hits Daimler Directly (LATE 2008 – 2009)
- **CEO:** Dieter Zetsche
- **Situation:** Having shed Chrysler in 2007 specifically to focus on the core Mercedes-Benz and truck businesses, Daimler is hit just over a year later by the global financial crisis: luxury car demand collapses worldwide, and the company posts billion-euro quarterly losses in early 2009.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Deep, immediate layoffs across German plants to protect margin; (2) lean on German short-time-work schemes and a broad cost-cutting program while preserving the core workforce; (3) seek German government loan guarantees under the auto-industry aid package being discussed in Berlin.
- **What actually happened:** Zetsche announced a roughly €5.3 billion cost-cutting and efficiency program in 2009, leaned heavily on flexible/short-time work arrangements rather than mass layoffs, and personally took a 51% pay cut (his 2008 compensation fell to about €4.82 million from the prior year).
- **Outcome:** Daimler still posted a full-year 2009 net loss of about €2.6 billion (versus a €1.4 billion profit in 2008), with revenue down 20% to €79 billion and unit sales down 25% to 1.6 million vehicles. The board recommended suspending the dividend entirely for 2009 — the first dividend cancellation in 14 years.
- **Source:** S&P Global Market Intelligence, "Daimler Posts 2.6-bil.-Euro Loss for 2009; Cancels Dividend for First Time in 14 Years" — https://www.spglobal.com/marketintelligence/en/mi/country-industry-forecasting.html?id=106594552 ; Bloomberg, "Daimler Chief Zetsche's 2008 Pay Falls 51% to EU4.82 Million" — https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2009-02-27/daimler-chief-zetsche-s-2008-pay-falls-51-to-eu4-82-million ; Automotive News Europe, "Daimler to deepen cost cuts after expected quarterly loss" — https://europe.autonews.com/automakers/daimler-deepen-cost-cuts-after-expected-quarterly-loss

#### 0.7 Selling a Stake to Abu Dhabi's Aabar Investments (MAR 2009)
- **CEO:** Dieter Zetsche
- **Situation:** In the depths of the crisis, with credit markets frozen and German state aid carrying political strings and reputational baggage, Daimler's board looks for a large, fast capital injection from a sovereign-wealth-style investor instead of (or alongside) government support.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Rely primarily on German state aid/loan guarantees being offered to the auto sector; (2) sell a large equity stake to a foreign sovereign wealth fund to raise capital quickly and signal market confidence; (3) raise capital purely through a rights issue to existing shareholders.
- **What actually happened:** In March 2009, Abu Dhabi's Aabar Investments acquired a roughly 9.1% stake in Daimler (96.4 million shares) for about €1.95 billion. This came on top of the Kuwait Investment Authority's long-standing Daimler shareholding (dating to a 1974 stake acquired from the Quandt family, then sitting around 6.9–7.6% through this period) — meaning two Gulf sovereign-linked investors together held a substantial bloc of Daimler equity through the crisis.
- **Outcome:** The Aabar investment gave Daimler a fast, large capital cushion without relying solely on German state guarantees, while deepening Daimler's relationship with Gulf capital — a relationship that extended later that same year into a joint purchase, with Aabar, of a 75.1% stake in the Brawn GP Formula One team (which became the Mercedes F1 works team).
- **Source:** Automotive World, "Germany: Aabar Investments sells 3% stake in Daimler" (background/retrospective) — https://www.automotiveworld.com/articles/96471-germany-aabar-investments-sells-3-stake-in-daimler/ ; Wikipedia, Mercedes-Benz Group — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_Group ; Wikipedia, Kuwait Investment Authority — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwait_Investment_Authority ; Automotive World, "Daimler and the Kuwait Investment Authority celebrate the anniversary of their 40-year partnership" — https://www.automotiveworld.com/news-releases/daimler-kuwait-investment-authority-celebrate-anniversary-40-year-partnership/

#### 0.8 Zetsche's Crisis Cost-Cutting Plan and Cancelled Dividend (APR–DEC 2009)
- **CEO:** Dieter Zetsche
- **Situation:** With the 2009 results deteriorating throughout the year, Zetsche must decide how publicly and aggressively to signal discipline to shareholders and German political stakeholders simultaneously, at a moment when other automakers (GM, Chrysler under its new owner) are filing for bankruptcy protection in the US.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) Maintain the dividend to reassure long-term shareholders despite the loss; (2) cancel the dividend entirely and direct all available cash to the balance sheet and the cost-cutting program; (3) pay a token, sharply reduced dividend as a middle path.
- **What actually happened:** Daimler's management proposed cancelling the dividend entirely for fiscal year 2009 — the company's first omitted dividend in 14 years — alongside the broader €5.3 billion efficiency program and continued use of short-time work to avoid mass layoffs.
- **Outcome:** The combination of capital raised from Aabar, the dividend suspension, and the cost program let Daimler enter 2010 with a far stronger balance sheet than many crisis-era peers, setting up the swift turnaround that defines the start of the existing in-game "Recovery & Expansion Era" (2010–2015).
- **Source:** S&P Global Market Intelligence (as above) — https://www.spglobal.com/marketintelligence/en/mi/country-industry-forecasting.html?id=106594552 ; Auto Remarketing, "Daimler Highlights Major Financial Turnaround in 2010" — https://www.autoremarketing.com/ar/daimler-highlights-major-financial-turnaround-2010/

### ERA E — All In, Then Walked Back (2021–2026)

#### E1. Källenius Pledges "All Electric by 2030" (JUL 2021)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** With Tesla's valuation soaring and Volkswagen, GM, and others making sweeping electrification pledges, Källenius — two years into the job — must decide how aggressively Mercedes commits its premium brand to a single-powertrain future, at a July 2021 strategy update framed around the "Ambition 2039" carbon-neutrality target.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) match rivals with a hard "all-electric by [year]" pledge to win investor and ESG credibility; (2) continue a flexible, multi-powertrain approach (closer to BMW's contemporaneous stance) without a firm all-electric date; (3) commit to electrification only in specific markets/segments (e.g., compact cars) while leaving flagship combustion models untouched indefinitely.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes-Benz announced it would go all-electric by 2030 "where market conditions allow," targeting 50% electrified (BEV + hybrid) sales by the middle of the decade, and stated spending on combustion-engine technology would fall to "close to zero" by 2025. Three new dedicated EV platforms were announced for 2025 launch.
- **Outcome:** The pledge generated a strong stock-price/investor reaction at the time as a signal of technology leadership, but set a high bar that EV demand would fail to support within three years, setting up the 2024 reversal (see E5).
- **Quote:** Ola Källenius, to Reuters (2021): "We really want to go for it... and be dominantly, if not all electric, by the end of the decade," adding that spending on combustion technology would be "close to zero" by 2025.
- **Source:** NBC News, "Mercedes-Benz to go all-electric by 2030" — https://www.nbcnews.com/business/autos/mercedes-benz-go-all-electric-2030-n1274708 ; Reuters via Investing.com, "Mercedes-Benz hits accelerator in e-car race with Tesla" — https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/daimler-to-invest-more-than-40-billion-euros-by-2030-in-electric-vehicles-2565111 ; Mercedes-Benz Group, Ambition 2039 — https://group.mercedes-benz.com/sustainability/environment-climate/decarbonisation/ambition-2039-our-path-to-co2-neutrality.html

#### E2. Launching the EQS and EQE on Dedicated EV Architecture (2021–2022)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** To back the 2030 pledge, Mercedes must bring genuinely dedicated, ground-up electric flagships to market quickly, rather than EV conversions of existing combustion models — directly answering both Tesla's Model S/Model 3 and BMW's more cautious, platform-shared approach.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) launch bespoke EV-only flagship sedans (EQS, EQE) with radical aerodynamic styling distinct from combustion S-Class/E-Class equivalents; (2) build EVs that visually mirror existing combustion flagships to ease customer transition; (3) delay flagship EVs and start instead with smaller, lower-risk compact EVs.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes launched the EQS (2021) and EQE (2022) as standalone, aerodynamically distinct EV flagships on a dedicated electric platform, positioned as the electric vanguard of the brand, separate in styling from the combustion S-Class and E-Class.
- **Outcome:** Critically well-reviewed for efficiency and technology, but commercially the EQS and EQE underperformed expectations from 2023 onward as customers proved reluctant to embrace the radically different "EV-specific" styling — a problem Mercedes would later explicitly cite and reverse course on (see E5/E8).
- **Source:** InsideEVs, "The Mercedes EQS Is Struggling — And Its Radical Design Might Be Why" — https://insideevs.com/news/764560/mercedes-eqs-sales-poor-design/ ; Autoblog, "Slow-Selling Mercedes-Benz EQS Won't be Seeing a Second Generation" — https://www.autoblog.com/news/mercedes-is-quietly-preparing-to-end-the-slow-selling-eqs

#### E3. Committing to an In-House Software Operating System — MB.OS (FEB 2023)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** As vehicles become software-defined and Chinese EV makers (and Tesla) demonstrate fast over-the-air update cycles and tightly integrated software stacks, Mercedes must decide whether to build its own vehicle operating system from scratch or rely more heavily on third-party/Android-based automotive software platforms.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) build a proprietary, in-house, chip-to-cloud operating system (MB.OS) covering infotainment, ADAS, body/comfort, and driving/charging domains; (2) license a third-party automotive software stack (e.g., Android Automotive) more deeply to save development cost and time; (3) maintain the existing, more fragmented multi-supplier software architecture.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes-Benz announced MB.OS at a February 2023 strategy update — an in-house-developed operating system intended to unify all major vehicle software domains and enable continuous over-the-air updates, with rollout planned for mid-decade alongside the new MMA platform.
- **Outcome:** MB.OS began appearing in production vehicles starting with the new CLA and GLB from 2025, positioned as Mercedes' answer to the software-integration advantage Chinese EV makers and Tesla were perceived to hold — though, like the hardware EV pledge, it represented a large, multi-year R&D commitment whose payoff depended on disciplined, on-time execution.
- **Source:** Mercedes-Benz Group, "Software architects: Mercedes-Benz previews its operating system MB.OS" — https://group.mercedes-benz.com/investors/events/2023-02-mercedes-benz-group-strategy-update.html ; media.mbusa.com press release (same title) — https://media.mbusa.com/releases/software-architects-mercedes-benz-previews-its-operating-system-mbos ; Mercedes-Benz Group, MB.OS overview — https://group.mercedes-benz.com/technology/digitalisation/operating-system/mb-os.html

#### E4. EQ Sales Collapse in Key Markets (2023–2024)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** Eighteen months after the EQS/EQE launch, US and European demand data make clear that Mercedes' bespoke EV flagships are not scaling — well before the official 2024 strategy reversal, management faces the question of how to respond to weakening EV demand in real time.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) cut EQ prices aggressively to defend volume; (2) hold price/margin discipline and accept declining EV volumes while waiting for the next-generation, more conventionally-styled EVs; (3) pull back EQ production allocation and quietly redirect capacity toward combustion/hybrid models already in demand.
- **What actually happened:** US sales data showed the EQS sedan/SUV combined falling from 14,499 units in 2023 to 6,963 in 2024 (down 52%), while the EQE sedan/SUV fell from 19,104 to 11,660 units (down 39%) over the same period; quarterly drops were even steeper at points (EQE family down 47% and EQS family down 57% year-over-year in Q2 2024 alone).
- **Outcome:** Resale values cratered in step with new-car demand — used EQS units were reported trading for as little as $33,000, despite a six-figure original sticker price — reinforcing the depreciation/brand-perception problem and adding pressure on management to change design direction for the next EV generation.
- **Source:** InsideEVs, "Mercedes U.S. EV Sales Plummeted In 2024 While PHEVs Surged" — https://insideevs.com/news/746611/mercedes-ev-sales-fall-2024/ ; Carscoops, "Mercedes EV Sales Are In Freefall, EQS Down More Than 50%" — https://www.carscoops.com/2024/07/mercedes-ev-sales-are-in-freefall-eqs-down-more-than-50/ ; Carscoops, "Customers Pull The Plug On Mercedes EV Sales, EQS Plummets 52%" — https://www.carscoops.com/2025/01/customers-pull-the-plug-on-mercedes-ev-sales-eqs-plummets-52/

#### E5. Walking Back the All-Electric Pledge (22 FEB 2024)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** With EV demand growth slowing across Mercedes' core US and European markets and the 2030 all-electric target now clearly unreachable on the original timeline, Källenius must decide whether to defend the 2021 pledge publicly or formally revise it — a high-visibility reversal given how loudly the original commitment had been marketed.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) publicly maintain the 2030 all-electric target and absorb the credibility cost of likely missing it later; (2) formally and explicitly walk back the target, reinvesting in combustion/hybrid technology, accepting the "U-turn" framing in the press; (3) quietly let the target lapse without a clear public statement either way.
- **What actually happened:** Källenius announced Mercedes was abandoning the goal of exclusively selling EVs in major markets by 2030, delaying the 50% hybrid+EV interim sales target by five years, and stated that next-generation combustion-engine updates would deliver "almost like... a new lineup by 2027 that will take us well into the 2030s" — including new hybrid-focused four-cylinder engines co-developed with China's Geely for a 2026 launch to meet Euro 7 standards.
- **Outcome:** The reversal was reported globally as a "drastic" or "major" backtrack on the 2021 pledge; it pre-dated and foreshadowed similar reversals from Volkswagen, GM, and others later in 2024, but unlike BMW (which never made a hard all-electric pledge), Mercedes absorbed the specific reputational cost of publicly reversing a high-profile prior commitment.
- **Quote:** Ola Källenius (Feb 2024): updates to combustion powertrains would make it "almost like we will have a new lineup by 2027 that will take us well into the 2030s."

#### E6. China Margin Pressure and the Local Investment Response (SEP 2024)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** China, Mercedes' largest single market, turns from a profit engine into a serious headwind: domestic EV and software-defined-vehicle makers undercut Mercedes on price and in-car technology, while the broader Chinese luxury market cools. Mercedes must decide whether to retreat from China investment or double down on localization to stay competitive on cost and product fit.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) scale back China investment and protect margin by ceding share to local competitors; (2) increase local investment to build more China-specific, cost-competitive products (including localizing the new MMA-based electric CLA for Chinese buyers); (3) compete purely on price with existing imported/global-spec products.
- **What actually happened:** In September 2024, Mercedes-Benz announced plans to invest more than RMB 14 billion (about $2 billion) in China together with local partners to diversify its localized passenger-car and light-commercial-vehicle lineup, including production of new MMA-platform models — such as an all-electric long-wheelbase CLA built specifically for Chinese customers — at the Beijing Benz Automotive (BBAC) joint venture.
- **Outcome:** Despite the investment, China sales continued to decline through 2024–2025 (multiple quarters down double digits year-over-year), illustrating that the China problem was structural (competitive and macroeconomic) rather than simply a localization gap — directly feeding into the broader 2024–2025 profit warning (see E7).

#### E7. Absorbing the 2024 Profit Collapse and Announcing "Next Level Performance" (NOV 2024 – FEB 2025)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** Full-year 2024 results land badly: net profit falls sharply, China sales drop, and EV sales fall as well even as PHEV sales partially offset the decline. Management must decide how aggressively to cut costs and how to communicate a credible recovery plan to investors and a nervous workforce simultaneously.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) absorb the downturn with minimal structural change, betting on a cyclical recovery; (2) launch a large, multi-year cost-cutting and job-reduction program targeting production costs and headcount; (3) cut R&D/product investment instead of headcount to preserve jobs in the near term.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes-Benz reported 2024 net profit down 28% to about €10.4 billion (other contemporary reporting cited a 37% headline decline depending on measure/segment) on revenue down roughly 4% to €145.6 billion, with China sales down 7% for the year. In February 2025 the company unveiled "Next Level Performance," targeting roughly €5 billion in annualized savings by 2027 (about half realized in 2025) via production-cost cuts of about 10%, alongside a potential reduction of up to 20,000 jobs (rising to as many as 33,000, roughly 20% of the global workforce, if conditions worsened) — implemented through voluntary buyouts and attrition, with management guaranteeing no forced redundancies before 2034.
- **Outcome:** Germany's IG Metall union and Mercedes' works council, led by Sindelfingen plant chair Ergun Lümali, pushed back on specific measures — including reductions to a previously agreed 2025–2026 wage increase and cross-department redeployment of workers regardless of pay grade — but ultimately reached agreement on a voluntary-departure framework rather than forced layoffs.
- **Quote:** Ola Källenius, February 2025: "To ensure the company's future competitiveness in an increasingly uncertain world, we are taking steps to make the company leaner, faster and stronger, while readying an intense product launch campaign for multiple new vehicles starting with the all new CLA."

#### E8. Retiring the EQ Brand Name and Resetting EV Design Direction (2023 Announcement; 2024–2025 Rollout)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** Customer confusion over the EQ naming scheme (EQA, EQB, EQC, EQE, EQE SUV, EQS, EQS SUV — with no consistent size/body relationship) and the EQS/EQE's design-driven sales struggles (see E2, E4) force a decision on how to brand and style the next EV generation.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) retire the standalone EQ nomenclature and instead badge EVs with the same model names as their combustion equivalents (e.g., "GLA" instead of "EQA"), styled to closely resemble combustion siblings; (2) keep the EQ brand and naming scheme but refresh the styling; (3) create an entirely new, third naming/branding scheme distinct from both EQ and combustion names.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes confirmed (per Handelsblatt reporting in January 2023) it would phase out standalone "EQ" model names starting with its next-generation compact cars in late 2024, rebadging future EVs with their combustion equivalents' names (e.g., future electric compacts using "GLA" rather than "EQA" naming) and aligning EV exterior/interior design more closely with combustion siblings — retaining "EQ" only as a sub-label (e.g., "with EQ Technology") and for non-vehicle items like wallboxes.
- **Outcome:** The new MMA-based CLA, unveiled March 2025, was the first model to apply this approach at scale: combustion and battery-electric versions share identical exterior and interior design and the same model name ("CLA with EQ Technology" for the electric version), a direct reversal of the EQS/EQE's deliberately distinct EV styling.

#### E9. Launching the MMA Platform and the New CLA — Combustion and Electric, Same Car (13 MAR 2025)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** The new Mercedes-Benz Modular Architecture (MMA) — the platform underpinning Mercedes' compact-car future — must be designed to flexibly support both battery-electric and combustion variants on one line, directly reflecting the lessons of the 2024 strategy reversal (E5) and the EQ design backlash (E2, E8), and explicitly diverging from Tesla's single-powertrain Model 3 approach.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) build MMA as a flexible platform supporting identical-design combustion and EV variants under one model name, prioritizing customer familiarity and production flexibility; (2) build MMA as an EV-only platform (continuing the EQS/EQE approach) and develop combustion compacts separately; (3) delay the EV variant until combustion demand data clarified further.
- **What actually happened:** The third-generation CLA, unveiled 13 March 2025 as the first MMA-based model, launched with combustion and battery-electric versions sharing identical exterior and interior design and the same model name for the first time at Mercedes — the electric CLA 250+ with EQ Technology offering an 85 kWh battery, 800-volt architecture, up to 320 kW DC fast charging, and roughly 325 km of range added in 10 minutes' charging — with a combustion/mild-hybrid petrol version following later in the year.
- **Outcome:** The dual-powertrain, shared-design MMA approach represented Mercedes' structural answer to having over-committed to single-powertrain EV design in 2021–2022: production flexibility to shift mix between combustion and electric on the same line based on real-time demand, explicitly cited by management as a hedge against further EV demand volatility.

#### E10. Continued China and EV Headwinds Through 2025 — Guidance and the Road Ahead (2025–2026)
- **CEO:** Ola Källenius
- **Situation:** Even after the 2024 strategic reset and the MMA/CLA launch, 2025 brings continued pressure: China sales and EV sales both remain weak for extended stretches, and management must decide how to communicate the multi-year nature of the recovery to investors already digesting the prior year's profit warning and job-cut announcement.
- **Alternatives on the table:** (1) project a fast, V-shaped recovery to reassure markets, risking a second credibility hit if it doesn't materialize; (2) communicate a longer, multi-year "Mastering Transformation" framing that sets more conservative near-term expectations while emphasizing the MMA/CLA and MB.OS product cycle as the medium-term answer; (3) pursue a faster, more drastic capacity-cutting response (plant closures) rather than the gradual, attrition-based approach chosen in 2025.
- **What actually happened:** Mercedes-Benz's leadership, including CFO Harald Wilhelm, continued through 2025 Capital Market Day presentations to frame the period as "Mastering Transformation" — emphasizing that the company was structurally positioned with production flexibility to readjust between combustion and BEV output based on demand, that combustion/PHEV products would remain "higher for longer" and margin-accretive, and that the "Next Level Performance" cost program plus the new CLA-led product campaign were the foundation of the recovery case, while still confirming a materially weaker profit outlook for 2025 itself.
- **Outcome:** By giving a deliberately multi-year, product-cycle-dependent recovery narrative rather than promising a quick rebound, Källenius's management team avoided repeating the credibility cost of the 2021 all-electric pledge, but left Mercedes' stock price and investor confidence directly tied to whether the MMA/CLA generation and China localization investments deliver as promised through 2026 and beyond.

## Notes, Gaps, and Flags for the Writer

1. **Beijing Benz/BBAC "shutdown" claim — explicitly excluded as unverified.** Multiple low-quality sources (a WordPress blog and several YouTube videos with no named journalistic byline) circulated in late 2025 claiming Mercedes abruptly terminated the entire Beijing Benz Automotive (BBAC) joint venture in December 2025 over a Chinese Communist Party board-seat dispute, with "3,000+ workers sent home." This research could find **no corroboration whatsoever** from Reuters, Bloomberg, Automotive News, the Financial Times, or any mainstream wire service. More importantly, Wikipedia's own BBAC/Beijing Benz page (checked live during this research pass) lists BBAC as **currently and continuously producing** multiple models including a "Mercedes-Benz Electric CLA L" with a 2025 production start, under an active 51% (BAIC) / 38.665% (Mercedes-Benz Group) / 10.335% (Mercedes-Benz China) ownership split — directly contradicting a "shutdown." Treat the shutdown story as false/unverified and do not use it in-game. The real, well-documented China story is margin/competitive pressure plus continued (not reduced) localization investment — see E6 and E7 above.
2. **DaimlerChrysler 2007 sale quote:** The punchy "new chapter" quote on the Chrysler sale is from Chrysler CEO Tom LaSorda, not Dieter Zetsche — flagged in 0.5 to avoid misattribution if the writer wants a quotation for that scenario.
3. **Schrempp's October 2000 Financial Times "division" quote (0.1):** Widely and consistently reported across multiple retrospectives, but this research relied on secondary accounts of the original Handelsblatt/FT interview rather than the primary FT archive text itself; treat as a well-corroborated paraphrase/quote rather than a directly re-verified primary transcript.
4. **2009 Daimler full-year loss figure:** Reported consistently as approximately €2.6 billion net loss for FY2009 with revenue of about €79 billion — note one source in this research pass cited a "37% profit decline" framing that appears to actually describe the 2024 results, not 2009; the 2009 figures used in section 0.6–0.8 above (the €2.6bn loss, 20% revenue decline, 25% unit sales decline, 14-year dividend cancellation) are independently cross-confirmed across S&P Global Market Intelligence and Auto Remarketing and should be treated as the reliable figures for that year.
5. **Mercedes 2024 net profit decline — "28%" vs. "37%":** Different contemporary outlets used slightly different profit bases (group net profit vs. car-division EBIT, or year-over-year comparison points), producing both a "28%" and a "37%" headline decline figure for FY2024 results announced in February 2025. This dossier uses "28% to about €10.4 billion" as the primary figure (France24/Fortune) but flags the discrepancy for the game writer to reconcile against Mercedes' own audited annual report before finalizing an exact in-game percentage.
6. **Job-cut numbers (E7):** The "20,000 rising to as many as 33,000" figures come from contemporary press reporting on the "Next Level Performance" program; Mercedes' own public statements emphasized voluntary departures/attrition and "no forced redundancies until 2034" rather than confirming a hard headcount-reduction target — treat the upper-bound 33,000 figure as a press estimate of worst-case exposure, not a company-confirmed target.

Era E is bounded 2021–2026 to pick up immediately where the existing "Transformation Era" (2016–2021) leaves off (which already ends on the 2021 CASE-strategy/EQ-brand-founding note) and run to the present day.
